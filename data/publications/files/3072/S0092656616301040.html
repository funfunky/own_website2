Abstract

We examined age differences in the six broad HEXACO personality factors and their defining facets, using self-reports from an online sample (N ≈ 100,000, ages 14–74). Honesty-Humility showed an upward trend of about one full standard deviation unit between ages 18 and 60. Age trends for Emotionality and Extraversion differed by facet, but overall were downward for Emotionality and upward for Extraversion. Agreeableness showed relatively weak age trends. Conscientiousness and Openness showed upward age trends during the teen years (extending to the mid-20s for Conscientiousness), but age trends thereafter differed considerably by facet. Results are discussed in relation to previous findings for the Big Five, to the “age-crime curve”, and to possible evolved developmental origins.

1. Age trends in the HEXACO personality factors

Some important questions in personality research concern differences in personality as a function of a person’s age. One such question is that of “rank-order” stability in personality trait levels: To what extent do people maintain their standing on a given personality trait, relative to other persons of their own age cohort, across various segments of the lifespan? Another question is that of mean-level personality differences across age groups: How much higher or lower is the average of a given personality trait for persons at one age versus another age? Depending on how the latter question is examined, any age-related differences might reflect developmental changes experienced by most persons, influences experienced by persons of a given birth cohort, or influences experienced by persons between two points in history.

In the present research, we focus on the latter question. Specifically, we examine age differences in personality trait levels using a large cross-sectional dataset in which participants provided self-reports on the HEXACO Personality Inventory—Revised (HEXACO-PI-R; e.g., Lee & Ashton, in press). With cross-sectional data for a very large participant sample, we can identify age trends whose meaning can be explored further in future longitudinal research. With personality assessed in terms of the HEXACO framework, we have the opportunity to identify important age trends that have not been observed in previous research, which has mainly been based on the Big Five personality framework. We begin by reviewing the major findings of that previous research.

1.1. Previous findings on age differences in personality trait levels

Since the beginning of the 21st century there has been much research on age trends in mean levels of the Big Five personality factors and in the narrower, facet-level traits that define those factors. Some of the major investigations, all of which have used self-report data, include the following:

a meta-analysis of 92 adult or adolescent samples (Roberts, Walton, & Viechtbauer, 2006) in which personality assessments had been obtained at two or more time points at least one year apart, with scales classified into six categories broadly corresponding to the Big Five personality factors but with Extraversion divided into “Social Vitality” and “Social Dominance” categories;

a cross-sectional study based on the Big Five Inventory (BFI) in an online sample of over 1,000,000 persons aged 10–64 years (Soto, John, Gosling, & Potter, 2011; see also the earlier report on a subset of this sample by Srivastava, John, Gosling, & Potter, 2003);

a series of investigations based on brief Big Five measures in nationally representative samples from panel survey participants in several countries, with cross-sectional data in Germany and the United Kingdom (Donnellan & Lucas, 2008), in Australia (Lucas & Donnellan, 2009), and in Switzerland and the United States (Anusic, Lucas, & Donnellan, 2012), and also with longitudinal data in the German sample (Lucas & Donnellan, 2011) and the Australian sample (Wortman, Lucas, & Donnellan, 2012);

a cross-sectional study based on a brief Big Five measure in a sample of nearly 6000 New Zealand adults (Sibley & Pirie, 2013);

a longitudinal as well as cross-sectional examination of the NEO Personality Inventory—Revised (NEO-PI-R), in a sample of nearly 2000 US adults (Terracciano, McCrae, Brant, and Costa (2005);

another NEO-based study, using cross-sectional data from over 2700 Estonian adults (Mõttus et al., 2015);

a relatively small-sample study involving longitudinal and cross-sectional self-reports on facet-level personality traits within each of the Big Five (Soto & John, 2012);

a cross-sectional investigation of age trends in facet-level traits within the Conscientiousness factor (Jackson et al., 2009).

Limitations of space preclude a detailed description of the results of the above investigations, but here we provide an overview of the main findings.

First, most studies have shown that across large segments of adulthood, there are downward trends in Big Five Neuroticism and upward trends in Big Five Agreeableness and Conscientiousness, with these results sometimes being interpreted as indicating greater maturity among older adults.

However, for each of the Big Five factors there has been considerable variation between studies in the timing and size (and sometimes the direction) of the age trends. For example, some studies show substantial downward trends in Openness between the teens and late adulthood, but some studies show appreciable upward trends. The differences between studies in Big Five age trends are likely attributable in part to differences in the representation of facet-level traits (and even in item content within facets) across various measures of the Big Five.

There are some large differences in age trends across facets of the same Big Five factor. For example, within Extraversion, sociability generally shows downward trends, but social confidence shows upward trends; within Conscientiousness, cautiousness shows upward trends, but perfectionism shows relatively steady trends. Moreover, even within a given facet-level scale, items may differ widely in their age trends.

Finally, when participants’ personalities are assessed at two or more ages, the age trends based on cross-sectional and longitudinal analyses are usually not identical but typically do not differ greatly; that is, a given age trend identified in cross-sectional data tends to be broadly similar in longitudinal data.

1.2. Present study

In the present investigation, we examine age trends in the HEXACO personality factors, using self-reports from a large sample of online respondents. We think that this investigation has several features that, in combination, allow it to improve our understanding of age trends in personality. First, it is based on a relatively large sample size (about 100,000 persons) distributed across a wide segment of the lifespan, allowing meaningful comparisons across ages from 14 to the mid-70s. Second, it is based on a relatively long personality assessment—containing 100 items representing 25 personality facets—and thereby allows comparisons across several facets defining the same factor and even across several items assessing the same facet. Third, it is based on the six-dimensional HEXACO model of personality structure, and thereby captures a large element of valid personality variance that is not captured within the Big Five or even within the Five-Factor Model.

We briefly note the relations between the HEXACO and the Big Five dimensions (e.g., Ashton, Lee, & De Vries, 2014). The HEXACO Extraversion, Conscientiousness, and Openness to Experience dimensions are similar to their same-named counterparts in five-dimensional frameworks. HEXACO Agreeableness and Emotionality roughly represent rotated versions of Big Five Agreeableness and Neuroticism; however, Neuroticism also tends to correlate negatively with Extraversion of both five-dimensional and HEXACO frameworks. HEXACO Honesty-Humility shares some variance with Big Five Agreeableness (especially with NEO-based measures of Agreeableness). Crucially, however, measures of HEXACO Honesty-Humility, Agreeableness, and Emotionality each capture a substantial amount of valid variance that is not represented within five-dimensional measures, whereas the valid variance of the latter measures is essentially fully captured by measures of the HEXACO factors (see, e.g., Lee & Ashton, 2013). The fact that the present investigation is based on the HEXACO framework means that age trends can be examined for personality variables that have not previously been studied in relation to age.

We list below some tentative predictions based on the previous findings of age trends in the Big Five and on the associations of the Big Five with HEXACO factors and facets.

For the Extraversion, Conscientiousness, and Openness factors, predictions can in principle follow directly from findings based on the Big Five, although the heterogeneity of those findings makes such predictions less than obvious. For Extraversion, we expect a mainly downward trend for the Sociability facet, but upward trends for the other facets (Social Self-Esteem, Social Boldness, and Liveliness). For Conscientiousness, we expect a considerable upward trend across adulthood for the Prudence facet and a nearly flat trend for the Perfectionism facet; for all Conscientiousness facets, however, we expect an upward trend during adolescence and early adulthood. For Openness, we expect an upward trend during late adolescence and early adulthood, but beyond that point we have no clear predictions of strong age trends.

Previous findings of upward trends for Big Five Agreeableness would suggest upward trends for those HEXACO factors and facets that are related to that dimension. For example, the Altruism facet (which in the HEXACO framework represents a blend of Honesty-Humility, Emotionality, and Agreeableness) is strongly related to Big Five Agreeableness, and therefore we would expect upward age trends comparable to those observed for that dimension. The Big Five Agreeableness factor is also related to the HEXACO Honesty-Humility and Agreeableness factors and to the Sentimentality facet of HEXACO Emotionality. This leads to the expectation of some upward trends in those HEXACO variables. However, given the conceptual and empirical distinctiveness of those variables from one another, they may show widely differing age trends.

Analogously, previous findings of downward trends for Big Five Neuroticism would suggest downward trends for the HEXACO variables that are related to Neuroticism. For example, Neuroticism has a strong conceptual and empirical link with the Anxiety facet of Emotionality, so we would expect a downward trend for this variable. But the heterogeneity of previous age trends for Big Five Neuroticism renders this expectation somewhat uncertain.

To our knowledge, only one published study has investigated age trends in the six HEXACO personality factors. Kawamoto (2016) compared HEXACO-PI-R self-reports in 2000 Japanese adults aged 30–49. Kawamoto found that older persons in this sample averaged slightly higher in Honesty-Humility and lower in Emotionality than did younger persons of the same sex. These findings lead us to expect similar age trends within this age span in our sample.

One other study has also examined age trends in Honesty-Humility: the investigation by Sibley and Pirie (2013) obtained self-reports on a brief instrument that assesses a variant of Honesty-Humility in addition to the Big Five dimensions. Sibley and Pirie found that the strongest age trend for any of the six scales was that for Honesty-Humility, which (as inferred from the reported regression weights) was roughly a full standard deviation unit higher at age 80 than at age 20. This result leads us to expect a similar sizeable upward trend for Honesty-Humility in the present sample.

We should mention that we have not attempted to generate theoretically driven predictions for personality age trends. This is for two reasons. First, our prior knowledge of age trends in the Big Five makes it difficult for us to make truly a priori hypotheses not influenced by previous findings. Second, there simply does not yet exist any theory (or combination of theories) that can specify differentiated predictions for age trends across various factor- and facet-level traits through various points of the lifespan between the teens and the seventies. Some researchers have noted that longitudinal increases in certain aspects of personality (such as the Big Five Conscientiousness, Agreeableness, and Emotional Stability factors) appear to reflect a developmental increase in psychosocial maturity, which presumably results from changing social role demands (e.g., Roberts et al., 2006) or from intrinsic biological processes (Costa & McCrae, 2006). However, this observation does not allow a detailed specification of age trends for various traits.

2. Method

The participants in the present research were persons who provided anonymous self-reports on the online English-language 100-item HEXACO-PI-R at the hexaco.org website between October 19, 2014 and October 18, 2015. The HEXACO-PI-R items were administered in the English language, in their standard order, and with the standard response scale with options ranging from 1 (strongly disagree) to 5 (strongly agree). Participants received a personality summary profile based on their self-report responses.1

As described in other reports (Bogaert et al., submitted for publication, Lee and Ashton, in press), a total of 104,467 persons submitted self-report responses on the HEXACO-PI-R, and of these persons, 100,639 responded to all 100 HEXACO-PI-R items and also responded correctly to all three “attention-check” items interspersed throughout the inventory items. We then conducted two additional checks for data quality to the responses of this latter sample of participants, removing participants who (a) showed extremely low variation in raw item responses (i.e., through selecting the same or similar responses for all or nearly all items), or (b) showed extremely inconsistent item responses (indexed as the average within-subject standard deviation of item responses for each of the six broad HEXACO scales). A total of 100,318 persons remained in the sample after removal of the 321 persons who were screened out by the above criteria; this sample was used for computation of component scores. See Lee and Ashton (in press) for further details of these screening processes.

After completing the inventory, participants next responded to a series of demographic questions, asking them to indicate their age, sex, and various other data. Of the 100,318 respondents, 1373 did not report their age, and a very small additional number provided obviously nonsensical responses (e.g., 22 persons indicated an age under 6 years and 14 persons indicated an age over 105 years). For the purpose of this report we consider respondents who indicated an age for which at least 100 persons indicated that same value, so that the standard error of mean scores for that age group will be relatively small. In our sample, there were at least 100 persons who indicated each year of age between 14 and 74 inclusive, and the total number of respondents within this age range was 98,256. Of those persons, 97,583 reported their sex, and of that number 49,684 (50.9%) described themselves as male and 47,899 (49.1%) described themselves as female. Our analyses of age trends are therefore based on the sample of 97,583 persons who indicated their sex and who indicated an age between 14 and 74 years inclusive.

As described in Lee and Ashton (in press), we computed six varimax-rotated principal components from scale scores on the 25 HEXACO-PI-R facets using self-reports from the entire sample of 100,318 persons who met the screening criteria noted above. The use of varimax-rotated principal components allows the variance of the Altruism facet to be incorporated into scores for the six dimensions (recall that Altruism is not assigned univocally to any HEXACO-PI-R factor) and also orthogonalizes those dimensions.

To obtain the mean on a HEXACO-PI-R variable for persons of a given age, we first obtained those statistics for men and for women separately and then averaged them. In this way, differences across years of age in the proportions of men and women would not influence the observed age trends in personality trait levels for the full sample. We report component scores for the six broad HEXACO dimensions and standardized scores on each of the 25 facet-level scales. Alpha reliabilities of the facet-level scales ranged from 0.59 to 0.83 with an average of 0.73; we cannot directly compute alpha reliabilities of the six broad HEXACO components, but the closely corresponding factor-level scales had alpha reliabilities ranging from 0.82 to 0.89. The component scores are mutually orthogonal but the corresponding factor-level scale scores all showed intercorrelations under 0.20 except that Honesty-Humility and Agreeableness correlated 0.42 (see Lee & Ashton, in press, for intercorrelations of all six broad scales).2

3. Results

Fig. 1, Fig. 2, Fig. 3, Fig. 4, Fig. 5, Fig. 6 show the mean z-scores on each HEXACO component (and on the four facet scales that primarily define that component) as averaged across men and women for each year of age from 14 to 74 inclusive. The sample sizes and means for men and women of each year of age are given in the Supplementary Materials. Note that given the very large sample size of this investigation, in describing our results we follow Soto et al. (2011) in focusing on the patterns and sizes of age trends in mean personality trait levels, in avoiding the use of (largely superfluous) statistical significance tests, and in not reporting regression models containing higher-order polynomial age terms.3 We also follow Soto et al. (2011) in referring to the patterns of age differences in this cross-sectional sample as involving upward or downward trends rather than changes, increases, or decreases.

3.1. Honesty-Humility

As shown in Fig. 1, the Honesty-Humility facets showed mostly downward trends across the teen years followed by an upward trend to about age 60. These trends were broadly similar across the Honesty-Humility facets, and the overall Honesty-Humility component showed rather large age differences, being nearly one-quarter of a standard deviation unit higher at age 14 than at age 18, and about one full standard deviation unit lower at age 18 than in the 60s.

3.2. Emotionality

As shown in Fig. 2, the Emotionality facets showed rather varied patterns of age trends. Fearfulness showed little if any systematic age trend. Anxiety was relatively stable through the teens and 20s, but after 30 it showed a downward trend, with persons in their 70s averaging about one-half of a standard deviation unit below persons in their 20s. Dependence showed only weak age trends, with a slight upward trend from the early teens to the 20s, but with a modest downward trend beyond age 30. Sentimentality showed a modest upward trend from the teens to about age 60, with a difference of nearly one-third of a standard deviation unit, and then a weak downward trend through the mid-70s. The overall Emotionality component was relatively stable across the teens and 20s, and then showed a downward trend from the 30s; persons in their 70s averaged about one-quarter of a standard deviation below persons in their 20s.

3.3. Extraversion

As shown in Fig. 3, three of the Extraversion facets (Social Self-Esteem, Social Boldness, and Liveliness) showed upward age trends from the teens to the 70s. The steepest trend among these three facets was that for Social Self-Esteem, which was about four-fifths of a standard deviation higher in the 70s than in the early-to-mid teens. Social Boldness showed an upward trend only about half as large, with Liveliness showing an upward trend of intermediate size. Sociability showed a much different pattern of age trends, with an apparent upward trend across the mid teens followed by a downward trend from the late teens to about age 40, followed by a nearly stable trend; average levels in the late teens were about one-quarter of a standard deviation higher than those in middle age. For the overall Extraversion component, the trend was similar to that of the first three components mentioned above, with persons in their 70s averaging about half of a standard deviation higher than persons in their early-to-mid teens.

3.4. Agreeableness

As shown in Fig. 4, three of the Agreeableness facets (Forgivingness, Gentleness, and Patience) showed slight downward age trends from the late teens to about age 40, followed by modest upward age trends through age 70. For each of these facets, the average levels at age 40 were about one-fifth of a standard deviation below the late teen average and about one-quarter of a standard deviation below the 70s average. The Flexibility facet showed a different pattern, with an upward trend from the mid-to-late teens to about age 60, with a difference of about one-half of a standard deviation unit. The overall Agreeableness component showed age trends similar in direction and magnitude to those of the first three Agreeableness facets noted above.

3.5. Conscientiousness

As shown in Fig. 5, the Conscientiousness facets also showed upward age trends between the teens and mid-20s, with differences of about one-quarter to one-third of a standard deviation unit. Three of those facets (Organization, Diligence, and Prudence) also showed shallower upward age trends across the adult years. For Prudence, average scores in the decade around age 70 were nearly two-fifths of a standard deviation unit higher than in the mid-20s; for Organization and Diligence, the difference was about half as large. The Perfectionism facet showed a different pattern, with average levels being slightly lower beyond age 30 than in the mid-20s but with no consistent trend for ages beyond 30. The overall Conscientiousness component showed age trends similar to those of the first three Conscientiousness facets noted above, but with the overall difference between the teens and age 70 being about two-thirds of a standard deviation unit, or about as large as for the Prudence facet.

3.6. Openness

As shown in Fig. 6, the Openness facets showed upward age trends across the teen years, but otherwise differed in their patterns of age trends.4 The Unconventionality facet showed a weak downward trend beyond the mid-20s, such that by age 70 the average level was about three-tenths of a standard deviation lower than in the mid-20s. The Creativity facet showed little overall trend between the mid-20s and the 70s. The Aesthetic Appreciation facet remained stable from the early 20s to about age 40 and then showed a modest upward trend, reaching by about age 70 a level about three-tenths of a standard deviation higher than in the mid-20s. The Inquisitiveness facet showed a modest upward trend beyond the mid-20s, with an increase of nearly three-tenths of a standard deviation by about age 70. The overall Openness component showed an upward trend across the teen years, then a nearly stable pattern in the 20s and 30s, then some modest but inconsistent upward trend between the 40s and late 60s, and then a modest downward trend such that the mean in the early 70s was about the same as at age 20.

3.7. Interstitial facet scale: altruism

The Altruism facet showed an upward trend from the mid-to-late teens to about age 50, beyond which point it was largely stable through the 70s. The average level at age 50 was more than half of a standard deviation unit higher than in the teen years.

3.8. Age trends in men and in women

When we examined the age trends for the personality variables for each sex separately, we found that the age trends were mostly quite similar for men and for women. However, a few differences were noteworthy. For Honesty-Humility, men showed a steeper upward age trend than did women, with the difference between 18-year-olds and persons in their 60s and 70s being about 25% greater for men than for women. This interaction between sex and age means that sex differences in Honesty-Humility—a dimension on which women generally average higher than do men—were considerably larger for younger adults than for older adults: among 18-year-olds, women averaged nearly half of a standard deviation unit higher than did men, whereas for persons in their 60s and 70s, women averaged nearly one-quarter of a standard deviation unit higher than did men. Age differences in Honesty-Humility were larger than sex differences, with men in their 60s and 70s actually averaging more than three-fifths of a standard deviation unit higher than did 18-year-old women.

Age trends also differed somewhat across the sexes for some facets of Emotionality between the teens and 20s: women but not men showed a modest upward trend in Dependence, whereas men but not women showed a modest downward trend in Fearfulness.

3.9. Differences in age trends between items of the same facet scale

The results described above revealed some considerable differences in age trends between facets defining the same dimension. In examining individual items of the HEXACO-PI-R, we found also that items belonging to the same facet scale often showed substantially different age trends. In some cases, the overall age trend across the teens-to-70s period was opposite in direction for items from the same facet scale.

For example, consider the following reverse-keyed Fearfulness items: “I don’t mind doing jobs involving dangerous work” showed a modest upward age trend roughly from age 20 to age 60 (i.e., younger people reported being less concerned about dangerous work), whereas “Even in an emergency I wouldn’t feel like panicking” showed a downward age trend from the teens through the 70s (i.e., older people reported less tendency to panic).

As another example, the Sociability item “I enjoy having lots of people around to talk with” showed a downward age trend from the late teens to age 60 (i.e., younger people reported more enjoyment of having people around), whereas the reverse-keyed Sociability item “I avoid making “small talk” with people” showed an upward age trend from the 20s through about age 60 (i.e., older people reported being more disposed to engage in small talk).

Another example involves the Diligence item “I often push myself very hard when trying to achieve a goal”, which showed a downward age trend roughly from age 20 to age 40 (i.e., younger people reported pushing themselves harder), and the reverse-keyed Diligence item “I do only the minimum amount of work needed to get by”, which showed a considerable upward age trend from the teens through age 70 (i.e., older people reported a greater tendency to do more than minimal work).

The above examples of items from the same facet scale showing widely differing age trends might suggest that these facet-level traits could in principle be subdivided further into even narrower, more specific traits.5 In other cases, however, differences in age trends between items of the same facet scale might represent instances of a general tendency for age trends to be steeper for undesirable item statements than for desirable item statements. That is, we noticed that older persons appeared to endorse undesirable item statements much less than younger persons did, but that older persons appeared to endorse desirable item statements only moderately more than younger persons did. To illustrate this phenomenon, we constructed two scales, one consisting of the most desirable item from each facet scale, and one consisting of the least desirable item from each facet scale. Note that each scale was computed on the basis of raw item responses (i.e., before any reverse-scoring), and that desirability ratings—as averaged across six graduate student raters who independently rated each item statement on a five-point scale (see De Vries, Realo, & Allik, submitted for publication)—were likewise based on the original item statements.6 As would be expected, the age trends for these two scales were roughly opposite: scores on the desirable items scale showed a rather steady upward trend from about age 16, whereas scores on the undesirable items scale showed a rather steady downward from about age 16. However, the overall downward trend for the undesirable scale (about 1.2 standard deviation units between the teens and the 70s) was steeper than the upward trend for the desirable scale (about 0.7 standard deviation units between the teens and the 70s).

4. Discussion

4.1. Summary of results for HEXACO factors and facets

We begin by summarizing results briefly for each HEXACO personality dimension:

Honesty-Humility showed a downward age trend during the teen years and then an upward age trend through age 60, with a full standard deviation difference between 18 and the 60s. Age trends for Emotionality and Extraversion differed by facet, but the overall trends were downward for Emotionality and upward for Extraversion. Agreeableness showed only weak age trends, with levels around age 40 being slightly lower than those around age 20 and around age 60. Conscientiousness and Openness showed upward age trends during the teen years (extending to the mid-20s for Conscientiousness), but age trends thereafter differed considerably by facet, with some showing upward trends but some showing stable or even downward trends.

4.2. Comparisons with previous findings

Our findings of considerable differences in age trends between facet-level personality traits defining the same broad dimension are reminiscent of some previous results. For example, consider our findings for the various Extraversion facets, whereby Social Boldness and Social Self-Esteem showed upward age trends and Sociability showed a downward age trend. These results broadly parallel those reported by Roberts et al. (2006) and by Soto and John (2012). Likewise, we also found differing age trends for the various Conscientiousness facets, with Prudence showing a fairly large upward age trend, Organization and Diligence showing smaller upward age trends, and Perfectionism showing a stable age trend. These results are also roughly consistent with previous findings of heterogeneous age trends for Conscientiousness facets (Jackson et al., 2009, Soto and John, 2012), despite some differences for certain facets.

Our finding of an upward trend for Openness during the teen years is consistent with previous findings, as is our finding of some differences between Openness facets in their age trends. But our findings are novel with regard to the specific pattern of Openness facet differences in age trends after young adulthood. In particular, we found strikingly large differences between, on the one hand, the Aesthetic Appreciation and Inquisitiveness facets (which showed considerable upward trends throughout adulthood), and on the other hand, the Unconventionality facet (which showed a downward trend throughout adulthood). We suspect that these differences may be better captured by the HEXACO-PI-R than by other inventories because many of the HEXACO-PI-R items of these facets describe behavioral tendencies rather than global trait evaluations.

Previous studies have generally shown downward age trends for Big Five Neuroticism, and the present results are mostly consistent with those findings. For example, the Anxiety facet of Emotionality showed a substantial downward age trend. If we compute for the present sample a HEXACO Neuroticism scale using the combination of facets given in the formula by Lee and Ashton (2013), persons in their 70s average more than three-quarters of a standard deviation unit lower than do persons in their teens.

The finding of little age trend for HEXACO Agreeableness is actually not inconsistent with previous findings of considerable upward age trends for Big Five Agreeableness. Although HEXACO Agreeableness overlaps somewhat with Big Five Agreeableness, the latter is more closely associated with the HEXACO-PI-R Altruism facet, which divides its loadings between Honesty-Humility, Emotionality, and Agreeableness. In the present study the Altruism facet showed a considerable upward trend, with persons older than 50 averaging more than half of a standard deviation unit higher than persons in their teens. This difference is comparable to previous findings involving Big Five Agreeableness (e.g., Soto et al., 2011).

Nevertheless, the very weak age trends for HEXACO Agreeableness are somewhat surprising in light of the fact that most previous studies have not only found upward age trends for Big Five Agreeableness but also downward age trends for Big Five Neuroticism. Given that HEXACO Agreeableness shows positive associations with Big Five Agreeableness but negative associations with Big Five Neuroticism, one would expect HEXACO Agreeableness to show some considerable upward age trend. The absence of such a trend indicates that the element of HEXACO Agreeableness which is not shared with those Big Five dimensions would actually show a modest downward age trend. One simple way to appreciate this point is to consider that the correlation of participant age with the HEXACO Agreeableness component was 0.01 in this sample, whereas the partial correlation controlling for the Altruism and Anxiety facets was −0.09.

Finally, we note that the age trends observed in the present study are in some ways similar to those of Kawamoto (2016), who found an upward trend for Honesty-Humility (and a smaller downward trend for Emotionality) between the ages of 30 and 49. Likewise, our findings of upward trends in Honesty-Humility throughout much of adulthood are similar to those of Sibley and Pirie (2013).

4.3. Interpretation of upward age trends in Honesty-Humility (and lack thereof in Agreeableness)

Perhaps the most striking findings of our study are those for Honesty-Humility and its facets. As described in the Results section, Honesty-Humility showed a downward trend across the teen years but then showed a consistent upward trend in the following decades, such that persons in their 60s and 70s averaged about one full standard deviation unit higher than did 18-year-olds. Each of the Honesty-Humility facets showed a similar upward trend, and in fact every Honesty-Humility item showed a clear upward trend after the teen years. These age trends for Honesty-Humility differed sharply from those for Agreeableness. The Agreeableness component and three of its four defining facets showed only modest age differences, with slight downward trends across young adulthood followed by upward trends from about age 40.

We consider the age trends for Honesty-Humility and Agreeableness in relation to our theoretical interpretation of those dimensions as reflecting two forms of cooperative or reciprocally altruistic tendency. As explained in detail elsewhere (e.g., Ashton & Lee, 2007), we interpret low Honesty-Humility as a tendency to exploit others and low Agreeableness as a tendency to react against perceived exploitation by others. We suggest that the age differences in Honesty-Humility represent an evolved developmental pattern that reflects a tendency for gains from exploiting others (relative to cooperating with others) to be potentially higher at the beginning of adulthood—a period when competition for mates, status, and resources is especially intense—as compared with later adulthood.

In relation to the above point, we note that the intensity of intrasexual competition is greater for men than for women, which follows from the fact that a competitively successful man can potentially have more offspring than can a competitively successful woman, due to the much sharper physiological limit on women’s reproductive success. This sex difference in intrasexual competition is most pronounced during late adolescence or early adulthood, and fits with our finding that Honesty-Humility showed considerably larger sex differences in the late teens or early 20s than in middle or late adulthood; phrased another way, the upward age trend in Honesty-Humility was steeper for men than for women. Nevertheless, the finding of substantial age trends even among women in Honesty-Humility—downward during the teens and upward thereafter—would suggest some considerable degree of intrasexual competition among young women.

With regard to Agreeableness, we suggest that there is not necessarily any evolved tendency for large developmental changes across the lifespan, as the costs of being exploited by others (relative to not cooperating with others) seem likely to be relatively constant across the lifespan (and also across the sexes). We do not know why Agreeableness tends to be about one-fifth of a standard deviation lower close to 40 years of age than at 20 or at 60, but one possibility is that many persons at that middle stage will be faced with the challenges of raising children, and that these challenges will lead to greater self-perceived anger-proneness and argumentativeness. In any case, the lack of an overall upward age trend for Agreeableness stands as an exception to the pattern whereby personality age trends appear to reflect greater psychological maturity in older persons: the forgivingness and even temper that define Agreeableness subjectively seem to be “mature” tendencies but show little upward age trend.

The strong upward age trend in Honesty-Humility beyond the late teens is perhaps the most noteworthy finding of the present investigation. The large sample size, in combination with the consistency of the upward age trends across all facets (and even across all items) of Honesty-Humility, suggests that the present finding of strong age trends in this dimension is likely to be robust. The fact that this important result had not been—and could not be—observed in studies based on the Big Five illustrates the importance of assessing all of the major personality dimensions. Those investigations could not detect what is arguably the largest—and possibly the most socially significant—age trend in personality.

Age trends in Honesty-Humility may help to explain age differences in crime rates, particularly the well-known “age-crime curve”, whereby crime rates increase between childhood and the mid-to-late teens and then decrease thereafter. Honesty-Humility (and particularly its Fairness facet) is associated negatively with delinquent and antisocial behaviors (e.g., Ashton and Lee, 2008, Gaughan et al., 2012), and in the present study we found the lowest levels of Honesty-Humility among persons in their late teens. This pattern of age trends was similar across all four Honesty-Humility facets; that is, it was observed not only for the Fairness facet (whose item content involves (hypothetical) criminal dishonesty) but also, and to nearly the same extent, for the other three facets (whose item content does not involve criminal dishonesty). Recall also that age differences in Honesty-Humility exceeded sex differences, with men in their 40s averaging higher in Honesty-Humility than did women in their late teens. This result is consistent with the fact that overall crime rates for women in their late teens actually exceed those of men in their 40s (Uniform Crime Reporting Program (U.S.), 2003). Thus, the present results support the suggestion (Blonigen, 2010) that the age-crime curve might be understood in relation to developmental changes in personality.7

4.4. Item-level differences in age trends

We found heterogeneous age trends not only for facet-level personality traits defining the same broad dimension but also, in some cases, for items defining the same facet. Given that the HEXACO-PI-R items of a given facet tend to define that facet to broadly similar extents (Lee & Ashton, in press), the larger of these inter-item differences are not likely simply to reflect some differential relevance of items to their intended traits. Instead, this result seems in part to be due to the specific content of particular items. One implication of this finding is that even rather highly intercorrelated scales intended to assess the same construct may also differ in their age trends as a function of the particular items that define them. Thus, not only will estimates of age trends for broad personality dimensions depend in part on the particular array of facet-level traits that define those dimensions, but estimates of age trends for narrower personality traits will likewise depend in part on the specific sets of items used in assessing those traits (see also Mõttus et al., 2015). In other words, a focus on broad personality dimensions is likely to conceal much of the age-related variation in personality, both because the broad dimensions may show relatively weak age trends and also because narrower personality traits—and even items assessing the same narrow personality trait—may show widely differing age trends.

One potential solution to this problem of determining the exact age trends for personality characteristics is to use complex CFA models to partition variance due to various sources, such as (a) the broad factors (with most facets and/or items having some appreciable secondary loadings), (b) the many narrower facet traits, (c) source-specific response styles (desirability and acquiescence) and (d) specific variance in each item. However, there remain some important practical difficulties, because minor differences in the specification of such models or in the sets of items used could produce substantial differences in estimates of the age trends associated with the various characteristics. (For an example in relation to item selection, see Mõttus et al. (2015).)

Another noteworthy finding involving differences between items was that endorsement of undesirable items showed a downward trend that was steeper than the upward trend for endorsement of desirable items. We should emphasize that our ad hoc scales consisting of desirable items and of undesirable items will each contain considerable substantive variance from the various personality characteristics; that is, they are to a large extent composites of such characteristics. Also, although these scales will contain some variance due to biases in responding, these biases are not likely to reflect “faking” in any important degree, given the anonymous, low-stakes conditions in which data were obtained.

The difference in the steepness of age trends for desirable-item and undesirable-item scales can also be viewed in terms of age trends in tendencies for desirable-versus-undesirable responding and for elevated or acquiescent responding. That is, suppose that older persons are more likely to endorse desirable items and less likely to endorse undesirable items, whether due to true age differences in the traits being assessed or due to response styles independent of trait levels, or both. Suppose also that older persons tend to indicate a lower overall level of endorsement of items independent of desirability. These two tendencies in combination would then produce the pattern whereby the upward trend for desirable items is less steep than the downward trend for undesirable items. Note that if we use the present dataset to compute a desirable-versus-undesirable items scale as the difference between the desirable-items and undesirable-items scales, and an elevation or acquiescence scale as the sum of those two scales, then we find an upward age trend for the former and a smaller downward age trend for the latter. A better understanding of age differences in self-report response tendencies will await the use of more sophisticated techniques (see, e.g., Wetzel et al., 2016, Zettler et al., 2016).8

5. Limitations and future directions

One limitation of the present participant sample is that the participants were self-selected. First they came to the hexaco.org website by searching for an online personality inventory or by following links found in online news articles or provided by other persons. Then they agreed to complete the HEXACO-PI-R in exchange for some basic scoring and interpretive information generated from their personality self-reports. Our descriptive data based on this online sample (see Lee & Ashton, in press) suggests that its participants tend to be somewhat above the population average in Openness to Experience, a result that might be expected given that participants were self-selected on the basis of being interested in exploring their personality profiles (see Bogaert et al., submitted for publication). With regard to the age trends in personality obtained in this study, it is possible that these trends reflect, at least to some small extent, differences between age groups in the extent to which personality trait levels are associated with the tendency to arrive at the hexaco.org website and then to complete the inventory. For example, if older persons with a higher level of a given trait are more likely to be participants in this sample, but if younger persons with a lower level of that same trait are more likely to be participants in this sample, then these opposing trends would produce some artifactual age trends in that personality trait. But although we acknowledge this hypothetical possibility, we do not have any a priori hypotheses about which traits should differentially influence participation probabilities across age groups, and we think it is unlikely that the age trends (and lack of age trends) observed in this study are attributable in any large part to this phenomenon. Nevertheless, it would be of interest to obtain data from very large samples that are representative of the population across the entire span of adolescence and adulthood.

Another limitation of our study is that our data are entirely cross-sectional and therefore cannot establish whether the observed age trends represent developmental changes or cohort effects. However, we believe that developmental changes are likely to be chiefly responsible for the age trends. One reason for this opinion is that previous research comparing longitudinal and cross-sectional personality age trends in reasonably large samples has found those trends to be broadly similar (e.g., Terracciano et al., 2005). Similarly, in Soto et al. (2011), data collected over a period of seven years showed virtually zero influence of year of participation on any of the Big Five mean scores, a result that suggests no substantial cohort or history effects.

Another reason for our opinion that the age trends in personality primarily reflect developmental changes is simply our subjective impression that these trends are unlikely to be due to cohort effects. For example, consider the age trends in Honesty-Humility. If these trends were due entirely to cohort effects, then this would mean that any such effects that increase Honesty-Humility must have been roughly stable for persons born between 1940 and 1955, then consistently in decline for persons born between 1955 and about 1997, and then increasing for persons born after 1997. We think it is instead more plausible that Honesty-Humility actually decreases between the early and late teens and then increases throughout early and middle adulthood. Likewise, consider also the age trends for Conscientiousness and Openness, both of which showed an upward trend during the teens (extending to the mid-20s for Conscientiousness) and then a roughly stable trend through about the mid-40s. If these changes were due entirely to cohort effects, then this would mean that any such effects that increase those dimensions must have been roughly stable for persons born between 1970 and 1990 or 1995, and then in sharp decline for persons born thereafter. We think it is instead more plausible that Conscientiousness and Openness actually increase during the teen years. Future research using longitudinal designs will be able to test these hypotheses.

6. Conclusion

Our analyses of age differences in the HEXACO personality dimensions indicate some similarities with previous findings based on the Big Five factors, but also some differences and some new results. Particularly noteworthy are (a) the upward trends from the late teens through late middle age for Honesty-Humility and its facets, and (b) for the other five dimensions, the considerable differences in age trends between facets belonging to the same factor and even between items belonging to the same facet.
