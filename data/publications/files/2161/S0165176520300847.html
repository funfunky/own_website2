External validity of a laboratory measure of cheating: Evidence from Czech juvenile detention centers☆

Author links open overlay panelLubomírCinglaVáclavKorbelb
https://doi.org/10.1016/j.econlet.2020.109094Get rights and content

Highlights

•

Lab-in-the-field experiment with adolescent delinquents.

•

Cheating is measured with a laboratory coin-toss task.

•

Coin-toss results significantly correlate with short- and long-term field measures.

•

Results support external validity of a lab cheating task into natural environments.

Abstract

Evidence of external validity of laboratory measures of the propensity to cheat is scarce. We conduct a lab-in-the-field experiment with 303 youths incarcerated in juvenile detention centers in the Czech Republic to study whether a laboratory measure of cheating correlates with their field behavior. We combine two complementary field-behavior measures: current (mis-)behavior reported by detention center staff as a short-term individual characteristic, and a sum of the types of delinquent acts (e.g. aggression, truancy, petty crimes) officially recorded as the reasons for their detention as a long-term characteristic. We find that behavior in the laboratory task measuring cheating significantly correlates with both field measures. Overall, our results suggest that the experimental cheating measure may generalize well into natural environments.

1. Introduction

Dishonest behavior is widespread in societies and carries serious economic and non-economic consequences. On an individual level, dishonesty and cheating often correlate with serious behavioral problems including aggression and delinquency, especially in childhood and adolescence (Stouthamer-Loeber, 1986).

Dishonest behavior is rather difficult to measure in the field. Therefore, most of our understanding of its underlying mechanisms comes from controlled laboratory experiments (Abeler et al., 2019). These experiments typically use paradigms involving a game of luck in which participants are instructed to follow an explicit rule that requires them to privately report the outcome(s) of a random device, but where the game set-up simultaneously motivates players to intentionally break the rule by misreporting the outcome in order to increase their payoff. By design, misreporting is impossible to detect on an individual level but can be identified at the aggregate level due to the law of large numbers and the known distribution of outcomes. Prominent examples include the die-under-the-cup task (Fischbacher and Föllmi-Heusi, 2013), and the coin toss (Bucciol and Piovesan, 2011). Key restrictions are their artificiality and limited generalizability.

Several studies have aimed to increase the external validity of the laboratory measures by applying them to non-student populations (Cohn et al., 2019), and by relating them to real-life norm-violations, such as fare-dodging (Dai et al., 2018), absenteeism at work (Hanna and Wang, 2017), or student misbehavior (Cohn and Maréchal, 2018). We argue that with one exception (Cohn et al., 2015), these real-world norm violations have been relatively minor and short-term, which limits the generalizability of the findings.

In this paper we extend the current literature by showing how a standard experimental measure of cheating (the coin-flipping task, Bucciol and Piovesan, 2011) correlates with two complementary measures of field misbehavior in a sample of 303 youth incarcerated in juvenile detention centers (JDCs) in the Czech Republic. Field behavior was assessed using (i) current (mis-)behavior as reported by JDC staff, and (ii) the total number of infractions reported in administrative records as reasons for their detention (e.g. aggression, truancy, petty thefts), which we use as proxy measures of short-term and long-term behavioral characteristics, respectively.

2. Methodology

We conducted a lab-in-the-field experiment with 303 adolescents in grades 5–9 from 15 JDCs in the Czech Republic.1 JDCs are correctional facilities for children aged 6–15 with severe behavioral problems, but who are typically not formally charged with a crime. The experiment occurred in May and June 2016. At that time, approximately 600 adolescents were incarcerated in all JDCs in the Czech Republic. Participants in our experiment were, on average, 14.4 years old, 69% were boys, they had on average 4.4 siblings, and only 34% came from a two-parent family.

Participants made decisions in the coin-flipping task (Bucciol and Piovesan, 2011) with a fair coin with black and white sides. They tossed it 20 times and reported each outcome on an answer sheet. If the coin landed showing the white side, they would earn one token, while the black side was worth nothing. Participants worked individually in cubicles (Figure A1 in ESM) which assured maximum privacy. They were not monitored and hence could misreport the outcome of the tosses.2

The first field measure (Index-of-misbehavior) is a subjective evaluation of the day-to-day behavior in the facility reported by responsible JDC staff on a scale from 1 (best) to 5 (worst) with 0.5 steps. The second measure (Sum-of-behavioral-problems) represents a sum of all types of infractions reported as reasons for JDC placement in the subject’s administrative records. Most frequent were being disrespectful to authorities (67%), truancy (51%), aggressive acts (50%), running away (30%), and drug and alcohol use (33%); 90% of subjects exhibited multiple problems. Both measures are significantly correlated (ρ=0.21, p<0.01).

The experiment was conducted in Czech in a pen and paper format. We obtained written permission from each headmaster prior to executing the experiment. The sessions took place during regular school classes and lasted about 45 min. All students present that day participated. They were informed that they could stop and leave anytime, but none did. Participants did not write their names anywhere to ensure full anonymity.3 They were paid in tokens which they exchanged at the end of the session for various rewards (sweets, sports equipment, toys, school supplies). The average pay-off was 21.7 tokens (66 CZK or 2.5 EUR) including a five-token show-up fee, which represents approximately 30% of their monthly allowance as defined by law.

3. Results

Subjects reported, on average, 68% of tosses as winning, which suggests that a sizeable fraction cheated, as it is significantly more than the 50% predicted by a random draw (t-test, p<0.001,t=20.34). Similarly, the whole distribution is shifted significantly higher than the binomial distribution (Kolmogorov–Smirnov test p<0.001).

Our main result is that the laboratory measure correlates with both field measures of misbehavior. We first split the sample according to the median share of reported winning tosses, i.e. those reporting fewer or equal to 65%, and those reporting more. Fig. 1 depicts a comparison of the two groups with respect to the two field measures. In Panel A, the group reporting a higher share of winning tosses than the median also shows an average of 0.26 points higher Index-of-misbehavior than the group reporting a lower share (0.26 SD; 2.48 vs. 2.74, t-test p=0.032,t=−2.155). Panel B presents a comparison for the Sum-of-behavioral-problems. The group reporting more than the median share of winning tosses has on average by 0.6 more officially recorded infractions than the other group (0.33 SD; 3.18 vs. 3.78, t-test p=0.007,t=−2.719).

We further apply a regression analysis (Table 1), where we regress the two field measures on the share of reported winning tosses, first without control variables. Columns 1 and 2 report that one SD more reported winning tosses in the cheating game is associated with 0.18 and 0.2 SD increases in the Index-of-misbehavior and in the Sum-of-behavioral-problems (p=0.004,p=0.041). Adding additional control variables does not fundamentally change the magnitude or standard errors of the main coefficients (Columns 3 and 4; p=0.028,p=0.039).4

Next, we test the robustness of our results. Approximately 60% of the sample participated in another experiment 6–12 months prior to this (Cingl and Korbel, 2018), which might affect their behavior in the current experiment. Therefore, we repeat the analysis on the inexperienced subjects only. Columns 5 and 6 document that our results are qualitatively robust to exclusion of the subjects who participated in the previous experiment.

4. Conclusion

We study whether a laboratory measure of the propensity to cheat is linked with field behavior in a sample of juveniles incarcerated in juvenile detention centers in the Czech Republic. We find that the coin-flipping task significantly correlates with two field measures of delinquent behavior — their current (mis-)behavior in the center (a short-term behavioral characteristic), and the outside infractions that resulted in their incarceration (a long-term characteristic). Our results, however, cannot speak to the flow of causality between a stay in the institution and the severity of their behavior or predict the behavior of subjects in the future.

We contribute to the emerging literature on the external validity of experimental measures. Our results complement the literature on the generalizability of laboratory cheating tasks to real life behavior (Dai et al., 2018, Hanna and Wang, 2017, Cohn et al., 2015, Cohn and Maréchal, 2018). This literature typically measures correlations with small transgressions like fare-dodging, classroom misbehavior or absenteeism. To our best knowledge, we are the first to document the relationship of both short-term and long-term variables of problematic behavior and a laboratory measure of cheating. Furthermore, our paper is the first to document such a relationship for juveniles with a history of delinquent behavior.

From a policy perspective, our results suggest a large potential of a simple cheating task for further research on underlying mechanisms driving dishonesty and delinquency. We believe that it can provide a suitable and an easy-to-collect proxy variable to study the effects of targeted interventions on delinquent behavior, which is especially important as childhood and adolescence are recognized as critical periods for development of preferences (Fehr et al., 2013), and remedial interventions administered in these stages should therefore result in the strongest positive impact.
