Volume 54, Issue 6, April 2013, Pages 756-760

Tales of two self-control scales: Relations with Five-Factor and HEXACO traits

Author links open overlay panelReinout E.de VriesaJean-Louisvan Gelderb
https://doi.org/10.1016/j.paid.2012.12.023Get rights and content

Abstract

This study compared the Five-Factor and HEXACO personality correlates of two common self-control constructs – Tangney self-control and Grasmick self-control – and their relations with delinquency. In both a student and a community sample, conscientiousness (mainly the prudence facet) was the most consistent and strongest correlate of both constructs. HEXACO honesty–humility was an important correlate of Grasmick self-control but not of Tangney self-control. Additionally, honesty–humility (mainly the fairness facet) was the most consistent predictor of delinquency, whereas the two self-control scales differed in the extent to which they predicted delinquency across samples.

Highlights

► Tangney and Grasmick self-control were related to Five-Factor and HEXACO traits. ► Both self-control scales were most strongly related to conscientiousness – prudence. ► Grasmick self-control was also related to HEXACO honesty–humility. ► Honesty–humility – fairness was the most consistent predictor of delinquency.

1. Introduction

Self-control is among the most widely studied constructs in the social sciences (Duckworth, 2011). This is not without reason; lack of self-control has been implicated in a wide variety of important life outcomes, such as poor health, teenage pregnancy, financial problems, drug abuse, and delinquency, even when controlling for intelligence and socio-economic background (Moffitt et al., 2011).

In spite of widespread scholarly interest in different disciplines such as psychology, behavioral economics, and criminology, and its pervasive influence on the lives of people, there is a lack of consensus regarding the nature of self-control and its relations with more general dimensions of personality. According to Gottfredson and Hirschi (1990), for example, people low in self-control are predisposed to engage in irresponsible behaviors because of a (learned) preference to respond to tangible, physical, and exciting stimuli in the immediate environment, a lack of tenacity and tolerance when facing frustrations, and an indifference to the needs and sufferings of others. In contrast, Tangney, Baumeister, and Boone (2004) define self-control as the “ability to override or change one’s inner responses, as well as to interrupt undesired behavioral tendencies (such as impulses) and refrain from acting on them” (p. 274). Whereas the Gottfredson and Hirschi (1990) conceptualization locates self-control in a set of different – but purportedly related – traits, Tangney et al. (2004) specifically locate it in theories on self-regulation, whereby the self is able to override its impulses and alter its moods, emotions, and behaviors.

This difference in conceptualization may be reflected in the different traits that have been linked to self-control. Self-control has been associated with conscientiousness alone (O’Gorman and Baxter, 2002), with a combination of conscientiousness, agreeableness, and emotional stability (Olson, 2005, Tangney et al., 2004), with sensation-seeking (Mishra & Lalumière, 2011), and with psychopathy (Jonason & Tost, 2010). Although both sensation-seeking and psychopathy have been related to conscientiousness, sensation-seeking has also been found to be related to openness to experience and extraversion (De Vries, De Vries, & Feij, 2009), whereas psychopathy has been found to be related to (low) honesty–humility, (low) emotionality, and (low) agreeableness (De Vries et al., 2008, De Vries and Van Kampen, 2010, Lee and Ashton, 2005). Consequently, although conscientiousness appears to be a central element of self-control, all of the other main personality dimensions have – in one study or another – also been implicated as correlates of self-control.

Self-control has been found to be an important and consistent predictor of delinquency (Pratt & Cullen, 2000), but there is a lack of studies comparing the effects of self-control on delinquency with the effects of traits from general models of personality. Several Five-Factor traits, i.e. (low) conscientiousness, neuroticism (low emotional stability), and (low) agreeableness have been shown to be correlates of delinquency (Caspi et al., 1994, Eysenck, 1977, Lynam, 2002, Miller and Lynam, 2001). In the HEXACO model, which posits six instead of five fundamental personality dimensions (Ashton et al., 2004), the main correlate of delinquency is honesty–humility (Lee et al., 2005, Van Gelder and De Vries, 2012). It is unclear, however, whether any Five-Factor or HEXACO personality variables are able to offer incremental validity in the prediction of delinquency beyond self-control.

Consequently, the present study sets out to answer the following questions: (1) “what are the main personality correlates of self-control?” and (2) “to what extent do the main personality dimensions, and especially honesty–humility, offer incremental validity beyond self-control in the prediction of delinquency?” We do so using (a) two common conceptualizations of self-control: one developed by Grasmick, Tittle, Bursik, and Arneklev (1993) and based on Gottfredson and Hirschi’s (1990) ‘General Theory of Crime’, which we will call ‘Grasmick self-control’, and another rooted in psychological research, which we will call ‘Tangney self-control’ (Tangney et al., 2004), and (b) two broad-bandwidth conceptualizations of personality, the Five-Factor model and the HEXACO model.

2. Method

2.1. Samples and procedures

2.1.1. Study 1

In this study, 775 undergraduate Psychology and Education students from two consecutive years, who had filled out a personality assessment as part of a course requirement, were sent an email invitation to participate in a follow-up study. In total, 133 (11 men, 122 women; 15% response rate) with a mean age of 19.96 years (SD = 2.22) agreed to participate in this study using a web survey. Of these, 72 students (54%) had filled out the personality assessment 3 months prior to the present study and 61 students (46%) had done so 15 months prior to it. In exchange for participation, respondents were entered in a raffle for one of two €100 gift certificates.

2.1.2. Study 2

A total of 1377 members of a large-scale internet research panel, for whom personality data were available, were approached through email. This community sample is representative of the Dutch adult population in terms of age, gender, education, and province of residence. Of all panel members targeted, 51% completed the web-based survey. This sample consisted of 709 adults (376 (53%) women) between 19 and 88 years old (M = 50.88, SD = 14.36). As in Study 1, data were gathered over two waves; personality data were gathered 1.5 years prior to the self-control and delinquency data.1

2.2. Instruments

2.2.1. Grasmick and Tangney self-control

Both the 24-item ‘Grasmick self-control’ scale (Grasmick et al., 1993) and the 13-item ‘Tangney self-control’ scale (Tangney et al., 2004) were forward and backward translated to create Dutch versions of these scales. The alpha reliabilities of both self-control scales were >.80 (Table 1).

2.2.2. Five Factor marker scales (Study 1)

From the original Dutch lexical personality study (De Raad, Hendriks, & Hofstee, 1992; see also De Vries, Ashton, & Lee, 2009), the 30 highest loading adjectives of each of the main five factors were extracted and presented to students using a four-point (0 = strongly disagree – 3 = strongly agree) scale. Alpha reliabilities ranged from .84 for intellect to .93 for extraversion.

2.2.3. 5DPT (Study 2)

The 5-Dimensional Personality Test (5DPT; Coolidge et al., 2008, De Vries and Van Kampen, 2010, Van Kampen, 2009) consists of 20 dichotomous (yes–no) items for each of the five factor scales, which have been found to converge (all convergent r’s > .55) with the NEO-FFI (Van Kampen, 2012). In this study, all alpha reliabilities exceeded .83.

2.2.4. HEXACO-PI-R

The (Dutch version of the) revised HEXACO Personality Inventory (Ashton and Lee, 2008, De Vries et al., 2009) consists of 200 items divided among the following six domain scales, honesty–humility, emotionality, eXtraversion, agreeableness, conscientiousness, and openness to experience, each of which consists of four facets, plus an additional (interstitial) facet, named Altruism. All of the alpha reliabilities of the HEXACO-PI-R factor scales were .84 or higher. In both studies, some differences in HEXACO personality were observed between the first and second wave (remaining) respondents. Specifically, in the student sample, students who participated in the second wave were slightly higher on honesty–humility (M2 = 3.70 (SD2 = .50) versus M1 = 3.57 (SD1 = .48); t = 2.89, p < .01) and on conscientiousness (M2 = 3.44 (SD2 = .48) versus M1 = 3.27 (SD1 = .50); t = 3.76, p < .01). Similarly, in the community sample, respondents in the second wave were somewhat higher on honesty–humility (M2 = 3.77 (SD2 = .46) versus M1 = 3.61 (SD1 = .50); t = 6.05, p < .01) and on conscientiousness (M2 = 3.44 (SD2 = .38) versus M1 = 3.39 (SD1 = .41); t = 2.17, p < .05).

2.2.5. Delinquency

In both studies, a 20-item modified version of the Self-Report Delinquency questionnaire (Donker, Smeenk, Van der Laan, & Verhulst, 2003), was used. Self-report measures have been shown to be reliable and valid predictors of delinquency (Caspi et al., 1994, Thornberry and Krohn, 2000). The items used refer to a wide array of delinquent acts, both minor offenses and more serious ones, such as vandalism, theft, burglary, assault, (hard)drug dealing, and fraud. We calculated the means of all items, which were measured using 6-point scales with the following options: (1) Never, (2) Never, but I have been tempted to do so, (3) 1–2 times, (4) 3–5 times, (5) 6–10 times, (6) more than ten times. In the student sample, one of the items (about tax evasion) was replaced by an item measuring failure to repay owed money. Furthermore, one item (about theft from a parked car) had no variance in the student sample and was omitted. The final alpha reliabilities were of .66 in the student sample (Study 1) and .76 in the community sample (Study 2).

2.3. Analyses

Apart from the correlational analyses reported in Table 1, we used regression and relative weights analyses to further explore the relations between the personality variables on the one hand and the self-control and delinquency variables on the other. Especially when collinearity among the independent variables occurs (as may be the case with the background variables gender and age, self-control, and the personality variables), relative weights provide a more accurate estimation of the proportionate contribution of each of the variables in an equation than regression and semipartial correlation coefficients do (Johnson, 2000, LeBreton and Tonidandel, 2008). The relative weight of an independent variable is expressed as a percentage of the total explained variance in the dependent variable. In all analyses, we controlled for gender and age, because these have been found to be important correlates of self-control and crime (Burton, Cullen, Evans, Alarid, & Dunaway, 1998).

As a second step, we added HEXACO honesty–humility to the Five-Factor scales to see whether it added incremental variance in the prediction of the self-control variables. As Table 2 shows, the results with Tangney self-control were rather unequivocal. In both studies and with all three instruments, it was mainly characterized by its relation with conscientiousness (or: orderliness, in the case of the 5DPT). In all four regression equations, more than 50% of the total explained variance was due to conscientiousness. None of the other variables explained more than 25% of the total explained variance in Tangney self-control, although in the case of the Five-Factor scales, lexical emotional stability (with 23% of the total explained variance) and 5DPT Neuroticism (with 18.5% of the total explained variance) were the two nearest contenders.

The picture was slightly less clear-cut with Grasmick self-control. Here the main explanatory variables were HEXACO honesty–Humility and HEXACO or lexical conscientiousness for three of the four regression equations. However, in the community sample (Study 2), orderliness (cf. conscientiousness) did not relate strongly to self-control, and instead Insensitivity (cf. reversed agreeableness) was – together with HEXACO honesty–humility – the main variable explaining self-control. Thus, although in the student sample (Study 1) agreeableness was unrelated to self-control, it was – together with honesty–humility – one of the strongest correlates of self-control in the community sample.

To specify the above relations, we also investigated the relations of personality with self-control on the facet level. The 25 HEXACO facets together with age and gender explained respectively 62% and 63% of the variance in Grasmick and Tangney self-control in the student sample, and respectively 42% and 46% of the variance in the community sample. In all cases the HEXACO conscientiousness facet prudence was one of the most important predictors of self-control (all r’s > .40). Whereas all honesty–humility facets were related to Grasmick self-control with r’s > .25, most of the honesty–humility facets were less strongly related or unrelated to Tangney self-control.

Is self-control a sufficient predictor of delinquency, or do other personality variables explain incremental variance? This question is addressed in Table 3. To interpret the results, note that in both studies the personality variables were obtained months (up to 1.5 years) prior to the self-control and delinquency variables. The findings offer strong support for the importance of HEXACO honesty–humility in the prediction of delinquency. In both samples, and for all sets of variables, honesty–humility added 20% or more in the total explained variance of delinquency.

However, there were some differences between the samples. In the student sample, apart from honesty–humility, the main uniquely explaining variables were gender and Grasmick self-control. In the community sample, apart from gender, honesty–humility, and 5DPT Insensitivity, the main uniquely explaining variables were age and Tangney self-control. Note that even when the other personality variables were not added in the equation (lower left panel in the bottom of Table 3), the effect of Grasmick self-control was marginalized due to the presence of gender, age, and Tangney self-control.

Finally, we checked the unique relations of the HEXACO facets with delinquency, while controlling for self-control, gender, and age. In both the student and the community sample, the honesty–humility facet Fairness was the most important unique predictor of delinquency (respective β’s = −.26 and −.34; r’s = −.45 and −.44; all p’s < .01). Although the conscientiousness facet prudence was related to delinquency, the relations were less strong (respective β’s = −.00 and −.03; both p’s > .10 and respective r’s = −.28 and −.14; both p’s < .01). No other HEXACO facet showed a consistently strong and unique relation with delinquency. Together, all HEXACO facets, self-control, gender, and age explained, respectively 53% and 34% of the variance in delinquency. Both the honesty–humility facets and the two self-control scales showed incremental validity when added together with all other scales. That is, of the total variance noted above, the honesty–humility facets explained respectively 6% and 8% of the variance and the two self-control scales explained respectively 5% and 2% of the variance when entered after all other variables (all ΔF’s < .01).

4. Discussion and conclusions

Many scholars have argued – and found – that self-control measures are not that much different from mainstream personality measures (O’Gorman and Baxter, 2002, Olson, 2005, Tangney et al., 2004). Although most scholars have associated self-control mainly with conscientiousness, other Big Five personality traits have also been implicated. This study shows that the answer to the question of the relations of self-control with personality traits depends in part on (a) the self-control measure and (b) the personality measure (Big Five versus HEXACO) used. That is, whereas some scholars have maintained that self-control is mainly conscientiousness (O’Gorman and Baxter, 2002), in two different samples this research shows that this is by and large true for the Tangney operationalization of self-control, but not for the Grasmick operationalization. In the Grasmick operationalization, which is based on Gottfredson and Hirschi’s (1990) general theory of crime, self-control is mainly associated with honesty–humility and conscientiousness and – somewhat less strong and consistently – with agreeableness.

The finding that Grasmick self-control is associated with both honesty–humility and conscientiousness may explain at least partly why this self-control scale has been found to be such an important predictor of crime in different studies (Pratt & Cullen, 2000). On the other hand, our findings show that both self-control scales are able to explain additional variance in delinquency when all other personality variables are present, even when personality facets are used. However, the most consistent predictor of delinquency was fairness, a honesty–humility facet, which may indicate that personality models lacking (facets of) honesty–humility are likely to offer an incomplete prediction of delinquency.

A limitation of our study is the possible bias that occurred as a result of the multistage design in both studies. Specifically, as noted in the method section, the subsamples in both the student and the community sample had somewhat higher scores on honesty–humility and conscientiousness. However, this range restriction may actually mean that the results are a somewhat conservative estimate of the actual relations between the variables.

To conclude, researchers should pay more heed to the similarities and differences between different conceptualizations of measures of self-control. Although conscientiousness (prudence) seems to be a key aspect of the two main conceptualizations investigated in this study, the two instruments differ mainly in their relation to honesty–humility. Furthermore, in the prediction of criminal or delinquent tendencies, researchers are advised to incorporate honesty–humility – or its fairness facet – in their research designs.
