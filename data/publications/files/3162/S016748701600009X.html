Abstract

In his recent bestselling book, The (Honest) Truth about Dishonesty (2012), Dan Ariely reports the results of an experiment which revealed that given the opportunity to cheat with seemingly no risk of getting caught and punished, many people would cheat, albeit by just a little bit. Furthermore, the modest level of cheating appeared to be insensitive to the gain from cheating, which leads Ariely to conclude that contrary to Becker’s (1968) simple model of rational crime, real life cheaters would not cheat more in response to an increase in the gain from cheating. The present paper shows, first, that Ariely’s claim with regard to Becker’s model is incorrect, as this model cannot predict at all how the level of rational crime responds to an increase in the gain from crime. Second, the paper offers an extended version of Becker’s model which allows for such prediction, showing that an increase in the gain from crime can rationally decrease the number of crimes committed. Third, the paper suggests a simple model of rational cheating adjusted to Ariely’s experiment setup which rationalizes his results. Finally, the paper reports the results of an experiment which offered participants an opportunity to cheat in a perfectly safe environment, revealing, contrary to Ariely’s findings, that when really feeling safe, many people would cheat by a large extent.

1. Introduction

Economists’ interest in dishonesty began with the publication of Becker’s (1968) classic paper on rational crime. It was followed by a large number of theoretical contributions which extended the original model in a number of directions as well as applied it to specific criminal offenses such as tax evasion (e.g., Allingham and Sandmo, 1972, Reinganum and Wilde, 1985, Yaniv, 1999), welfare fraud (e.g., Burgees, 1992, Wolf and Greenberg, 1986, Yaniv, 1997) and bribery (e.g., Polinsky and Shavell, 2001, Rose-Ackerman, 1975, Shleifer and Vishny, 1993). Over the past decade, with the growing appeal of experimental techniques, behavioral economists have been designing numerous lab and field experiments with the purpose of deriving insights on real people’s honesty behavior, incentivizing subjects with monetary payoffs. While there is a wide variety of honesty experiments reported in the literature, the most prominent genre involves a simple task performed by participants in privacy, such as flipping a coin (e.g., Bucciol and Piovesan, 2011, Houser et al., 2012), rolling a dice (e.g., Arbel et al., 2014, Fischbacher and Foellmi-Heusi, 2013) or finding pairs of numbers that add up to 10 in as many matrices as possible (e.g., Grolleau et al., 2014, Mazar et al., 2008), the outcome of which they are requested to honestly self-report. Other genres of honesty experiments include sender-receiver games where senders may convey deceptive messages to receivers under conditions of asymmetric information (e.g., Gneezy, 2005, Sutter, 2009), dropping wallets or cash envelopes in public places to examine the return rates (West, 2005) and handing cash-paying customers, such as restaurant diners, excessive change to elucidate their tendency to return the undeserved amount (Azar, Yosef, & Bar-Eli, 2013).

Insights derived from honesty experiments appeal not just to researchers, but to the general public as well. It is not surprising that some leading behavioral economists have found it worth their while to report their findings not just in dry academic journals but also in popular-written books that became a commercial success. In a recent book that hit the jackpot, The (Honest) Truth about Dishonesty, Dan Ariely (2012) reports the results of an experiment conducted with his colleagues (Mazar et al., 2008) which he claims to reveal that given the opportunity to cheat with no risk of getting caught and punished, many people would cheat, albeit by just a little bit. Furthermore, the modest level of cheating appeared to be insensitive to the gain from cheating, which leads Ariely to conclude that real life cheaters do not behave in the way predicted by Gary Becker’s (1968) simple model of rational crime (SMORC, as denoted by Ariely) because

“The SMORC tells us that people should cheat more when they stand a chance of getting more money without being caught or punished” (p. 30)

To test whether real-life cheating behavior conforms to the SMORC alleged premise, Ariely and colleagues set up an experiment where each participant received a sheet of paper containing a series of 20 different matrices. Participants were told that their task was to find in each of these matrices two numbers that added up to 10. They were given 5 min to solve as many of the 20 matrices as possible. For each matrix solved correctly, a monetary reward was promised, the magnitude of which varied among participants (from 25 cents up to 10 dollars). After the 5 min were up, participants were told to count the number of their correct answers, put their worksheet through a shredder located at the back of the room, come to the front of the room and tell the experimenter how many matrices they solved correctly.

Compared to a control treatment where cheating was not possible (since rather than shredding their worksheet participants had to show their solution to the experimenter), it turned out that participants in the shredding treatment added, on average, just two matrices to their scores (reporting 6 solved matrices as compared to 4), regardless of the amount of money they could make per reported solved matrix. The revealed insensitivity of cheating to the amount of reward offered leads Ariely to conclude that

“Dishonesty is most likely not an outcome of a cost-benefit analysis. If it were, the increase in the benefit (the amount of money offered) would lead to more cheating” (p. 31)

In a related experiment, rather than taking the matrix test themselves, another group of participants were asked to guess how many matrices those in the shredding treatment would claim to solve correctly at each level of payment. Their predictions were that the claims of correctly solved matrices would increase as the amount of money went up. Ariely explains this prediction by arguing that

“Essentially, their intuitive theory was the same as the premise of the SMORC” (p. 31)

The purpose of the present paper is four fold. First, show, contrary to Ariely’s claim, that Becker’s simple model of rational crime (i.e., the SMORC) does not tell us that crime increases with the gain from crime. In fact, the SMORC is totally irrelevant to Ariely’s experiment as it addresses the incentives to commit just a single crime rather than determines the number of crimes to be committed. As such, it has nothing to say on how rational criminals will respond to an increase in the gain from crime. Second, examining an extended version of the SMORC (the ESMORC) which allows for the choice of more than one crime, the present paper shows that an increase in the gain from crime can rationally decrease the number of crimes committed. Applied, however, to Ariely’s case where there is no risk of getting caught and punished, the ESMORC predicts that offenders will commit as many crimes (or cheating acts) as they possibly can, regardless of the gain from crime. This means that participants in Ariely’s experiment, if they were rational, should have reported 20 solved matrices irrespective of whether the gain from a solved matrix was 1 cent or 1 million dollars. Thus, rational cost-benefit analysis does not imply, as Ariely claims, that raising the amount of money paid for a solved matrix would increase cheating, but rather that it would leave the level of cheating intact. While the latter implication of the ESMORC conforms with the insensitivity to increased rewards exhibited by Ariely’s participants, there remains the question of why they opted to report much less than the rationally-optimal amount of 20 solved matrices. A third purpose of the present paper is to suggest a shame-adjusted simple model of rational cheating (the SHASMORC) which rationalizes this behavior as well. Finally, the paper reports the results of an experiment which offered participants an opportunity to cheat on the number of solved matrices in a perfectly safe environment, revealing, contrary to Ariely’s findings, that when really feeling safe, many people would cheat by a large extent.

2. The simple model of rational crime (SMORC)

In his classic paper on the economics of crime, Becker (1968) defines the expected utility (EU) to a rational criminal from committing a certain crime in a given period of time as(1)EU=(1-p)U(g)+pU(g-f),where g denotes the gain, in monetary terms, expected from committing the crime, p denotes the probability of getting caught (after committing the crime) and f denotes the monetary equivalent of the punishment imposed if being caught. Becker argues that the crime will be committed if the expected utility to the potential criminal exceeds the utility he could get by using his time and other resources at other activities. Formally, denoting the latter utility by U‾, a potential criminal will rationally commit the crime if EU>U‾, which is the “entry condition” into crime. Accordingly, he will refrain from committing the crime if the entry condition does not hold, i.e., if EU⩽U‾. This is the SMORC.

What will happen, according to the SMORC, if the potential criminal expects a higher gain from committing the crime? Clearly, the expected utility from committing the crime will increase as well. This will make the crime more attractive as compared to other activities, increasing the likelihood that the entry condition holds and, consequently, that the crime will be committed. In contrast, an increase in the probability of getting caught or in the severity of punishment will decrease the expected utility, reducing the likelihood that the entry condition holds and that the crime will be committed.

And what, according to the SMORC, will happen to the number of crimes committed if the gain from crime increases? Unfortunately, the SMORC cannot provide a formal answer to this question, because the number of crimes does not appear anywhere in its building blocks. All it is able to tell us is that the incentive to commit a single crime will increase, yet not necessarily enough to ensure that the entry condition will hold and that the crime will actually be committed. Similarly, the SMORC cannot tell us formally what will happen to the number of crimes committed if either the probability of getting caught or the severity of punishment will increase. Nevertheless, Becker takes the liberty of speculating intuitively that, by reducing the utility expected from a crime, an increase in either one of these policy instruments would tend to reduce the number of crimes committed. He refrains, however, from offering any intuition with regard to the effect that an increase in the gain from crime may have on the number of crimes. In particular, he avoids speculating that due to its favorable effect on the utility expected from a crime, an increase in the gain from crime would tend to increase the number of crimes. Apparently this is so because an increase in the gain from crime would make the criminal better off even if he leaves his criminal activity intact. He may prefer this option to increasing his criminal activity and subject himself to higher risk. He may also take this opportunity to reduce a bit his criminal activity and still ends up with a total gain from crime greater than before as well as with lower risk. To see what exactly is going on in this case we need to extend the SMORC in a way that incorporates the number of crimes as a decision variable in the criminal’s expected utility. This brings us to the ESMORC.

3. The extended simple model of rational crime (ESMORC)

Assume now that the potential criminal faces the opportunity of committing several crimes of a certain type in a given period of time. Suppose, for simplicity, that the considered type of crime does not require any input of time or other costly resources. The potential criminal, if being rational, will choose the number of crimes to be committed, n, so as to maximize his expected utility(2)EU=(1-p)U(gn)+pU(gn-F),where g denotes the gain from a single crime, p denotes the probability of getting caught and punished (assuming that if he gets caught committing any single crime all other crimes committed are revealed) and F denotes the monetary equivalent of the overall punishment imposed on him in case of getting caught.

Consider first Ariely’s cheating experiment, where there is no risk of getting caught and punished. In this case p = 0, thus the expected utility from crime reduces to a certain utility level, U(gn). Clearly, the utility level from crime will be maximized when n is raised to the maximum amount possible, irrespective of g. That is, it is rationally-optimal for the criminal to commit as many crimes as he possibly can, regardless of whether the gain per single crime is one cent or one million dollars. Contrary to Ariely’s claim, an increase in the gain per single crime (or cheating act) would not lead to more crime (or cheating) but rather leave its level intact. Hence, the revealed insensitivity of Ariely’s participants to an increase in the gain expected from a cheating act conforms to the rational cost-benefit model.

Consider now the more general case where the risk of getting caught and punished is positive. Suppose, for simplicity, that the punishment takes the form of a monetary fine, which may be of two basic forms: when the gain from crime is monetary and can be assessed by the law enforcement agency (e.g., bribery, tax evasion), the fine is usually proportional to the overall monetary gain; however, when the gain from crime is non-monetary (e.g., illegal parking, animal abuse), the fine is usually proportional to the number of crimes committed. In the former case, F = λgn, where λ is a positive coefficient; in the latter case, F = fn, where f is the fine per crime.

Consider the latter case first. Substituting F = fn in (2) and differentiating with respect to n, the first-order condition for expected-utility maximization is(3)d(EU)dn=(1-p)gU′(gn)+p(g-f)U′(gn-F)=0.Evidently, a prerequisite for an interior optimum is f > g, otherwise the solution is again obtained at the maximum amount of crimes possible. The second-order condition for interior maximization is(4)d2(EU)dn2≡Δ=(1-p)g2U″(gn)+p(g-f)2U″(gn-F)<0,implying that an interior optimum is possible only for a risk-averse criminal, for whom U″ < 0. To determine the effect on the optimal number of crimes, n∗, of an increase in g, one needs to totally differentiate (3) with respect to n and g, obtaining(5)dn∗dg=-1Δ[(1-p)U′(gn)+pU′(gn-F)+(1-p)gnU″(gn)+p(g-f)nU″(g-F)].The sign of (5) is indeterminate at this stage. However, substituting (3) in (5) and rearranging yields(5′)dn∗dg=-1Δ{(1-p)U′(gn)+pU′(gn-F)+(1-p)gnU′(gn)[RA(gn-F)-RA(gn)]},where RA denotes the Arrow–Pratt measure of absolute risk aversion. Under the accepted assumption of decreasing absolute risk aversion RA(gn-F)>RA(gn), thus the sign of (5) is unambiguously positive. Hence, an increase in the gain per crime would increase the number of crimes committed.

Consider, however, the alternative form of fine. Substituting F = λgn in (2) and maximizing with respect to n, the first-order condition is(6)d(EU)dn=(1-p)U′(gn)+p(1-λ)U′(gn-F)=0,which is satisfied at an interior optimum only if λ > 1. It is clear now that the effect of an increase in the gain per crime on the number of crimes committed by a rational risk-averse criminal depends on the exact form of the fine he expects to bear in case he gets caught: if the fine relates to the amount of crime committed, crime would rise, but if the fine relates to the amount of illegal gains collected, crime would fall. How can we explain these contradictory results obtained under the alternative forms of fine? Two kinds of incentives seem to arise at the margin following an increase in the gain per crime: a marginal incentive to commit a crime and a marginal incentive to take a risk. Evidently, an increase in the gain per crime increases the marginal incentive to commit a crime. This is the intuitive reasoning applied by Ariely when he expects participants in his experiment to cheat more when they stand a chance of getting more money without being caught or punished. Indeed, when F = fn, an increase in g, by increasing the marginal incentive to commit a crime, acts to increase the number of crimes committed [an effect captured by the first two terms of (5′)]. However, when F = λgn, an increase in g increases not just the gain per crime, but also, and by the same proportion, the magnitude of the fine imposed in case of getting caught, thereby creating a same-strength marginal disincentive to commit a crime which cancels out the former incentive. Secondly, when F = fn, an increase in g makes the criminal more wealthy in both possible ‘states of the world’, being caught and not being caught, increasing, under decreasing absolute risk-aversion, the marginal incentive to take a risk and acting to increase the number of crimes committed [an effect captured by the last term of (5′)]. In contrast, when F = λgn, an increase in g makes the criminal more wealthy when he is not caught, but less wealthy when he is caught (as the fine overweighs the gain). Decreasing absolute risk aversion cannot help explain his response in this case. However, perceiving risk aversion as a stimulant for cushioning (or for smoothing over both states of the world) any exogenous shock affecting either state of the world, an increase in g would generate a marginal incentive to take less risk, as this would increase wealth in the state of being caught and decrease wealth in the state of not being caught. Consequently, an increase in g would counter-intuitively reduce the number of crimes committed by a rational criminal.

4. The shame-adjusted simple model of rational crime (SHASMORC)

While the insensitivity of cheating to increased gain exhibited by Ariely’s participants conforms to the ESMORC prediction, their chosen level of cheating does not: rather than report the rationally-optimal amount of 20 correct matrices, they chose to report much less, adding, on average, two matrices to their actual score. Ariely concludes that cheating was so low and insensitive to the expected gain because cheaters are not affected by cost-benefit considerations but rather by their moral feelings which restrict their cheating to the level that enables them to preserve their personal image as honest and decent people. In view of this conclusion, one should expect a failing student who takes a 20-question quiz exam in Economics 101 and glances over for help to his neighbor’s worksheet when the proctor is playing with her smart-phone, to dishonestly retrieve answers to just two questions rather than to the rationally-optimal amount of 20 questions. Similarly, one should expect a cheating husband whose wife went away to visit her parents for 20 days to cheat on her with their sexy neighbor no more than two nights during this period and a Game of Thrones fan to illegally download just two episodes of the fifth 13-episode season. Or, there may be an alternative explanation to the modest level of cheating exhibited by Ariely’s participants.

While criminals and cheaters usually operate in the shadows, where their actions are unseen, Ariely’s cheaters must face the experimenter, lower their gaze (or worse, look her in the eye) and tell a lie. They are unsure of the purpose of the experiment and a bit anxious that even though they have shredded their worksheet, the experimenter can in some tricky way figure out that they are lying. The experimenter might even remark sarcastically that in the many times that she has run this experiment, no one has ever claimed to solve so many matrices. Although getting paid in full irrespective of what the experimenter might think or say, those who claim to have solved a significant number of matrices above the actual number solved are about to encounter a potentially embarrassing situation. Furthermore, the experimenter might be a teaching assistant or a regular faculty member they are likely to bump into in the future. Each time this happens, a “here goes a cheater” thought might cross her mind. Ending up with a respectable persona on campus knowing that you have lied is shameful and must be taken into account by a rational cheater.

Consider now a shame-adjusted simple model of rational cheating (SHASMORC). Suppose that the participant believes that the more matrices he reports above the number of matrices he has actually solved, m‾, the more likely is the experimenter to suspect that he is cheating. Denoting by m the number of matrices reported, the participant’s assessed probability that his report will raise the experimenter’s suspicion, ρ, is thus given by ρ=ρ(m-m‾), where ρ′(m-m‾)>0, ρ″(m-m‾)⩾0 and ρ(0)=0. If the experimenter’s suspicion is raised, there exists some probability, δ, that she will let the participant know about this by giving him a skeptical look or by saying something embarrassing. Being suspected of cheating will fill the participant with shame, S, which is a form of disutility that should be incorporated into his target function. The more money he collects from the experimenter upon reporting his achievement, the greater will be the shame he experiences. Denoting by g the monetary gain per reported solved matrix and by s the disutility from shame arises per dollar of money collected from the experimenter, the rational participant is assumed to choose the number of matrices to report so as to maximize the sum, W, of the utility from cheating and the expected disutility from shame(9)W(U,S)=U(gm)-ρ(m-m‾)δS,where S = sgm. The first- and second order conditions for an interior maximum are(10)dWdm=g{U′(gm)-δs[ρ(m-m‾)+mρ′(m-m‾)]}=0(11)d2Wdm2≡Δ=g{gU″(gm)-δs[2ρ′(m-m‾)+mρ″(m-m‾)]}<0,the latter of which is clearly satisfied for a risk-averse (U″ < 0) and a risk-neutral (U″ = 0) participant, but may also hold for a risk-seeker (U″ > 0) participant.

Evaluating the optimum condition (10) at the extreme points of m=m‾ and m = 20 reveals that if the parameters of the model satisfy the condition(12)U′(g20)ρ(20-m‾)+20ρ′(20-m‾)<δs<U′(gm‾)m‾ρ′(0),it would be rationally optimal for the participant to cheat, i.e., to fraudulently report solving more matrices than he actually did, yet less than the maximum amount possible of 20 matrices.

Suppose, for example, that the participant is risk-neutral [U(gm) = gm] and that the probability of raising the experimenter’s suspicion is linear [ρ(m-m‾)=m-m‾]. Suppose further, in view of Ariely’s finding, that the number of matrices a participant actually solves is m‾=4. Substituting in (12) reveals that if 0.027<δs<0.250, cheating will be desirable, and at a level lower than 20 matrices (i.e., m‾<m∗<20). Substituting in the optimum condition (10) and solving for m yields m∗=0.5/δs+2. Whether cheating will be “high” or “low” thus depends on the value of δs. If δs=0.125, we get Ariely’s result of m∗ = 6, which implies that optimal cheating is quite modest, at the level of m-m‾=2.

Totally differentiating now (10) with respect to m and g yields(13)dm∗dg=-gmΔU″(gm),the sign of which depends on the participant’s attitude towards risk, being positive, zero or negative, depending on whether the participant is risk seeker, risk neutral or risk averse, respectively. It thus follows that if Ariely’s participants are, on average, risk neutral, their revealed insensitivity to an increase in the gain from cheating conforms to the SHASMORC. Contrary to Ariely’s assertion, the exhibited rigidness of dishonesty to increased gain may be the outcome of cost-benefit considerations.

5. The experiment

To dismiss the idea that his participants cheated so little because they feared that cheating more would raise the experimenter’s suspicion, Ariely ran another experiment where half of the participants were told that the average student in this experiment solves about four matrices (which was true) while the other half were told that the average student solves about eight matrices. It turned out that this knowledge had no influence on participants’ behavior: they cheated by about two extra answers regardless of whether they thought that others solved on average four or eight matrices. Ariely argues that this proves that the level of cheating was not based on the desire to avoid standing out, because if it had been, his participants would have cheated in both conditions by a few matrices beyond what they believed was the average performance, meaning that they would have claimed to solve about six matrices when they thought the average was four and about ten matrices when they thought the average was eight. The problem with this argument is that it rests on the assumption that participants believed the information they were told. But why should they? In particular, a participant who succeeds in solving just four matrices after being told that the average is eight might reasonably suspect that he has been fed with false information with the purpose of seeing if he bites on the bait and adjusts his cheating accordingly. “If I report ten, or even eight matrices”, he reckons, “they will know for sure that I cheat”. So he reports six.

Rather than report their achievement to the experimenter and being paid accordingly, participants in another experiment run by Ariely were even allowed to pay themselves from a sizable bowl of money containing small bills and coins. Furthermore, in one version of this experiment, the experimenter in charge was noticeably blind. Still, it turned out that while many participants cheated, they did so by just a little bit, reporting on average 6 matrices, as compared to 4 matrices solved successfully by participants in the control treatment where cheating was not possible. But did Ariely’s participants actually feel safe to cheat as much as they otherwise would? After all, they were introduced to the experiment just minutes earlier. They were not given the opportunity to thoroughly examine the environment for hidden cameras nor the time to figure out whether or not there was some tricky way by which their cheating could be revealed. We hypothesize that the modest level of cheating they exhibited was due to caution they exercised in an unfamiliar setup and that had they really felt safe they would have cheated by much more. To test this hypothesis, we ran an experiment of our own.

5.1. Method

We first recruited 60 students from COMAS (Israel) to serve as a control treatment. We handed each participant a sheet of paper containing a series of 20 matrices taken from the Appendix to Amir and Lobel (2010) and asked them to try solve as many matrices as they could (i.e., find in each matrix two numbers that add up to 10) in 5 min. After the time was up, we counted the number of their correct answers and paid them NIS 5 (equivalent to about 1.2 Euro) for each correct answer as well as NIS 20 for agreeing to participate in the experiment.

We then recruited 115 students to participate in the experimental treatment. We handed each participant the 20 matrices, a sheet of paper containing instructions (see Appendix A) as well as NIS 120. Participants were instructed to take the matrices home and try to solve as many of them as they could in 5 min taken at their convenient time within the next 10 days. After they finish, they were to count the number of matrices they succeeded to solve, pay themselves NIS 5 per each solved matrix as well as NIS 20 for participating in the experiment, calculate the change they owed us from NIS 120, put it in an unidentified envelope of their choice and insert the envelope into our mailbox on campus within 10 days. There were no transaction costs in doing so, since the campus is small in area, our mailbox is located at its center, and the students’ schedule requires then to arrive to campus at least three times a week. To make things even simpler, the instructions pointed out that in case they succeeded in solving all 20 matrices, they owed us no change and did not have to insert any envelope into our mailbox.

5.2. Results

Similar to Ariely’s results, participants in our control treatment solved on average 4.2 matrices out of 20. However, as shown in Table 1, participants in our experimental treatment behaved strikingly differently than Ariely’s, claiming, by means of their change returns, to have solved 16(!) matrices on average. That is, rather than adding 2 matrices to their actual scores, they took the liberty of adding 12. Of particular interest is the fact that 46.9% of them opted not to return an envelope at all, implicitly claiming to owe us no change. That is, almost half the participants in our experimental treatment felt comfortable enough to deliver the message that they have successfully solved all 20 matrices. One may suggest that after collecting the NIS 120, half of our participants were struck by amnesia, conveniently forgetting all about the money they owed us. Needless to say, we find this possibility highly unlikely. First, three days before the 10-day deadline, we posted a reminder that the change-return period for our experiment was about to terminate. Secondly, no one forbade participants who might have forgotten or happened to be too busy to return the envelope on time, to do so afterwards. If they were honest, they could have returned the envelope later when their recollection finally recovered or when they succeeded in finding a few minutes to do so, accompanied with a note of apology. After all, money due is always welcome. Regrettably, by the time these lines are written, about three months after the termination of the 10-day deadline, no late-returned envelopes have been dropped into our mailbox.

As seen in Table 1, about 18% of our participants seem to be inherently honest, claiming to have solved up to 9 matrices (5 matrices, on average). However, while there is zero representation in the 10–12 matrix domain, the number of participants who claimed to have done even better rises steadily thereafter, with almost 35(!) percent unabashedly claiming to have solved 13–18 matrices (not surprisingly, no one claimed to have solved 19 matrices.). Together with the 47% who did not return an envelope, thereby claiming to have solved all 20 matrices, we ended up with 82% of our participants cheating us big time! Compared to the control treatment, a participant in the experimental treatment ripped us off by about NIS 60 (≈Euro 14.5), on average, which amounts to about NIS 6900 (≈Euro 1668) for the whole group. It is not only that so many people opted to cheat when giving the opportunity to safely do so, but, in sharp contrast to Ariely’s findings, they cheated a lot, approximating the ESMORC implication that when there is no risk of getting caught and punished, it is rationally-optimal to cheat as much as one possibly can.

6. Concluding remarks

This paper has challenged Dan Ariely’s claim, reported in his recent bestselling book The (Honest) Truth About Dishonesty, that people tend to cheat by just a little bit even in the absence of any risk to get caught and punished. Hypothesizing that the modest level of cheating exhibited in his experiment was due to caution participants exercised in an unfamiliar setup, the paper develops a theoretic utility-maximizing model which rationalizes this behavior as well as reports the results of an experiment which offered participants an opportunity to cheat in the safety of their own homes, revealing that when really feeling safe people cheat by a large extent. Furthermore, surprised by his participants failure to cheat more when offered a higher payoff, Ariely concludes that real-life cheaters do not behave in accordance with Becker’s simple model of rational crime which, in his claim, predicts that cheaters should respond to a higher payoff by increasing their level of cheating. The present paper refutes this claim arguing that Becker’s simple model addresses a single crime (or cheating act) only and as such cannot predict any relationship between the number of crimes committed and the payoff per crime. Secondly, extending Becker’s model to allow for the choice of more than one crime, the paper shows that in the presence of risk to get caught and punished, increasing the payoff per crime may lead to a fall in crime, whereas in the absence of such risk, which is seemingly the case in Ariely’s experiment, it is rationally-optimal to commit as many crimes as possible irrespective of the payoff per crime. Indeed, when allowed to cheat in the safety of their own homes, 82% of our participants turned from prudent to large-scale cheaters, approximating the rationally-optimal result.

Ariely’s (and our own) experiment is closely related to honesty experiments which request participants to self-report the outcome of flipping a coin or rolling a dice. While Ariely determines the magnitude of cheating by comparing the average score reported by the shredding treatment to the average score of a control treatment for which cheating was not possible, the other experimenters use the known statistical distribution of the possible outcomes to determine the magnitude of cheating in favor of the profitable outcomes. In the coin-flipping experiment of Bucciol and Piovesan (2011), the profitable outcome was reported by 85.39% of the participants (which is significantly higher than the 50% probability). In the dice-rolling experiment of Fischbacher and Foellmi-Heusi (2013), which rewarded each side of the dice by a corresponding payoff (with the exception of ’6’ which yielded zero payoff), 35% of the participants claimed to have rolled the highest possible score of ‘5’ (which is twice as much as the 6.7% probability), whereas only 6.4% claimed to have rolled ‘6’. Because in these experiments individual payoffs are determined by chance, a certain proportion of participants are likely to truly obtain the most profitable outcome. This could have boosted cheating in these experiments as it helps participants to tell a lie unembarrasedly as compared to Ariely’s experiment which required the exertion of mental effort.

To guarantee our participants a full sense of safety to cheat as much as they wish, we paid them the maximum amount of money they could earn in advance, asking them to anonymously return the change they owed us for the matrices they did not solve. In a recent working paper, Grolleau et al. (2014) hypothesize that, being loss averse, people are more likely to lie to preserve a certain benefit (or avoid a loss) than to acquire it, thus receiving money in advance for performing a certain task might generate a stronger incentive to lie than receiving it after performing the task. Conducting a matrix-solving lab experiment a-la Ariely with one treatment being paid before and another after performing the task, Grolleau et al. conclude that “the level of cheating is almost doubled under advance payments compared to ex-post payments” (p. 3). While other recent studies have questioned the existence of loss aversion, especially in the case of small payoffs (e.g., Harinck, Van Dijk, Van Beest, & Mersmann, 2007), we cannot rule out the possibility that loss aversion contributed to the high level of cheating in our experiment. Furthermore, some participants were keeping the money for over a week, which could have increased their attachment to the money, as revealed in a study on timing and loss aversion where subjects were paid two weeks prior to participating in the experiment (Shavit & Rosenboim, 2012). We are unable to assess the effect of time-induced loss aversion on cheating, but using Grolleau et al.’s result to assess the effect of advance payment in our experiment still leaves much cheating attributed to the safety of a home environment. In particular, only 15% of Grolleau et al.’s participants reported the maximum performance of 20 solved matrices in their non-monitored advance payment treatment, as compared to 47% in our experiment.

The level of cheating exhibited in our experiment is considerably high, irrespective of whether loss aversion played a minor or a major role in inducing it. In any case, loss aversion may explain cheating but not justify or launder it. One’s lying cannot be excused by having fallen victim to his or her inherent loss aversion. So even if we take the liberty to assume that our participants felt as much comfortable to cheat in the classroom as in their own homes and that loss aversion is solely responsible for their unabashed extent of cheating, Ariely’s assertion that people cheat by just a little bit cannot hold as is. At best we could say that when people can cheat to receive money they do not deserve, they cheat by just a little bit, but when people can cheat to avoid paying money they owe, they cheat by a large extent.
