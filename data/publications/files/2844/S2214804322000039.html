Abstract

As fundamental conditions and subject attributes in lab and field are very different, insights from existing meta-analyses performed on combined data from lab and field might imprecisely summarize the behavior in lab. Therefore, we focus on lab experiments to examine the influence of ‘earned versus unearned’ and ‘student versus charity recipient’ experimental protocols on donations using meta-analysis based on 80 dictator game studies spread over the time frame of 23 years (1997 – 2020). We also take advantage of more recent meta-analysis techniques to improve the robustness and offer methodological advancements for the examination of human behavior. We find that dictators on average share approximately 22% of their endowment. We also find robust evidence that earned endowment reduces benevolence when the recipient is a charity, while the use of charity instead of student as the recipient enhances benevolence with unearned endowment. These findings extend our understanding regarding the conditional effects of nature of endowment and recipient type on the behavior in lab.

1. Introduction

The origins of the dictator game trace back to the work of Kahneman et al. (1986) and Forsythe et al. (1994). In a typical dictator game proposed by Forsythe et al. (1994), one player (known as the dictator) decides the division of a monetary endowment with another player (known as the recipient). The dictator game in essence is not a typical game; it is in fact a single person decision problem that is termed as the ‘dictator game’ in the work of Forsythe et al. (1994). The homo economicus prediction of zero donation is frequently violated in the dictator game and often humans are significantly benevolent than predicted by the payoff maximization hypothesis1. Researchers have frequently used lab and field experiments to explore this benevolent tendency of humans influenced by the demographic and situational dimensions (Engel, 2011). A search at RePEc with the ‘Dictator Game’ returned 1129 papers indicating the popularity of this game among researchers.

Each of such papers discusses the effect of experimental designs or characteristics of the dictator on the fraction of endowment given to the recipient. However, each paper differs substantially in its particular focus of picking up the preference shifter, making it difficult to generalize the pattern. Given the accumulation of experimental studies using the dictator game, meta-analysis can potentially provide the answer. We have found seven meta-analyses based on the dictator game studies published so far: Camerer (2003) used 11 papers, Cardenas and Carpenter (2008) employed 10 studies, Engel (2011) used 129 studies, Rand et al. (2016) employed 22 studies, Larney et al. (2019) employed 14 papers, Fromell et al. (2020) used 22 studies while Cochard et al. (2021) used 96 articles2. The meta-analysis by Engel (2011) is a pioneer work that examined the effect of different experimental conditions on the proportion of endowment shared in the dictator game. The latest meta-analysis by Larney et al. (2019) examines only the effect of stake size while that by Cochard et al. (2021) focusses on the examination of fairness hypothesis. Also, studies used by Cochard et al. (2021) stretch until 2013 and does not cover relatively newer studies.

Since the decade-old comprehensive meta-analysis of Engel (2011), numerous dictator game studies are added to the literature. In fact, a search with the key phrase “Dictator Game” at RePEc returned 774 studies published after 2011. Several of these newly published studies have examined the effect of experimental conditions previously unknown or rarely examined by the researchers. One such important experimental condition examines the effect of ‘earned versus unearned’ endowment on donations. If the source of money (earned versus unearned) influences the amount shared by the dictators, it violates the fundamental assumption of fungibility in economics (Arkes Hal et al., 1994). There are several factors that can cause this violation when donating with money acquired through exerting effort. Some of these factors are discussed below.

1)

The earned versus unearned endowment primarily reflects the elicitation of decisions in a ‘natural versus artificial’ environment. Many researchers believe that earning an endowment in experiments reflects a natural life scenario where people have to earn money before they donate it, and therefore offers a relatively precise measure of generosity with reasonable external validity (Carlsson et al., 2013). On the other hand, unearned endowment is close to an artificial environment and hence can possibly lead to a different level of pro-social behavior.

2)

Earning an endowment creates a stronger feeling of ownership or property rights and therefore has higher tendency to stimulate self-serving behavior (Hoffman et al., 1994; Fahr and Irlenbusch, 2000; Oxoby & Spraggon, 2008; Jakiela, 2011; Danková & Servátka, 2015). The unearned endowment can overstate generosity in lab because subjects do not exert effort to obtain the endowment.

3)

Studies on mental accounting predict that unearned endowment is going to be a part of the ‘mental current account’ that has a higher marginal propensity of consumption and hence more likely to be shared with the recipient. This argument however is less resounding because unearned income might not be more spendable as compared to other types of incomes in the ‘mental current account’ (Arkes Hal et al., 1994).

4)

The egalitarian approach considers any inequality to be unfair (Cappelen et al, 2007). According to egalitarianism, the source of income should not affect the extent of dictator's pro-sociality. However, theories of justice consider inequality to be fair if it is caused by differences in effort or ability (Konow, 2003; Barr et al., 2015; Jakiela, 2015). Therefore, according to theories of justice, the dictator is less likely to be concerned about the inequality between herself and the recipient if she exerts an effort to earn the endowment. This in turn implies lower donations with earned income compared to the unearned one.

Despite these well-defined economic and psychological differences linked to the earned and unearned endowment, the individual dictator game studies are inconclusive regarding the differential effects of these two types of endowments on donations. We do find useful insights that people donate less with earned money from the meta-analysis by Engel (2011).

Another important aspect of the dictator game experiments in lab is the selection of recipients: studies employ either students or charity organizations as recipients. However, these two types of recipients are not perfect substitutes and can attract different levels of donations because of the following reasons.

1)

The dictators may show higher generosity towards charity organizations because they might have benefited from their services in the past, or expect a future benefit (Dawson, 1998). This is less likely to happen in the case of fellow student recipients.

2)

The donations to charities can be significantly influenced by the empathetic motivations and a concern about the welfare of people that potentially benefit from the charity (Crumpler and Grossman, 2008). On the other hand, as students on average share the same level of economic wellbeing, the empathetic concerns and subsequently donations are likely to be weaker in the case of fellow student recipients.

3)

When paired with student recipients, the dictator can believe that the recipient expects money from her. Contrarily, with charity recipients, such expectations are absent and hence the donations to charity recipients can be lower compared to the fellow student recipients.

4)

The extrinsic motivations such as tax deductions, thank you gifts, reputational concerns and signaling wealth can also influence donations specifically to charities. In lab experiments, however, such concerns are either non-applicable (for example: tax cuts) or do not play a significant role due to the experimental setting (for example: image concerns and signaling wealth are eliminated in a double-blind dictator game).

5)

It is argued that donations to charities can be higher than student recipients because the dictators have the option to choose a right mission charity, while they generally do not have the option to choose a student recipient (Fehrler and Kosfeld, 2014). The existing evidence, however, does not provide enough support in favor of this argument (Tonin and Vlassopoulos, 2010; Fehrler and Kosfeld, 2014).

For the charity versus student comparison, we find evidence from the meta-analysis by Engel (2011) that dictators are more generous towards charity recipients.

An important aspect of the meta-analysis by Engel (2011) is the data set; it consists of both lab and field experiments. Usually there are striking demographic (for example: age and marital status, education), geographic (for example: rural versus urban environment) and economic (for example: income, endowment in the dictator game) differences among lab and field subjects. These differences are important to control because the existing studies show that elderly people are more generous than younger ones (Midlarsky and Hannah, 1989; Yen, 2002; Engel, 2011), education and income are positively associated with donations (Yen 2002), while the evidence for the influence of joint donations decisions for married couples is mixed (for example: Andreoni et al., 2003 report a negative influence while Yörük, 2010 report a positive influence of joint decisions on donations). Similarly, geography also plays an important role in donations because the social norms and culture are different across regions and these are often found to play a significant role in shaping generosity and other pro-social behaviors (Wolpert, 1988). Furthermore, often subjects in lab already have experience of participation in experiments while it is rarely the case in field experiments. This experience in turn can positively or negatively influence the behavior in lab.

Probably the most important difference between lab and field experiments performed with human recipients is the extent of association between the dictator and the recipient. In lab experiments with student recipients, the dictator knows that the recipient is a fellow student and generally no further information is disclosed. In field experiments, the recipient is usually from the same locality or village. In closely knit rural societies, even if limited recipient information is disclosed, the association between the dictator and recipient can be stronger because in such societies generally everyone knows everyone, therefore the money transferred by the dictator remains within the same locality and could benefit the dictator indirectly. This extra layer of association between the dictator and recipient in field experiments in closely knit rural societies renders the field environment incomparable to the lab environment.

Another important yet less explored aspect related to the recipient type and endowment nature is how these two experimental protocols interact with each other. While meta-analysis by Engel (2011) examines the unconditional effects of these two protocols, it does not explore donations made to the charity and student recipients conditional on the endowment nature. This conditional analysis is important because it can further refine our understanding whether or not the charity effect dominates conditional on the nature of endowment, and the outcome can have significant implications on the protocols of the future dictator game experiments.

As the meta-analysis by Engel (2011) does not exclusively controls for most of the aforementioned differences, it is evident that results from Engel (2011) are not directly and exclusively applicable to lab and they might differ if meta-analysis is restricted to only lab experiments. Furthermore, the possibility that aforementioned factors interact with heterogeneity in endowments and recipients further challenges the precision of combined results from lab and field. Therefore, a meta-analysis based only on lab experiments and student subjects can provide more controlled evidence for the effects of earned money and charity recipient on donations in lab.

Lately, numerous dictator game lab experiments performed in relatively unexplored countries (such as Pakistan3 and Taiwan) have been published since the decade-old analysis by Engel (2011). The evidence suggests that cultural differences, informal institutions or norms (relatively stronger in developing countries), property rights, status effects associated with ‘earned income’, and the perceived importance and utility of charities could differ across populations and are important factors in the determination of generosity (Platteau, 2000; Jakiela, 2011 & 2015). The availability of experimental data from different populations offers a unique scenario to test whether dominance of unearned endowment and charity effect extends or diminishes with the addition of newer studies performed in different cultures that are available after the meta-analysis by Engel (2011).

Considering the vast applicability of ‘earned versus unearned endowment’ and ‘charity versus student recipient’ experimental protocols in the experimental studies and availability of data from numerous well-explored as well as underexplored cultures, we explore these experimental protocols by performing a meta-analysis based on 80 dictator game lab experiment studies that cover over two decades of research (1997 – 2020). We find robust evidence that the dictators are more generous towards charity in comparison to student recipients with unearned endowment, while with earned endowment, the charity effect disappears. We also find that earned endowment reduces benevolence when the recipient is a charity. Our results suggest that the recipient type and nature of endowment interact with each other.

Our work advances the existing dictator game and experimental literature through several avenues. First, we report meta-analysis that examines important experimental protocols (earned versus unearned endowment, charity versus student recipient) based exclusively on lab experiments and therefore offer precise and more reliable insights for the selected experimental protocols. Second, our data set has dictator game lab experiments stretching over the last three decades and accounts for several studies published after the decade-old comprehensive meta-analysis by Engel (2011) (50 studies in our data set are published after the meta-analysis of Engel published in 2011). The evidence from newer studies often performed in under explored cultures offers results with higher generalizability. Third, unlike most existing dictator game meta-analyses, we use recent meta-analyses techniques that are preferred over traditional methods specifically in the presence of publication bias. As we observe publication bias in our data, the use of these newer techniques helps us to draw reliable conclusions and differentiates our work from the existing DG meta-analyses. Fourth, our findings suggest that endowment and recipient type could interact with each other; charity effect dominates student effect with unearned endowment, earned endowment influences donations negatively when the recipient is a charity; when the recipient is a student, the negative effect of earning an endowment disappears.

The third section reports the result of the meta-synthesis while the fourth section reports the outcome of the multivariate analysis. The last section concludes the paper.

2. Study Selection Procedure and Data Overview

For a meta-analysis, it is important to have either a clear inclusion or exclusion criteria for studies. While the inclusion criteria can be difficult, it is relatively easier to specify and implement the exclusion criteria. We also relied on the exclusion mechanism and restricted the analysis to studies (both published and unpublished) that report lab experiments implementing the dictator game, use a relatively unrestricted decision space4 for either real or hypothetical5 voluntary donations, use student dictators with no bargaining power assigned to the recipient6 and do not use any deception7. Any study that did not qualify the aforementioned restrictions was not considered for the analysis. These restrictions were implemented to ensure that selected studies are comparable at least in their core procedures, most important ones being the reliance on lab experiments and student dictators.

The search for studies qualifying the aforementioned restrictions started on 28th October, 2020, using the RePEc literature database (http://repec.org/)8. The “Dictator Game” phrase returned 1129 papers. As a first step, judging from the study title, abstract and other relevant information we excluded papers relying on field experiments and those using non-student dictators and narrowed down the list to more than 500 studies. As a second step, we closely examined the contents of these research works and obtained 80 studies that met the set criteria and had the necessary statistical information required to perform meta-analysis9. The search for studies ended on 15th January, 2021 after going through all 1129 studies at RePEc. The coded studies were rechecked to eliminate any errors and to obtain a consensus regarding the classification of experimental treatments.

Out of 80 studies, 65 are published in journals while 15 are unpublished works. The studies cover a period of 23 years (1997 – 2020)10 and have been published continuously over the past 16 years, but year 2018 had the most publications with nine papers, followed by 2020 with eight papers. From these 80 studies, we extracted a total of 317 estimates for the fraction of endowment given to the recipient by the dictator (mean: 3.96 per study) based on 25,703 observations. For extraction of estimates, we do not use regression coefficients or data on individual donations from each study, instead we use the average fraction of endowment shared by dictators per study. We call the estimate “Donated”, which is the dependent variable for the meta-analysis. This is somewhat different from a typical meta-analysis in which the estimate of concern is the effect of a certain variable on another. Further breakdown of studies for the dependent variable and our primary meta-independent variables (earned and unearned endowment, charity and student recipient) are reported in table 1.

Next, we provide an overview of meta-independent variables used in the meta-regression analysis. We do not examine variables that are reported by either one or just two studies in our data set because of the very limited cross-study variation11 (20 variables are reported only once and 4 are reported twice) and focus on those variables that are most likely to cause differences in results among the selected studies. Applying these protocols leaves us with two focus meta-independent variables (earned or unearned endowment; charity or student recipient) and 11 other meta-independent variables as controls. Further details about these variables are reported in Table 2.

For one of our focus meta-independent variable (Earned Endowment), we use 312 estimates and do not consider five estimates because they report hypothetical donations that do not fall in the earned or unearned endowment protocol. This focus meta-independent variable examines individual effort exerted to earn an endowment. The nature of the effort differs across studies. Appendix 1 lists all earning methods used by selected studies. In the meta-analysis, we do not consider heterogeneity in the method of earning endowment because of the limited number of studies in our data set that rely on the earned endowment protocol. Also, to perform precise analysis for the effects of heterogeneity in earning methods on donations, the intelligence of subjects has to be controlled in the in meta-regressions. However, most often such information is not available for the selected studies. Furthermore, some studies (for example: Carlsson et al., 2013) offer a fixed payment for performing the task while others (for example: Umer, 2020) incentivize subjects based on the correctly completed tasks. In the former case, there is no income inequality across subjects while in the latter case, there is a possibility of income inequality dependent on the effort. Therefore, in essence, the earning methods across the two aforementioned set of studies are not perfectly comparable because in case of fixed and lump-sum payment, there is no extra motivation to exert a higher level while in case of effort-dependent payment, the dictator has an incentive to work harder. Due to all the aforementioned reasons, we perform only a comparison between earned versus unearned endowment and do not explore how the method of earning influences donations.

For other meta-independent variables as controls, when it is not defined for a subsample, the number of estimates became smaller than 317. In addition, when the information required to compile a meta-independent variable is missing in the original study, the number of estimates became smaller than 317. The categorical variable for gender takes on three values. This is primarily because studies reporting donations separately for male and female dictators are relatively fewer. Most studies in the data set report donations cumulative of male and female dictators12. Regarding the anonymity of the dictator's decisions, some studies do not explicitly state whether they relied on the double-blind or single-blind procedure. For such studies, based on the reported information on the experimental design, we categorize them as either using double-blind or single-blind procedures. If no clear details about the anonymity of the dictator's decisions are mentioned in the experimental design, we leave those studies as uncategorized.

Furthermore, based on the country of experiment, we divided the data set into two groups depending on the income status of the country (Table 2 Middle-income Country). Following the World Bank's latest classification13, countries with GNI per capita in the range of $1,036 to $12,535 (lower and upper middle income) are grouped together as middle-income countries. Similarly, countries with GNI per capita in excess of $12,535 are classified as high-income countries. As shown in Appendix 2, our sample includes 19 countries that belong to the high-income category and four belong to the middle-income category. We also have two other variables to control for the year in which the study was published online and to control for whether the study is a journal publication or otherwise. A very high proportion of studies in our sample are journal publications.

3. Meta-Synthesis

Our dependent variable for the meta-analysis is a fraction of endowment given to the recipient by the dictator (variable “Donated” in Table 2). In this section, we synthesize it across studies. The simple average of the fraction of endowment donated calculated from all 317 estimates is 29.65% (standard deviation = 15.3) as already reported in Table 2. When compared with the previous average fraction of endowment donated (28.35%) in the meta-analysis of Engel (2011), our data shows a slightly larger average among the sample estimates. The donations are leftward skewed with majority of them falling in the 20% to 40% of the donated endowment range (Figure 1, left panel). Each estimate comprising the histogram bars in the left panel is associated with a different level of precision. Therefore, we plot in the right panel of figure 1 (a variant of a funnel plot) the endowment ratio on the horizontal axis and the standard error on the vertical axis. Each plot in the right panel corresponds to each estimate out of 317.

As we focus on two aspects of experiments, i.e., whether the endowment is earned or not and whether the recipient is a student or a charity, we decompose the distribution shown in the left panel of figure 1 along these dimensions and report it in figure 2.

A majority of donations to student recipients with unearned endowment fall in the 20% to 30% range while with earned endowment fall in the 10% to 15% range. For charity recipients, most donations with unearned endowment fall in the 40% to 60% range while for earned endowment majority of donations fall in the 10% to 20% range. When the endowment is unearned, the donations for student recipients are leftward skewed while for charity recipients are not leftward skewed with some dictators donating around 80% of their endowment. However, with earned endowment donations for student as well as charity recipients are leftward skewed. The aforementioned numbers, however, do not consider the heterogeneity across studies in terms of the number of dictator observations and standard errors. Therefore, we perform a meta-synthesis based on the recent work of Brada et al. (2021) as a guiding source.

Following the procedure proposed by Stanley and Doucouliagos (2017) and Stanley et al. (2017), we use unrestricted weighted least squares (UWA) and that with only adequately powered estimates (i.e., weighted average of the adequately powered: WAAP) to perform meta-synthesis. The mathematical modelling behind meta-synthesis is reported in Appendix 3. The synthesis results are reported in Table 3. The overall donation rate for all estimates turns out to be 22%. Therefore, our new dataset of meta-analysis is associated with a lower donation ratio compared to the one reported by Engel (2011).

For focus meta-independent variables, UWA and WAAP estimates establish a donation rate of 15% for earned and 22% for unearned endowments. These results provide robust support that generosity with earned endowment is lower in comparison to unearned endowment. We also find robust evidence that charity effect dominates the student effect; the UWA and WAAP estimates establish around 35% donation rate for charity and around 21% for student recipients. Both estimates provide robust support that dictators are more generous towards charity in comparison to student recipients. Further analysis reveals that dictators are very generous towards charity recipients with unearned money (donate 45% of their endowment) while less generous towards student recipients and donate around 21% of their endowment. However, with earned endowment the differences between donations directed towards student and charity recipients almost diminish.

We also performed meta-synthesis using traditional fixed-effect and random effects methods and report the output in appendix 4. The homogeneity test indicates that effect sizes are not homogenous across studies and therefore a random-effects specification is preferred over a fixed-effects specification. The results from the random-effects synthesis also lead to similar findings; the dictators are more generous with unearned money and towards charity recipients. However, estimates obtained from the random-effects specification establish a higher donation rate in comparison to UWA and WAAP estimates. Stanley and Doucouliagos (2017) discuss that UWA estimates are less susceptible to excess heterogeneity than fixed-effect while Stanley et al. (2017) report that WAAP synthesis has lower publication-selection bias than the traditional random effects synthesis. We do observe publication bias in our data (reported in section 5) and therefore, WAAP estimates are preferred over traditional random effects estimates. The use of WAAP differentiates the analysis reported in this study from the previous DG meta-analyses and therefore contributes to the DG meta-analyses literature by offering precise estimates for donations specifically in the presence of publication bias.

4. Meta-Regression Analysis

To explore factors causing heterogeneity among studies in the data set, we perform meta-regression analysis (MRA). More specifically, following Brada et al. (2021, page 45), we estimate the following equation:yk=β0+∑n=1Nβnmnk+δk,k=1,…,KWhere mnk represents nth meta-independent variable, βn is meta-regression coefficient, δk is an error term while N represents the total number of meta-independent variables.

There is no clear consensus among meta-analysts about the best model to estimate βn and controls for heterogeneity across studies in a meta-regression analysis, and, thus, we need to check the robustness of coefficients by comparing various models. We used five meta-regression models that lately gained importance in the meta-analysis literature and recently used by several studies that include Kočenda and Iwasaki (2021), Brada et al. (2021), Iwasaki et al. (2020) and Stanley and Doucouliagos (2012). These five models are

1)

Clustered-robust weighted least squares (WLS) that weighs by the inverse of standard error (1/SE) and offers a measure of precise estimates. The clustered-robust estimated treats a study as the cluster variable and therefore enables us to control for the dependence that might exist among several estimates reported by the study (Kočenda and Iwasaki, 2021).

2)

The number of estimates reported in studies selected for meta-analysis shows a large variation and there is a possibility that studies with the large number of estimates influence results because of their ‘over representativeness’. To deal with this issue, we use weighted least squares by using inverse of estimates per study (1/ EST) that accounts for the ‘over representativeness’ of studies with a large number of estimates.

3)

Cluster-robust fixed effects panel estimator obtained by the least squares dummy variable (LSDV). The fixed-effects specification is selected over random-effects based on Breusch-Pagan and Hausman tests. This model is used because it controls for possible endogeneity that might exist among meta-independent variables (Cameron and Miller, 2015).

4)

Clustered-robust ordinary least squares (OLS) that clusters estimate by study and relies on robust standard errors.

5)

Weighted least squares by using degrees of freedom (df) as weight to account for differences in the sample size across studies.

The regression results from these five models are reported in table 4. Following the existing work of Iwasaki et al. (2020), we consider the estimates to be significantly different from zero and offer robust evidence of non-zero effect if they turn out to be significant in at least three of the five meta-regression models.

As table 3 suggests that the student-charity contrast may differ under earned or unearned endowment protocols, we include the focused meta-independent variables with their interaction term, using the combination of the student recipient and unearned endowment as the reference category. Then the coefficient on the charity recipient dummy (b1) shows the effect of charity recipients with unearned endowment, the coefficient on the earned endowment dummy (b2) shows the effect of earned endowment with student recipients, and the coefficient on the interaction term (b3) shows the additional effect of combining charity recipients and earned endowment. Three more parameters can be derived from estimated parameters: (1) b1 + b2 + b3, which shows the net difference between the earned and charity protocol and the unearned and student protocol; (2) b1 + b3, which shows the effect of charity recipients with earned endowment, and (3) b2 + b3, which shows the effect of earned endowment with charity recipients. In the bottom of table 4, we report these three derived parameters.

5. Assessment of Publication Bias

To conclude the analysis, we examine the possibility of a publication-selection bias in our data set. The publication-selection bias in a typical meta-analysis refers to the phenomenon in which the effect size find in the literature is overestimated because studies with the expected outcome are more likely to be published by journals (Brada et al., 2021). “Donated” cannot be negative by construction and we do not have expectation around the exact value “Donated” should take in the range of 0 and 1. We examine publication bias first by using a traditional method that relies on funnel plots of the reported fraction of endowment donated and then by using specific analyses designated to assess publication bias.

The funnel plot is essentially a scatterplot with the effect size (in the current study it is the fraction of endowment donated) on the horizontal axis and the estimate precision measured by the inverse of standard error (1/SE) on the vertical axis. In the absence of publication-selection bias, estimates from independent studies should be distributed symmetrically around the true effect. Moreover, the dispersion of estimates and precision of estimate are negatively correlated, indicating the plot should have inverted funnel shape. In case the funnel plot lacks bilateral symmetry, the publication-selection bias is doubted suggesting estimates with expected sign are more frequently published (Brada et al., 2021). Figure 3 shows the funnel plot for the fraction of endowment donated. As “Donated” is truncated at 0 and 1 and its mean is less than 0.5, it is difficult to judge from this figure regarding the skewness. The traditional Egger test however, provides evidence for the existence of publication bias (z-stat = 4.85; p-value = 0.000).

To further analyze the possibility of publication-selection bias, we perform a funnel-asymmetry test (FAT) proposed by Stanley and Doucouliagos (2012) by estimating Equation (1) and subsequently testing the null hypothesis H0:β0=0, where Equation (1) is given by:(1)tk=β0+β1·(1/SEk)+εkWhere tk is t-value for Kth estimate for fraction of endowment donated and εk is error term. If the intercept (β0) is not zero, the estimates are asymmetrically distributed and the publication-selection bias may exist. The outcome for FAT reported in panel (a) in table 6 for all three regressions provide strong support that those studies whose standard error is small are more likely to be published. Therefore, a publication selection is implied from the regression results.

6. Conclusions

We performed a meta-analysis based on 80 dictator game studies that relied on lab experiments and examined the influence of ‘earned versus unearned’ and ‘student versus charity recipient’ on donations. We examined the aforementioned treatment effects by using typical meta-analytic method (random effects) as well as five recent methods frequently used in the latest studies. We find sufficient evidence that earning an endowment reduces benevolence when the recipient is a charity, while using charity instead of fellow students as the recipient enhances benevolence when the dictators make donations with unearned endowment. As our data consists of lab experiments only, we offer precise evidence that in lab experiments the interaction of endowment nature and recipient type has an important influence on the generosity of the dictators.

We also examined the effect of several control meta-variables on donations. However, most of them lead to insignificant results except for public donations and the effect of country of experiments: the dictators share less when their donations are made public (the generosity seems to crowd out) while dictators in the high-income countries are less generous than those in lower-income countries. The latter finding supports earlier findings by Engel (2011). Even though our data set contains 50 studies published after 2011, a relatively higher generosity in lower-income countries still exists and reflects the stability of this effect over time. As our sample does not include experiments conducted in the least developed countries, whether this contrast extends into such countries remains to be analyzed in the future research.

Our study also offers important lessons to researchers that examine behavior in lab. First, experimentalists measuring pro-social behavior should carefully decide between ‘earned and unearned’ protocols because the choice of the unearned protocol could lead to disproportionate generosity. Even though this selection depends on the research topic and objectives of the study, some lessons are obvious. For example, experimentalists aiming for higher external validity should employ the earned endowment protocol as it closely mimics the real-world scenario. Similarly, researchers interested in the effect of ‘effort’, ‘property rights’ or ‘ownership effect’ on generosity can achieve it if they use the earned endowment protocol. On the other hand, researchers interested in the effect of windfall transfers (such as lotteries or one-time cash transfers by governments14) on generosity should use unearned endowment in experiments as it is a true representation of a windfall gain.

Second, the nature of the recipient itself has a significant effect on generosity and we caution the experimentalists to carefully exercise this choice. Here again, the choice is largely motivated by the research objectives. For example, if researchers want to examine the influence of matching subsidy on charity donations, the obvious choice of recipients would be a charity organization. Furthermore, if the objective of research is to examine munificence towards religious and non-religious organizations, it would require religious and non-religious charities. Contrarily, a research that aims to examine the influence of in-group on pro-social behavior would require human subjects to create the in-group connections. If the research objective is neutral to the selection of recipients, we advise researchers to elicit generosity for both charity organizations and student recipient and report both. Lastly, the interaction of nature of endowment and recipient type indicates that generosity towards charity organizations is sensitive to the nature of endowment. Therefore, researchers should carefully decide the combination of nature of endowment and recipient because this combination can influence outcomes.

Appendix 3. Mathematical Analysis for Syntheses of Estimates

To perform meta-synthesis, we rely on the recent work of Brada et al. (2021, page 43-44) as a guiding source. If we assume that the proportion of endowment donated reported by the Kth estimate rk has a mean θk and standard error sk and θ1=θ2=…=θk=θ, then all studies in our meta-analysis measure the common underlying population effect and the estimates differ only because of the sampling errors. We can obtain an asymptotically efficient estimator of the unknown true population parameter θ by weighing the mean of all estimates by the inverse variance of each estimate (Brada et al., 2021). This is given by:(1a)Rf=∑k=1Krksk2/∑k=1K1sk2Rf is known as the meta-fixed effect estimate. In order to utilize fixed-effect estimates, it is important to examine that the estimates are homogeneous. This is achieved with the help of a test statistic with Chi-square distribution and K-1 degrees of freedom reported in equation 2a.(2a)Qr=∑k=1K1sk2(rk−Rf)2∼χ2(K−1)

The null hypothesis that effect sizes are homogenous across studies is rejected if Qr > the critical value. The results reported in Appendix 4 confirm that the null hypothesis is rejected indicating that the effect sizes are heterogenous across studies.

As the null hypothesis for homogeneous effect is rejected, we perform a random-effects estimation that incorporates differences due to underlying population as well as those due to sampling errors. If δθ2 represents the variation between estimates, the unconditional variance of Kth estimate is sk2+δθ2.Using the unconditional variance the meta-random effects estimates are calculated as:(3)Rr=∑k=1Krk(sk2+δθ2)/∑k=1K1(sk2+δθ2)

The between estimate variance δθ2is estimated by following equation:δθ2=Qr−(K−1)∑k=1K1(sk2+δθ2)−(∑k=1K1(sk2+δθ2)2/∑k−1K1(sk2+δθ2)),where Qr is the value of homogeneity test obtained from equation 2a.

The unrestricted weighted least squares (UWA) uses regression to obtain a synthesized effect. The regression used the standardized effect (t-value) as the dependent variable and the precision of estimates (inverse standard errors) as the independent variable to estimate the following equation:tk=α(1/sk)+∈kwhere α represents the synthesized value for the proportion of endowment donated while ∈k represents residual. The UWA is less subject to influence from excess heterogeneity than is the fixed-effect model. Furthermore, Stanley and Doucouliagos (2017) and Stanley et al. (2017) proposed conducting an UWA analysis in which the statistical power of the estimate exceeds the threshold of 0.80, and called this estimation method the weighted average of the adequately powered (WAAP). They stated that the WAAP synthesis has less publication selection bias than the traditional random-effects one.
