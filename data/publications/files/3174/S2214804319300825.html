Abstract

This paper explores the potential “bright side” of overconfidence. We examine the effects of two types of overconfidence—overestimation and overprecision—on the contribution to the public good in a two-person threshold public good game. Experimental results show that the influence of overconfidence on contribution in public goods crucially depends on the agents’ type of preferences. Overestimation leads to a higher contribution by conditional cooperators, but a lower contribution by free-riders. Overprecision, in general, leads to a higher contribution by conditional cooperators, but has little effect on the contribution of free-riders.

1. Introduction

Ample evidence in behavioral and experimental economics has shown that people deviate from the “homo economicus” model in public goods games (Isaac et al., 1984, Isaac et al., 1994, Andreoni, 1995, Keser and Van Winden, 2000, Kurzban et al., 2001, Andreoni and Petrie, 2004, Ahn et al., 2009, Gächter and Renner, 2010, Chaudhuri, 2011). An individual who purely maximizes a monetary payoff should make zero contribution, but subjects on average contribute 40% and 60% of their total endowment in one-shot and repeated linear public good games (Chaudhuri and Paichayontvijit, 2006). These results suggest a heterogeneity in individual preferences: some people are free-riders who only care about their own payoff, but many others can be categorized as conditional cooperators who tend to match the average contribution by others (Liebrand, 1984, Fischbacher et al., 2001, Burlando and Guala, 2005). Therefore, identifying institutions that can facilitate and sustain cooperation in the public good game when subjects exhibit heterogeneous preferences has received wide attention.

According to Ledyard (1995), communication and a threshold structure have long been considered effective means to enhance cooperation in public goods games. Communication can improve cooperation by influencing group members’ beliefs (Ellingsen et al., 2013). For example, “They (Individuals) communicate their intended actions in the hope of swaying the expectations and actions of others” (Duffy and Feltovich, 2002, P. 2), which also influences their beliefs. The threshold structure imposes a punishment if the total contribution by all group members is lower than a threshold level. This punishment provides an extra incentive for the group members to cooperate. In particular, when the marginal rate of punishment is greater than 1, free-riders also have incentives to contribute when they expect that the total contribution is going to be lower than the threshold otherwise. Hence, belief is also critical in determining how effective the threshold structure will be regarding enhancing cooperation.

Belief is a factor regarding the effectiveness of communication and threshold structure in promoting cooperation in public good games, and behavioral biases are similar in belief formation. As a commonly observed behavioral bias in belief formation, overconfidence may influence the contribution of free-riders and conditional cooperators. Overconfidence may easily occur in the context of a public goods game, that is, allowing subjects in the same group to communicate with each other through verbal communication/cheap talk (Duffy and Feltovich, 2002, Cason et al., 2012, Feltovich and Grossman, 2015, Palfrey et al., 2017) may generate the illusion of control; thus, a subject may be overconfident regarding how their message can influence others’ contribution level.1 In this situation, communication/cheap talk between partners in the same group acts as the “channel” that generates the illusion of control, that is, when a subject is overconfident about their ability to influence or persuade the others, they will overestimate the contribution by others.

The literature (Plous, 1993, Moore and Healy, 2008) has typically considered overconfidence a source of trouble in decision-making. We explore the potential “bright side” of overconfidence in this paper, and the main idea is as follows: if a conditional cooperator is overconfident about their ability to forecast others’ behavior and holds the belief that other players will contribute a lot, a conditional cooperator will probably match the overestimated contribution by the others and contribute more. By contrast, a free-rider aims to pursue that the maximization of their own interests may not match the contribution of their partner, or even reduce their contribution, when they think that the contribution by their opponent is sufficient to pass the threshold of contribution.

In the literature, overconfidence has been further divided into types (Cain et al., 2015). Among those types, overestimation and overprecision have been studied most extensively (Moore and Healy, 2008). Overestimation occurs when one's belief regarding one's ability, performance, or success rate is greater than the true level, whereas overprecision occurs when one is overconfident regarding the accuracy of their beliefs (Moore and Healy, 2008), for example, people usually answer a general knowledge question with too narrow a range of possible answers (Cain et al., 2015). In this paper, overestimation means the subject overestimates the contribution level by their opponents, whereas overprecision means the subject's prediction of the range (maximum and minimum) of their opponents is smaller than the true range. We set up a model with heterogeneous preferences to study how these two types of overconfidence influence contribution in a public good with threshold structure. We differentiate between overestimation and overprecision, hypothesize how they influence the contribution decision, and test our hypotheses.

According to our review of the literature, this paper is the first to establish a model to analyze the effect of overconfidence on contribution in a public good game in a comprehensive manner. The contribution of this study is thus showing how overconfidence influences contribution in public good games. Notably, Fischbacher and Gächter (2010) suggested that the individual contribution in public goods is influenced by heterogeneous preferences and beliefs, but they did not study how the two factors interact, especially how belief affects contribution considering free-rider and conditional cooperators, respectively.

Our model predicts that the influence of overconfidence depends on an individual's type of preference, and our results largely confirm the theoretical predictions of our model. More specifically, overestimation leads to a higher contribution by conditional cooperators, but a lower contribution by free-riders. Overprecision, in general, leads to a greater contribution by conditional cooperators.

The remainder of the paper is organized as follows: Section 2 reviews the literature; Section 3 provides the theoretical analysis and testable hypotheses; Section 4 details the experimental design; Section 5 reports the experimental results and tests of the hypotheses; and Section 6 concludes the paper and discusses potential future extensions.

2. Literature review

This paper studies how the interactions between overconfidence and heterogeneous preferences influence contribution in a threshold public good game. The related literature falls into three areas: first, the study of overconfidence in experiments; second, the threshold public goods game and cheap talk; and third, the beliefs and heterogeneous preferences in public goods games.

2.1. Experimental literature on the definition and measurement of overconfidence

Overconfidence is a ubiquitous behavioral bias in decision-making. As many psychological studies (e.g. Svenson, 1981) have shown, more than 93% of students in the United States (US) think they are more skillful than the median drivers, although this assertion is unlikely to be statistically possible.

Moore and Healy (2008) defined three types of overconfidence: overestimation, overprecision, and overplacement. Overestimation occurs when individuals’ beliefs in their own ability, performance, level of control and success rate are systematically higher than reality. For example, leaders of corporations may overestimate the success of expanding their firms (Malmendier and Tate, 2005, Brown and Sarma, 2007). Overprecision occurs when individuals underestimate the range or variation of the random variable, such as when “newsvendors” in the seminal newsvendor problem underestimate the variation in demand for newspapers (Ren and Croson, 2013). Overplacement, also called the “better than average effect,” refers to cases in which people consider themselves better than others in terms of ability or luck (Moore and Healy, 2008). We focus on overestimation and overprecision because they are the most prevalent in the literature. Moore and Healy (2008) observed approximately 64% and 31% of the empirical papers on overconfidence examine overestimation and overprecision, respectively.

Klayman et al., 1999, Moore and Healy, 2008, and Beer et al. (2010) have asked their subjects a few general knowledge questions before the experiments. They have used the difference between a participant's estimate of their number of correct answers and the actual number as a measure of overestimation. The measurement of overprecision usually takes the form of confidence intervals: the subjects give the maximum and minimum possible number for the answers to ten questions, such that there is a 90% chance that the correct answer falls into this interval (Nosić and Weber, 2010, Ren and Croson, 2013). If we assume the perfect rationality of the subject, the interval should include the correct answer for nine out of ten questions. If r is the number of correct answers provided by a subject, then 9−r is the measure for overprecision. For example, if a subject misses the correct answer for all ten questions, that is, he or she answers no question correctly, their intervals are overprecise, and the level of overprecision is 9.

Although overconfidence has been studied in different contexts in economics and business, including the expansion of firms (Roll, 1986), asset trading (Ko and Huang, 2007, Grégoire, 2016, Hanaki et al., 2018), the principal–agent problem (Zhou, 2015), and supply chain decisions (Ren and Croson, 2013), according to our review of the literature, no studies have been conducted on the public goods game.

2.2. Threshold public goods and cheap talk

We adopt a threshold public goods game with cheap talk. Threshold public goods are a form of nonlinear public good for which individual payoffs from the public good follow a piecewise linear function and depend on whether the total contribution of the group reaches a threshold (or provision point). Usually, the public good is only provided (and generates a positive return) if the total contribution reaches or exceeds the threshold (Isaac et al., 1989; Rondeau et al., 1999, Croson and Marks, 2000, Cadsby et al., 2008, Mak et al., 2015, Feltovich and Grossman, 2015).

The framework of threshold public goods can effectively capture different mechanisms in some real-life situations by setting the so-called refund and rebate rules. The refund (or money-back guarantee) rules indicate that players’ contributions will be returned if the threshold is not reached, whereas the rebate rules are commitments to return contributions which exceed the threshold (Cadsby and Maynes, 1999, Donazzan et al., 2016). Specifically, Isaac et al. (1989) proposed a rebate rule, defined as the utilization rebate policy by Marks and Croson (1998), where excess contributions were not wasted and provided continuous public goods.

We employed a threshold public good game with the utilization rebate policy and without any refund. Additionally, we imposed an additional punishment for the negative deviation of the contribution from the threshold, to simulate economic issues such as team production. Although some classical papers have proposed that team production can be examined by liner a public goods game to capture the positive externality of a team member's effort on the others (Alchian and Demsetz, 1972, Fehr and Gachter, 2000), we consider public good with threshold a more suitable design. For instance, there is a minimum requirement (i.e., threshold) for the performance or effort of a team in a firm and the whole team incurs severe punishment (e.g., layoff, penalty) if the performance is below this threshold, to ensure that the team members have less incentive to free-ride on other members. Another relevant example put forward by Mak et al. (2015) involves a post on the Wikimedia to request users’ donation, in which the Wikimedia claims that the subscription fee will potentially be higher than the donation charged if donations are insufficient to sustain the website.

Ample experimental evidence has shown that institutional factors such as offering rebates and refunds had a significant positive impact on contributions (Cadsby and Maynes, 1999, Croson and Marks, 2000, Cadsby et al., 2008, Donazzan et al., 2016). Additionally, permitting continuous rather than discrete contributions (usually designed as binary ‘‘all-or-nothing’’) and communication also facilitate contributions, whereas increasing the threshold level and group size decreases the contribution (Cadsby and Maynes, 1999, Croson and Marks, 2000). Among these factors, communication, even nonbinding cheap talk, is considered one of most effective solutions to improve cooperation regarding social dilemmas and often used in a public goods game (Balliet, 2010, Cason et al., 2012, Palfrey et al., 2017). Balliet (2010, p. 40) claimed, “if there is any single solution that has harnessed the most support and reduced the most conflict, it must be communication.”

Communication can facilitate cooperation and coordination in a comprehensive manner (Dawes and Messick, 2000, Duffy and Feltovich, 2002, van Dijk et al., 2009, Ellingsen and Östling, 2010, Feltovich and Grossman, 2015). On the one hand, communication helps establish norms of ‘‘appropriate’’ behavior by enhancing group identity and common understanding (Dawes and Messick, 2000, van Dijk et al., 2009) and reaching commitments regarding how to act, which is suggested as maybe more important by Mak et al. (2015). One the other hand, all communication, including nonbinding cheap talk, can decrease the strategic uncertainty from others (Riechmann and Weimann, 2008) and coordination difficulties (Feltovich and Swierzbinski, 2011), which may also influence willingness to cooperate (Cason et al., 2012).

We offer a novel perspective that communication also draws concerns regarding overconfidence. According to theory on the illusion of control, allowing subjects to communicate may generate a larger illusion of control such that a subject may be overconfident regarding how their message can influence others’ contribution level. When an individual thinks they can influence their partner's contribution through verbal communication/cheap talk (Cason et al., 2012, Palfrey et al., 2017), they are more likely to consider themselves able to influence others’ contribution decision, and thus will overestimate their partner's contribution. In other words, cheap talk between partners in the same group is the “channel” that generates overconfidence in our paper. We expect to observe an upward bias in the belief of another's contributions in a public goods game with cheap talk, and consider this bias mainly overconfidence (rather than over-optimism or unrealistic optimism).2

2.3. Studies on belief and heterogeneous preferences in public goods games

As overconfidence is a type of systematic bias in belief (Klayman et al., 1999, Moore and Healy, 2008, Beer et al., 2010), how overconfidence affects contributions can be explored by the roles of belief in the public goods game.

Beliefs, preferences, and constraints are central concepts in modern economics and at the core of the investigation of any game with strategic interaction (Gächter and Renner, 2010, Blanco et al., 2010). Experiments based on the liner public goods game have indicated that individuals’ contributions are highly influenced by their beliefs about other group members’ contributions (Fischbacher and Gachter, 2010, Kocher et al., 2015, Smith, 2015), and similar results have been found in the threshold (also called step-level) public goods game with binary contributions (Offerman et al., 1996, Offerman et al., 2001).

The positive relationship between beliefs and contributions in the public goods game can be largely attributed to the preference of conditional cooperation (Fischbacher and Gachter, 2010, de Oliveira et al., 2016). Conditional cooperation occurs when “the contribution to public good increases when the contribution by others, or one's belief on the contribution by others increases” (Fischbacher et al., 2001), that is, belief in others’ contribution is crucial in determining an individual's contribution: an individual will decrease (increase) their level of cooperation if they believes others will free-ride (cooperate) (Chaudhuri and Paichayontvijit, 2006, Fischbacher and Gachter, 2010, Rustagi et al., 2010, Chaudhuri, 2011).

According to Fischbacher et al. (2001) and Burlando and Guala (2005), most players can be categorized as conditional cooperators or free-riders. Fischbacher et al. (2001) differentiated four types of preferences (i.e., conditional cooperators, free-riders, triangle contributors, and uncategorized players) in a two-stage, strategic form public goods game and found 50% of subjects were categorized as conditional cooperators, whereas approximately 30% of subjects could be classified as free-riders. Similarly, Burlando and Guala (2005) differentiated three types of individuals (i.e., free-riders, conditional cooperators and pure or unconditional cooperators) by using four methods, namely, a two-stages strategic form public goods game, the decomposed game technique, a repeated linear public goods game, and a questionnaire survey, and found the results from the four methods were highly correlated.

To sum up, as contributions to a public good depend on belief, any systematic bias in belief (Klayman et al., 1999, Moore and Healy, 2008, Beer et al., 2010), such as overconfidence (including overestimation and overprecision), should also influence contributions. Additionally, overconfidence may interact with heterogeneous preferences on individuals’ contributions. For example, when a conditional cooperator overestimates a contribution by the others, he or she will also tend to contribute a large amount. However, the overestimation of free-riders makes them believe that the probability of group contributions reaching or exceeding the threshold will be higher, which relieves their concerns regarding suffering punishments due to the negative gap between group contributions and the threshold; thus, the free-riders will be more likely to free-ride and lower their contribution.

3. Theoretical framework and testable hypotheses

3.1. Theoretical framework

We employ a threshold public good game that imposes an additional incentive/punishment on the linear public good to better capture the mechanism in some real-life situations (Bagnoli and McKee, 1991). For example, to discourage free-riding behavior, many firms adopt incentive mechanisms into team production, which can be considered a threshold public good design: there is a minimum requirement for the performance of a team in a firm and the whole team will incur severe punishment (e.g., layoff, penalty) if the performance is below this minimum level. A major feature that differs is we incorporate a penalty when contributions fall below the threshold. This penalty is sufficiently large to ensure there is no free-riding equilibrium, that is, the optimal contribution is not zero even for purely self-interested individuals; instead, they should attempt to ensure the total contributions to the public good in a group increases to the threshold. The threshold public good design in this paper can be modeled as Eq. (1).(1)xi={Ei−Ii+λ(∑1nIj−T),if∑1nIj≥TEi−Ii−δ(T−∑1nIj),if∑1nIj<T,1n<λ<1,δ>1where Ei is the initial cash endowment, Ii is the contribution, λ is the marginal per capita return (MPCR) of the public good, and T is the threshold level. Payoff xi depends on whether the total contribution in i’s group, ∑1nIj reaches or exceeds T: if ∑1nIj>T, the difference between the total contribution and T, ∑1nIj−T generates returns for all individuals; otherwise, every group member is punished according to the difference between the total contributions and T multiplied by a coefficient δ, in which case a 1 unit increase in individual contribution leads to a δ unit decrease in the punishment. When the threshold T=0, the threshold public good game degenerates to a standard linear public good game.

In the free-riders section, we study how the effect of overconfidence on contribution in a public good depends on the preferences of the subjects. We differentiate types of preferences because subjects’ utilities are directly affected by their types of preferences: free-riders purely maximize their own material benefit xi, and conditional cooperators attempt to match the contributions of their counterparts.3

3.1.2. Conditional cooperators

Fischbacher et al. (2001) observed that approximately 50% of subjects in public good experiments can be categorized as conditional cooperators. Conditional cooperation occurs when participants attempt to match the contributions of their counterparts (Chaudhuri and Paichayontvijit, 2006, Fischbacher and Gachter, 2010, Rustagi et al., 2010, Chaudhuri, 2011). For example, experimental studies usually treat a subject as a conditional cooperator if a highly significant (at the 0.1% level) and positive Spearman rank correlation coefficient is observed between the subject's and others’ contribution (Fischbacher et al., 2001), or the difference between the subject's and other's contribution (or the subject's belief in their partner's contribution) is less than 10% of the initial endowment (Burlando and Guala, 2005).

Therefore, a perfect conditional cooperator will attempt to ensure their contribution I equals their partners’ contribution J, and any bias between I and J generates psychological cost or utility loss. More specifically, we assume one partner suffers a utility loss θ·(I−J) when contributing more (I ≥ J), and a utility loss φ·(J−I) when contributing less than that their partner (I < J). In this situation, the utility function of a conditional cooperator can be expressed by Eq. (11).(11)S={−θ·(I−J),ifI≥J−φ·(J−I),ifI<J

The individual does not know the specific value of the contribution of their partner; thus, an individual has difficulty ensuring their contribution is exactly the same as their partner's contribution. In this situation, the individual must evaluate the tradeoff between φ(J−I) and θ(I−J). The question here is whether the magnitude of utility loss suffered when I < J equals that when I > J, that is, is φ equals to θ.

The research related to the inequality aversion (Fehr and Schmidt, 1999) suggests θ > φ, in which authors claim individuals respond differently when their contributions are greater than others compared with less than others, and “individuals typically care more, and so respond more, to cases where they find that they are giving more than others (unfavorable inequality) than cases where they are giving less (favorable inequality, Ashley et al., 2010, p. 19).”

Additionally, the literature has suggested that conditional cooperation is related to many preferences, such as inequity aversion, reciprocity, altruism, warm-glow, conformity, or social norms (Fischbacher et al., 2001, Frey and Meier, 2004, Chaudhuri, 2011) or, according to Fischbacher et al. (2001), a motivation. This suggestion indicates conditional cooperation is not just a consequence of inequity aversion, and θ > φ may not always be correct. For example, theories of reciprocity indicate no asymmetry of response and “predict only that agents will try to do what other members of their group do” (Ashley et al., 2010, p.16). To make Eq. (11) more general, we consider situations θ > φ and θ=φ and assert following propositions. The calculations are in Appendix A.

Proposition 3

A greater overestimation parameter α leads to a higher contribution in public good by the conditional cooperators.

Proposition 4a

A greater overprecision parameter β leads to higher contribution in public good by the conditional cooperators, if θ > φ.

Proposition 4b

the overprecision parameter β has no effects on contribution in public good by the conditional cooperators, if θ=φ.

3.2. Theoretical hypotheses

According to Propositions 1 and 3, the contribution of a free-rider to the public good is negatively correlated with their level of overestimation, whereas the contribution of a conditional cooperator is positively correlated with their level of overestimation. Free-riders intuitively maximize their payoff and attempt to contribute less as long as the contributions of the whole group reach the threshold. When free-riders overestimate others’ contribution, they also overestimate the likelihood of the total contribution of the group passing the threshold, which leads them to contribute less (Io* < I*). For conditional cooperators, the logic behind Proposition 3 is easier to interpret: they attempt to “match” the contribution level by their partners and tend to contribute more if they overestimate their partners’ contribution.

Hypothesis 1

Individuals’ contributions in a public good are influenced by their level of overestimation.

–

Hypothesis 1a: Free-riders’ contributions are lower when their level of overestimation is higher.

–

Hypothesis 1b: Conditional cooperators’ contributions are higher when their level of overestimation is higher.

According to Proposition 2, a free-rider's contribution in a threshold public good is positively correlated with MPCR. A free-rider searches for an optimal contribution level I* to maximize their individual payoff. A free-rider incurs a punishment of δ(I*−I) if their contribution I is below the optimal level I* and suffers a “loss” of income (1−λ)(I−I*) due to the failure to free-ride if they contribute more (I−I*>0). A free-rider thus faces a tradeoff between these two potential losses. When λ (i.e., MPCR) increases, a free-rider suffers less when they over-contributes. A free-rider should then allocate more weight to the potential loss due to under-contribution, and hence contribute more.

Proposition 2 also indicates that overprecision lowers the extent to which MPCR positively influences contribution in a public good. Consider one extreme case as an example: suppose the threshold of public good contribution is 10, and a free-rider knows their partner will certainly contribute 6. In this situation, the free-rider will contribute 4 no matter how high or low the MPCR. To test Proposition 2, we set two treatments in the experiment that vary the MPCR at two levels (MPCR = 0.8 VS. MPCR = 0.6) and state Hypothesis 2 as follows.

Hypothesis 2

The increase in the contribution by free-riders when MPCR increases is negatively correlated with overprecision.

According to Proposition 4a, if θ > φ, the conditional cooperator will tend to under-contribute because they dislike disadvantageous inequality more than advantageous inequality, which is consistent with asymmetry in inequality aversion (Fehr and Schmidt, 1999, Ashley et al., 2010). In this situation, overprecision means lower uncertainty in the contribution by the partner, and hence lower under-contribution due to the tradeoff. Thus, overprecision should lead to a higher contribution by conditional cooperators. By contrast, if θ=φ, Proposition 4b suggests the conditional cooperator is not affected by the uncertainty of their partner's contribution, because the magnitude of utility loss they suffered when contributing less than their partner equals the situation that their partner contributes more. Overall, conditional cooperators’ overprecision either leads to higher contribution in a public good or has no effect on contribution, depending on how they tradeoff between the advantageous inequality parameter φ and disadvantageous inequality parameter θ.

Hypothesis 3

Conditional cooperators’ overprecision either leads to higher contribution in a public good or has no effect on contribution.

Hypothesis 3a

Contribution by conditional cooperators increases with overprecision.

Hypothesis 3b

Contribution by conditional cooperators is not related to overprecision.

4. Experimental design

This experiment employs a within-subject design and consists of three parts: one public goods task including two treatments/stages (Stage I and Stage II), one social value orientation (SVO) task (Stage III), and one general knowledge task (Stage IV). The unit of payoff in the experiment is tokens, and the exchange ratio is 1 token = RMB 0.5 for the public goods task and general knowledge task, and 1 token = RMB 0.1 for the SVO task. After the experiment, the payments in tokens are exchanged for cash without rounding. For the public good task, one randomly chosen period is paid in Stages I and II. All subjects can refer to the information regarding payoff determination in the Experimental Instructions.

4.1. Public goods task

Our public goods task comprises two computerized experimental stages (Stage I and Stage II) to assess the treatment effect of MPCR, in which the only difference is in the value of MPCR (the low MPCR treatment and high MPCR treatment). Both experiments are threshold public good games with cheap talk.

In Stage I (the high MPCR treatment), the subjects are randomly assigned to groups of two people. Each subject is endowed with 20 tokens. The payoff functions for each subject are as follows.

(1)

When the total number of tokens contributed to the public good is below 10,payoffi=20−contributioni−1.3(10−Totalcontribution).

(2)

When the total number of tokens contributed to the public good is greater than or equal to 10,payoffi=20−contributioni+0.8(Totalcontribution−10).

The subjects can conduct 90-second nonbinding verbal communication (cheap talk) at the beginning of each period. The subjects are not allowed to mention any identity information such as their name, major, or school. After communication, the subjects are asked to contribute to the public good with an integer amount and indicate their beliefs about their partners’ contribution in the current period.

(1)

Please indicate your belief on how many tokens your partner contributes to the public account.

(2)

Please indicate your belief on the maximum number of tokens your partner contributes to the public account.

(3)

Please indicate your belief on the minimum number of tokens your partner contributes to the public account.

In addition to their earnings from the public goods game, subjects are also incentivized based on the accuracy of their estimates, but to a smaller scale to avoid hedging (Fischbacher and Gächter, 2010). For question (1), if the difference between the subject's answer and the true number is no more than one token, the subject will receive 5 additional tokens. For questions (2) and (3), if the number of the tokens invested by the subject's partner falls between [minimum belief, maximum belief], the subject receives an additional 0.5*[20-(maximum belief - minimum belief)]. Otherwise, there is no additional payoff for these two questions.

We elicited beliefs for two reasons. First, we can assess the correlation between beliefs and contributions, which we expect to differ between types of players (Burlando and Guala, 2005, Fischbacher and Gachter, 2010). Second, we create a measure of overconfidence based on elicited beliefs regarding others’ contributions in a public goods game. When an individual has a high level of overestimation, they believe their partner's contribution should be larger than the actual contribution of partner. We measure overestimation as overestimation = belief - partner's contribution. Moreover, when an individual has a high level of overprecision, the gap between their upper and lower bound of belief on their partner's contribution should be smaller. Thus, overprecision can be expressed by the indicator overprecision = −(maximum belief - minimum belief)

Communication facilitates cooperation in the public good game (Cason et al., 2012), for example, communication can enhance group identity and common understanding (Dawes and Messick, 2000, van Dijk et al., 2009); most important, communication achieves commitments regarding how to act or the amount to contribute (Mak et al., 2015). To control the effect of communication on contribution, we ask subjects to evaluate the effectiveness of the communication (on a 5-point scale). Additionally, considering the self-reported data may be noisy, we also introduce a dummy variable “Commitment” by hand collecting their messages during the communication, which equals 1 if the group members achieve commitments or consensus on the level of contribution, and 0 otherwise. The advantage of this variable is it is suggested to be the most important method for measuring how communication affects behavior (Mak et al., 2015) and has greater objectivity than other subjective measures.

Stage I consists of four periods. One randomly chosen period is paid after the experiment. The subjects are re-matched after each period to avoid reputation effects between subjects in repeated games. The subjects do not know the contributions and beliefs of their partner, or the payoff in each period, to avoid subjects updating their beliefs through dynamic learning.4 We use the multi-period design to increase the number of observations and increase the more power of the results. Based on Burlando and Guala (2005), the multi-period design is also useful for measuring the heterogeneity of individual preferences.

Stage II (the low MPCR treatment) is the same as Stage I, except the MPCR decreases from 0.8 to 0.6. The comparison between Stages I and II is helpful in testing Hypotheses 2. There is also an SVO task (Stage III) involving the decomposed game technique (Section 4.2).

The final part is the post-experiment questionnaire, to collect the subjects’ demographic information such as sex, age, education, and degree level.

4.2. Method to categorize heterogeneous preferences and SVO task

Burlando and Guala (2005) summarized four methods to categorize heterogeneous preferences: a two-stage strategic game, the decomposed game technique, a linear public good game, and the questionnaire method.

The categorization for a linear public good game works as follows: if the average contribution by an individual is not very different from the average contribution by their partner, this person is categorized as type R (conditional cooperators). When the average contribution by an individual is very small and does not vary in response to the average contribution by their partner, the individual is categorized as type F (free-riders). Otherwise, the individual is categorized as type C (other cooperators).

We mainly use the method of public good game to categorize heterogeneous preferences for the following reasons. First, Burlando and Guala (2005) demonstrated remarkable consistency between their four methods of categorizing heterogeneous preferences. Second, we have a communication stage in our experiment; thus, communication tends to promote prosocial behavior even when a nonbinding (Cason et al., 2012), two-stage strategic game is not very suitable.5

However, compared with linear public good, the threshold public good design in our paper alters the payoff function of the game, and this leads the behavior of free-riders to differ substantially from the linear version. Hence, different criteria are required to categorize preferences. To maximize benefits, a free-rider should attempt to ensure that the total contributions to the public good in their group reaches the threshold (i.e. making the free-rider's contribution+belief=10). In addition, if a free-rider believes their partner contributes more than 10, the free-rider will enjoy free-riding and contribute as little as possible. Therefore, the theoretical prediction of contribution by the free-rider is contribution={0,ifbelief≥1010−belief,ifbelief<10, and the theoretical prediction of contributions by the conditional cooperator is contribution=belief.

We categorize an individual into a type if the difference between their actual contribution and the theoretical prediction is less than 10% of the initial endowment (Burlando and Guala, 2005). Our criteria are therefore,

(1)

When |contribution−belief|≤2, consider this person a conditional cooperator; and

(2)

When contribution ≤ 10, and one of the following is satisfied, consider this person as a Free-rider:

a.

When belief≥10,contribution<=2

b.

When belief < 10,|contribution+belief−10|≤2

(3)

When contribution > 10 and |contribution−belief|>2, consider this person as an unconditional cooperator.

(4)

Consider those who do not satisfy (1), (2) or (3) as Noisy.

We use the first period of each four-period game to classify types of preference, and the last three periods to collect data on contribution in the public good.6 Some individuals may satisfy (1) and (2) simultaneously. For example, it is impossible to classify a subject as a conditional cooperator or a free-rider by relying on the criteria if their contribution and belief are both 5 points. Therefore, we elicit the type of preferences by using a decomposed game technique in Stage III, as suggested by Burlando and Guala (2005), as an auxiliary criterion.

Burlando and Guala (2005) performed the decomposed game technique to divide preference types. The decomposed game has been widely used by psychologists who prefer calling social preferences or other-regarding preferences SVO, which indicates the weights people assign to their own and others’ outcomes in situations of social interaction (Balliet et al., 2009, Murphy et al., 2011, Yang et al., 2016). In Stage III, we perform the primary SVO slider items, a modified decomposed game technique proposed by Murphy et al., (2011) in which a subject can be clarified as a free-rider (be individualistic) or cooperator (be prosocial) by investigating their SVO angle.7

In this stage, each subject is informed they have been randomly paired with an anonymous person. The subject is requested to make a series of six decisions regarding allocating resources between the subject and this other person by selecting the respective button along the midline. In the example given in Fig. 1, a person chose to distribute money so that he/she receives 85 tokens, but the anonymous other person receives 50 tokens. One of the six questions will be randomly chosen to be paid after the end of the experiment.

4.3. General knowledge task

We also measure overestimation and overprecision by performing abstract, general knowledge tasks (Klayman et al., 1999, Moore and Healy, 2008, Beer et al., 2010). Overprecision is measured by the standard confidence interval method, that is, by finding the minimum and maximum value of the 90% confidence interval of the answers to ten general knowledge questions (Nosić and Weber, 2010, Ren and Croson, 2013), in which subjects receive a fixed reward of 10 tokens after the completion of the task. Subjects were first requested to answer ten general knowledge questions sparked by Moore and Healy (2008) and rewarded with one token for each question answered correctly. We use the number of questions the subjects’ think they answered correctly minus the actual number of correct answers as the measure of overestimation.

However, we are aware that the overconfidence measured in the general knowledge task may not have a strong correlation to overconfidence in another specific task, like in the public good game, because Moore and Healy (2008) demonstrated that individuals may exhibit different types and levels of overconfidence in different games. Therefore, designing a context-dependent measure for overconfidence is better when applying this concept to different fields of study or practice (e.g., public good game, investment in stock markets, mergers and acquisition). Since overconfidence measured in the general knowledge task may have insufficient predictive power in contributions to the public goods, we focus on overconfidence measured by elicited beliefs rather than general knowledge tasks in this paper, and treat overconfidence measured in the general knowledge task as control variables.8

The experiment was run in the Selten Experimental Economics Lab of Nankai University in June 2015. Six sessions were conducted, and 20 subjects participated in each session. We used the “Questionnaire Star” (www.wjx.cn/weixinlogin.aspx) app on the WeChat platform to recruit subjects. All 120 subjects were bachelor's or master's students at Nankai University or Tianjin University; 65.83% of were female, and 44.17% were economic-related students. To consider the order effect, we ran MPCR = 0.8 (high MPCR treatment) first in sessions 1–3, and MPCR = 0.6 (low MPCR treatment) first in sessions 4–6. The average duration of each session was 90 min, with an average payment of RMB 45 (approximately USD 7). The experimental software was Z-tree (Fischbacher, 2007). 5.4. Overconfidence in a public good without communication

We set a communication device to induce overconfidence in a public good game, assuming intention transmissions among group members can generate the illusion of control and overconfidence. However, communication may also influence contributions to the public good by affecting the social distance/group identity among subjects (Dawes and Messick, 2000, van Dijk et al., 2009). To test whether communication generates overconfidence, we run two additional sessions where there is no communication, as suggested by the reviewer. If communication generates overconfidence, the overestimation in these two sessions without communication should be lower than those of the sessions with communication.

The experiment was conducted in the same laboratory in September 2018, and 42 subjects participated in the new sessions. The subjects were bachelor's or master's students at Nankai University or Tianjin University, the same as those in the experiment with communication. As above results show there are no order effects we conducted one MPCR order that starts with the high MPCR treatment in both sessions. The experiment procedure was the same as in the previous experiment, except we removed the communication stage. The average duration of each session was approximately 1 h with an average payment of RMB 50 (approximately USD 7–8).

The descriptive statistics of the experiments with and without communication are reported in Table 6. We are mainly interested in whether communication leads to overconfidence. The results support our conjecture. The level of overestimation is significantly smaller when communication is removed (1.194<2.585,t=−2.514,p=0.046). The lower level of the overestimate also leads to a lower belief on others’ contribution and lower contribution (8.274<13.1,t=−4.804,p=0.003). Additionally, the range between the minimum belief and the maximum belief in the experiment without communication is [4.563, 13.742], which is larger than the range [12.563, 17.093] in the experiment with communication. A smaller range shows the subjects feel less uncertainty regarding their partner's contribution, and thus have a higher level of overprecision after communicating. Overall, the results suggest that communication among group members generates overconfidence and a higher contribution by the participants.

6. Conclusion

This paper investigates the effect of overconfidence in the two-person threshold public good game with cheap talk. We consider two forms of overconfidence—overestimation and overprecision—and study the interaction between overconfidence and heterogeneous preferences. We observe that the effect of overestimates on contribution depends on the type of agent: a higher contribution for conditional cooperators, but a lower contribution by free-riders. Further, we observe that high overprecision leads to a higher contribution by conditional cooperators, but has little influence on the contribution by free-riders.

First, our findings increase the understanding of the roles of preferences and beliefs in the public goods game. The experimental studies on public goods have usually ignored the interactions between preferences and beliefs and do not consider how the bias of belief, such as overconfidence, affects the contribution under different preference types. This paper therefore makes a theoretical contribution by analyzing how preferences, beliefs, and belief biases interact to influence behavior.

Second, we introduce overconfidence, a concept typically used in the context of individual decision-making, to decisions in a group in the public goods game framework. Most studies on overconfidence have mainly considered the impact of overconfidence on individual interests and have rarely analyzed how overconfidence influences cooperation and thus affects collective interests. Our findings suggest that although overconfidence usually leads to one's contribution in a public good to deviate from the optimal level, and thus a lower individual utility, overconfidence may improve social welfare under some conditions. This result is because when conditional cooperators overestimate the contribution by their partners, they may contribute more. As most of the participants (59.2%) can be categorized as conditional cooperators, overconfidence as a whole plays a positive role in facilitating contributions to public goods, and this is an example of how overconfidence may help to overcome a social dilemma, internalize positive externality, and enhance social welfare.

Some may wonder whether the result in our paper can be generalized to the situation linear public good games without threshold. We expect that the results of overestimation and overprecision for conditional cooperators should be the same in a linear public goods game, as conditional cooperators aim to match the behavior of their partners and thus are not affected by the penalty related to the threshold framework. Therefore, overestimation and overprecision should still lead to a higher contribution from conditional cooperators. Free-riders only need to contribute zero without worrying about any penalty in a liner public goods game, and overestimation and overprecision should have no influence on their contributions.

Our study considers the interaction between overconfidence and heterogeneous preferences in a static setting. We leave the case in which individuals can update their beliefs and experience learning in dynamic games to future studies. Additionally, we “induce” overconfidence by allowing subjects communicate with each other in this paper, other means of generating and measuring overconfidence and its effects on contribution in the public goods can also be considered in future works.

In general, when there is no uncertainty about subjects’ type, the solution of the game looks trivial. When there is uncertainty about subjects’ type, the subjects have to form belief mainly about their opponents’ type and their strategy in equilibrium is a corner solution.

The result of the analysis based on Nash Equilibrium goes in line with our result based on individual optimization. On the one hand, the level of contribution is endogenous in the model based on NE, so it is difficult to introduce overconfidence directly. On the other hand, since we know that the contribution by a conditional cooperator is in general higher than the contribution by a free rider, the fraction of free riders p is an inverse proxy of the contribution by the other person. To this end, the findings in NE that a free rider/conditional cooperator will contribute more/less when p is larger/smaller are equivalent to the results in individual optimization that a free rider/conditional cooperator will contribute more/less when the he/she is underestimates/overestimates the contribution by the other person.

According to the theory of illusion of control, if individuals consider themselves capable of influencing or controlling an event (though they may not be actually able to), they will have a tendency to think they have the ability to steer the event to a desirable outcome, and thus be overconfident (Weinstein, 1980, Malmendier and Tate, 2005).

Upward bias in belief formation is sometimes referred to as “over-optimism” rather than “overconfidence” (Malmendier and Tate, 2005). Theory on illusion of control suggests individuals tend to be overconfident if they consider themselves capable of controlling or influencing an event (Weinstein, 1980, Malmendier and Tate, 2005). Thus, Malmendier and Tate (2005) choose the label "overconfidence" when the belief bias regarding the outcome of an event could be due to one's own abilities (such as IQ or managerial skills) and hold the idea that “over-optimism” would occur when faced with events not under personal control (such as the growth of the US economy).
