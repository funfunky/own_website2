Dictator game variants with probabilistic (and cost-saving) payoffs: A systematic test☆

Abstract

Driven by methodological concerns, theoretical considerations, and previous evidence, I systematically test the validity of common dictator game variants with probabilistic payoffs. Using a unified experimental framework, I include four approaches and compare them to a standard dictator game: involving fewer receivers than dictators, paying only some players, paying only some decisions, and role uncertainty. I also relate transfers in the dictator game variants to established complementary individual difference measures of prosociality: social value orientation, personal values, a donation to charity, and the Big Five personality factor agreeableness. My data shows that the standard dictator game presents the expected correlations with the complementary measures of prosociality. Involving fewer receivers yields comparably valid results. By contrast, when only some players or decisions are paid or, particularly, when subjects face role uncertainty, the expected associations with complementary prosociality measures are distorted. Under role uncertainty, generosity is also significantly biased upward. I conclude that the validity of dictator game outcomes is highly sensitive to the applied methods. Not all dictator game variants can be recommended for the valid measurement of social preferences.

1. Introduction

The dictator game (DG) is an extensively used paradigm to study social preferences (see Engel, 2011). It is very popular because of its simplicity. The standard DG entails dyads of randomly one-to-one matched dictators and receivers. Typically, player roles are allocated randomly. The dictator makes one decision and determines her own and the payoff of the matched receiver – who remains inactive – by splitting a given prize. Despite the simple structure, experimenters face practical constraints that complicate the accurate implementation of the standard DG: available monetary funds and laboratory capacities are often limited; the required experimental subjects are scarce or hardly accessible. Think, for example, of the challenges that a DG experiment with highly paid company executives would entail.

To handle such constraints, researchers modify the standard protocol of the DG. There are different approaches: (i) As the receiver is an inactive player, the number of receivers can be reduced. An unbalanced matching is applied such that only some from the group of dictators are actually matched with the available receivers (e.g., Houser and Schunk, 2009). Researchers do this for practical reasons, but typically do not reveal this to their subjects. In social psychology experiments, receivers often do not even exist (e.g., Batson et al., 1997, admitted on p. 1339). (ii) To make the experiment more cost-effective, only a randomly determined fraction of dictators and receivers might be paid (e.g., Dalbert and Umlauft, 2009, Dufwenberg and Muren, 2006, Holm et al., 2005, Kahneman et al., 1986, Sefton, 1992). (iii) The DG can be repeated or subjects can make several similarly structured decisions, including a DG transfer, either in separate games or by applying the strategy method. For payoffs, one decision is randomly chosen and paid out (e.g., Ashraf and Bohnet, 2006, Cappelen et al., 2007, Eckel and Grossman, 2000, Rankin, 2006). (iv) Introducing role uncertainty, all subjects can initially make a decision in the role of the dictator. Afterward, actual player roles are assigned randomly, the determined dictators are randomly matched with the receivers, and the players are paid according to the decision of the actual, assigned dictator (e.g., Charness and Grosskopf, 2001, Engelmann and Strobel, 2004, Iriberri and Rey-Biel, 2011). In this way, one observation per participant can be gained and the DG is applicable as a “companion measure” of generosity. Many studies also combine random payment of decisions and role uncertainty to obtain a maximum amount of data and enable within-subject comparisons. In such cases, the subjects make several decisions as dictators. Afterwards, the payoff-determining decision is randomly chosen, actual player roles are randomly assigned, and players are randomly matched and paid out (e.g., Andreoni and Miller, 2002, Andreoni and Vesterlund, 2001, Ashraf and Bohnet, 2006, Blanco et al., 2011, Castillo and Cross, 2008, Charness and Rabin, 2002, Engelmann and Strobel, 2004). v) In some cases, the researchers do not offer the participants monetary incentives and let them make hypothetical decisions (Ben-Ner et al., 2008, Bühren and Kundt, 2015, Dalbert and Umlauft, 2009).

The different randomization procedures yield probabilistic incentives, i.e., different probabilities for how a dictator’s decision eventually converts into her own payoff and that of a receiver. In this paper, I systematically test the impact of probabilistic incentives on DG outcomes. Using a strictly controlled approach, I study whether the source of randomization – using unbalanced matching of dictators and receivers, random payment of players or decisions, or role uncertainty – interferes with measured generosity and the correlations with complementary measures of prosociality. Furthermore, I investigate how the degree of randomization, whether single (e.g., in case of unbalanced matching) or multiple randomizations (e.g., in situations with random payment of decisions and role uncertainty) alter dictator behavior. The degree of randomization determines the probability with which a dictator’s decision specifies players’ payoffs.

My data confirm that dictator behavior is quite sensitive to the methodology applied. The standard DG presents the expected correlations with the complementary measures of prosociality. Further, only the DG variant that uses less receivers than dictators yields comparably valid results. Results from the DG variants with role uncertainty substantiate concerns previously expressed by other researchers: transfers are biased upward and the associations with complementary measures of prosociality are distorted. Moreover, although I find no evidence that the transfer level is significantly different relative to the standard DG variant, in DG variants where decisions (in the case of multiple decisions) or only some players are paid randomly, giving is not associated with the complementary measures of prosociality.

In Section 2, I summarize the literature’s suggestions regarding the impact of probabilistic incentives in DGs. In Section 3, I describe the experimental setup and the specific procedures that ensure strict control and comparability of the applied DG variants. In Section 4, I present my results and run additional validity tests. In Section 5, I discuss the results and conclude.

2. The impact of probabilistic incentives

Assuming self-regarding, purely money-maximizing preferences, dictator transfers should not differ across DG variants. Dictators would keep the entire prize for themselves. A lack of transfer differences is also predicted by the invariance hypothesis (Güth et al., 1982, Bolle, 1990). When decision costs are small and anonymity is guaranteed (i.e., the dictator does not have to care about her image), dictators would make identical transfers across DG variants independently of how their decisions convert into actual payoffs. Contrasting the predictions from the classical model and the invariance hypothesis, Camerer, 2003, Cox, 2010, and Smith (2010) emphasize that DG results are quite sensitive to procedural changes. Looking at further evidence from the literature confirms this view.

The probability of being pivotal. A first line of results illuminates the impact of how a dictator decision translates into actual payoffs. Engel (2011) finds that the less a dictator is sure that her intended behavior will be effective (i.e., can enhance the receiver’s welfare), the less she transfers to the receiver. One possible explanation for this pattern can be derived from the dictator’s social image concerns. Low transfers are less likely to hurt the receiver, and the less effective the dictator’s behavior becomes, the less she needs to care about her appearance in the eyes of the receiver (Andreoni and Bernheim, 2009). This may also create moral wiggle room for the dictators to exploit. Hence, in a DG variant where only some dictators are actually matched with a receiver (see Receiver50 below), this implies that dictators should transfer less compared to the standard DG.TheFehr-Schmidt model on inequity aversion would predict the same tendency, as disutility from advantageous inequality becomes less likely.1

When only some decisions of a dictator or some randomly chosen dictators are paid, no significant effect on generosity has been detected (Engel, 2011; see also Bolle, 1990, Laury, 2005).2 However, referring to the random payment of decisions in their experiment (which included a DG), Ashraf and Bohnet (2006) acknowledge, “While we control for design effects to the best of our ability, we cannot exclude the possibility that our design affects behavior differently than other, more standard, designs” (p. 197).

While some studies (e.g., Engelmann and Strobel, 2004) suggest that results are robust toward role uncertainty, Iriberri and Rey-Biel (2011), who directly studied the impact of role uncertainty in a modified DG, find that it overestimates the prevalence of altruistic preferences substantially (see also Mesa-Vázquez et al., 2019). They conclude that “[…] our results definitely warn against the use of role uncertainty in future experimental designs aiming to identify different motives behind non-selfish behavior” (p. 171).

Expected payoffs. A second stream of literature deals with the impact of stake size. In their review articles, Camerer et al. (1999) and Engel (2011) show that people are less generous when playing with higher stakes. Higher stakes also reduce transfers induced by presentation effects or image concerns. According to these results, if a dictator’s expected payoff becomes smaller due to the application of multiple randomization procedures, her transfers will increase and she can display (more) socially desirable behavior at a lower cost.

In DG variants where the dictator’s decision becomes less effective but also her own stake also becomes smaller compared to the standard DG (see Pay50,Decision50,Role50, and DecisionRole25 below), the potential effects of pivotality and expected payoffs conflict: on the one hand, transfers may increase, presentation effects or image concerns may become more prevalent, as they are less costly. On the other hand, transfers may decrease, because the dictators need to care less about their appearance in the eyes of the receivers. Assuming that dictators prefer to save money over spending money – i.e., that the first argument dominates the second one – I expect less generosity as long as a dictator has some stake (i.e., her decision influences her own payoff to some extent). An argument supporting this assumption is that people tend to be self-servingly biased (e.g., Loewenstein et al., 1993), such that they care more for their own payoffs than for the payoffs of other players. Fehr and Schmidt (1999) make a similar assumption in their model on inequity aversion: A player’s disutility from disadvantageous inequality is greater than her disutility if the player is better off than another player. TheFehr-Schmidt model also partly predicts lesser transfers in a situation where a dictator’s decision becomes less effective for herself and the receiver. For β - parameters 0 and 0.25, the model predicts DG variant-invariant transfers of x = 0 given a pie of 10. For β = 0.6, dictator transfers are equal to x = 5 of the pie in the standard DG, and x = 0 in the other variants.3

All in all, the evidence on how specific forms of randomization interfere with generosity is mixed, and it remains an open question how changing the incentive structure influences dictator giving and its foundations. Because the existing studies feature heterogeneous procedures, their results are hard to compare conclusively. To the best of my knowledge, my study is the first to systematically investigate the different randomization procedures and their potential influence on DG giving within a cohesive and controlled experimental approach.

3. The experiment

3.1. Dictator game variants

In all experiments, I study the same underlying DG structure: the dictator determines the allocation of €10 among herself and a receiver by choosing an integer amountx from €0 to €10 to be given to the receiver. I implement six DG variants with monetary incentives.4 Hypothetical DG variants are not included because paying at least some subjects has become standard in experimental economics. It is argued that monetary incentives ensure that participants perceive their behavior as relevant and experience real emotions. Only when the subjects face real economic consequences from their decisions can the experimer have control over their preferences: subjects have to prefer more of the reward medium to less of the reward medium, must not become satiated, and the reward must depend on the subject’s actions (Falk and Heckman, 2009, Smith, 1976).5

Reference Game (Baseline): Baseline represents the most common, but also most demanding, form of DG implementation. It requires the most resources (subjects, budget, time, and lab space) and serves as the gold standard for my comparisons. Subjects are first randomly assigned a player role (dictator or receiver) with a probability of 1/2. After role assignment, there is the same number of dictators and receivers. Then, all dictators and receivers are randomly matched. This implies that the dictators’ decisions determine their own payoffs and those of the matched receivers for certain. Finally, each dictator determines the allocation. All dictator decisions are paid.

Unbalanced Matching (Receiver50): Compared to Baseline and depending on the specification of the parameters, fewer receivers need to be recruited and paid. First, subjects are randomly assigned a player role with a probability of 2/3 for taking the role of a dictator and with a probability of 1/3 for taking the role of a receiver. Consequently, there are twice as many dictators than receivers. Next, 50% of the dictators are randomly chosen and matched randomly with a receiver.This implies that 50% of the dictators actually remain unmatched. Dictator decisions determine their own payoffs with certainty. A receiver’s payoff is determined only with a probability of 1/2 from the perspective of a dictator. Finally, each dictator determines the allocation. All dictator decisions are paid (including to the dictators who remain unmatched).

Probabilistic Player Payment (Pay50): Pay50 costs less than Baseline because only 1/2 of the subjects are actually to be paid. First, subjects are randomly assigned a player role (dictator or receiver) with a probability of 1/2. After role assignment, there is the same number of dictators and receivers. Then, dictators and receivers are randomly matched. Finally, each dictator determines the allocation. For payoff determination, 1/2 of the dictator–receiver dyads are randomly selected and paid out. This means that the dictators’ decisions determine their own and the matched receivers’ payoffs with a probability of 1/2.

Probabilistic Decision Payment (Decision50): Twice as many observations are collected relative to Baseline.Moreover, within-subject comparisons are possible. First, subjects are randomly assigned a player role with a probability of 1/2. After role assignment, there is the same amount of dictators and receivers. Then, dictators and receivers are randomly matched. Dictators make the same decision as in Baseline twice. For payoff determination, each decision is chosen with a probability of 1/2 and yields the payoffs for either player. This means that a dictator’s decision in one specific game determines her own payoff and the matched receiver’s payoff with a probability of 1/2. There is no intermediate feedback provided on the outcome of the first decision. I chose exactly the same DG decision twice in order to study a pure repetition effect. Choosing a different task would have been arbitrary.

Role Uncertainty (Role50): Role50 generates twice as many independent dictator observations relative to Baseline, using the same amount of subjects. Further, data can be obtained from all subjects in a session. First, all subjects determine the allocation as dictators. Then, they are randomly assigned their actual player roles with a probability of 1/2. Consequently, there are as many dictators as there are receivers. Dictators and receivers are then randomly matched. For payoff determination, the decisions of players who are still dictators are implemented. This implies that a subject’s decision determines her own payoff and another subject’s payoff with a probability of 1/2.

Probabilistic Decision Payment and Role Uncertainty (DecisionRole25): In contrast to Baseline, this variant generates twice as many observations and requires only half as many subjects. It also permits within-subject comparisons. This variant combines the features of Decision50 and Role50. First, all subjects determine the allocation in two consecutive DGs, as in Decision50. To determine the players’ payoffs, subjects are randomly assigned their actual player roles with a probability of 1/2. Consequently, there is the same number of dictators and receivers. Dictators and receivers are randomly matched. Then, one of the two decisions from each remaining dictator is chosen with a probability of 1/2 and paid out. The procedure implies that a subject’s decision in one of the two dictator games determines her own payoff and another subject’s payoff with a probability of 1/4.

3.2. Experimental procedures

General procedures. The experiment was conducted at the Cologne Laboratory for Economic Research (CLER), located at the University of Cologne. The lab has 32 cubicles, allowing me to include 16 dictator–receiver dyads in the standard DG. For each DG variant, the number of sessions and involved subjects depended on the variant features (see Table 1). I collected 32 dictator decisions in each variant. In total, 304 subjects (192 dictators) participated.6 Subjects were recruited via the online recruiting system ORSEE (Greiner, 2015) and participated only in one session each. After subjects had arrived at the laboratory, they drew a random code and were seated in separate opaque cubicles. Subjects were not allowed to communicate. In case of any questions, they had to raise their hands so that the experimenter could come and help. The experiment started after all subjects had read the instructions on paper, the experimenter had publicly read through the instructions, and questions regarding the structure of the experiment had been answered in private. The experiments were conducted on the computer, using the experimental platform z-Tree (Fischbacher, 2007), and lasted about one hour. After the main experimental task, subjects were asked to fill in a list of questionnaires with items on their understanding of the experiment, empirical and normative beliefs, social value orientation, personal values, Big Five personality traits, willingness to make a donation, and sociodemographic background. Concluding the session, subjects were compensated individually with a fixed amount of €4 for showing up, along with the amount that they had earned in the experiment.Appendix B in the supplementary material depicts the different stages of the experiment.

Specific Controls. Due to the nature of the included DG variants, random treatment assignment was not advisable within experimental sessions.7 Therefore, I took specific care of the following factors in order to keep the experimental conditions as constant as possible across DG variants and sessions: i) Schedule: The experimental sessions were scheduled on a Tuesday or Wednesday, between 9:00 a.m. and 12:15 p.m., during the students’ summer term. In the DG variants with two sessions, I placed one session on a Tuesday and the other one on a Wednesday in two different weeks (for organizational reasons, Decision50 took place only on a Tuesday; Pay50, which was added later, was played within one week). Further, I scheduled one session at 9 a.m. and one session at 11 a.m. Taken together, this ensured no bridging days, balanced weekdays and daytime, and a short time span for conducting the entire experiment.8ii) Experimenter: To avoid experimenter effects (e.g., Roth et al., 1991, Zizzo, 2010), the same experimenter conducted the experiment and applied exactly the same experimental protocol. Instructions were neutrally written, avoiding terms like “dictator,” “giving,” or “fair,” because loaded instructions may alter dictator behavior (e.g., Burnham et al., 2000). iii)Sex: I strictly balanced the sex of the participants, because women tend to participate more often than men in experiments (e.g., Cleave et al., 2013) and typically transfer more than men in dictator games (e.g., Eckel and Grossman, 1998). For this purpose, males and females were separately invited to the same session. iv) Age: My aim was to restrict the student sample to participants who were born between 1990 and 1999, yielding a typical age span for the current student cohort, because age might have a positive effect on generosity (e.g., Engel, 2011). v) Culture: To avoid variance in the subjects’ decisions due varying cultural backgrounds (e.g., Henrich et al., 2005, Chuah et al., 2007), I only invited subjects who indicated German as their mother tongue, which was used as an indicator of the subjects’ origin. vi) Experience: I restricted the subject pool to participants who had no previous experience with DG experiments. Moreover, I aimed to invite only subjects who had not participated more than seven times in other experiments before (however, at some stage, I also had to invite more experienced subjects in order to fill the experimental sessions; see Table 6). This procedure was chosen to enroll subjects who had some experience with the situation and procedures typically applied in laboratory experiments. On the other hand, extensive personal experiences may cause the subjects to play interactive games in the lab as if they have some repetition, and the experimenter may have little recourse to moderate this phenomenon (for a similar argument, see Levitt and List, 2007, Matthey and Regner, 2013, find that participation in previous experiments tends to increase the amount subjects allocate to themselves in dictator games). vii) Major: I also limited the share of business and economics students – the largest group in our university’s laboratory subject pool, and potentially familiar with the DG and less cooperative (e.g., Frank et al., 1993) – by initially inviting only students who indicated that they study other subjects.9

4.2. Correlation with complementary measures of prosociality

To further asses the validity of the different DG variants, I relate exposed dictator generosity to four complementary individual difference measures of prosociality: social value orientation (SVO); personal values; a naturally occurring decision situation on charitable giving, which creates a positive externality outside the laboratory (this approach is similar to Benz and Meier, 2008); and the personality factor agreeableness from the Big Five personality inventory across DG variants.

i) Social value orientation. In the incentivized SVO slider measure (Murphy and Ackermann, 2014), subjects repeatedly choose between different their own and others’ payoff allocations. The decisions resemble repeated DG decisions. From the respondents’ aggregated decisions, an SVO angle can be calculated. A higher angle indicates a greater concern for the welfare of others. I expected the angle to be positively correlated with dictator transfers.

ii) Personal values. In the non-incentivized Personal Values Questionnaire (Schwartz et al., 2012), subjects respond to questions on how similar they consider themselves to a described person. From their responses, the importance of ten basic values is derived for each individual (Schwartz, 1992). Among the basic values, I expect the values along the dimensions of self-transcendence (consisting of benevolence and universalism and mapping the ability to transcend one’s own interests for the sake of others) and the self-enhancement (consisting of power and achievement and mapping the importance of one’s will to pursue one’s own interests) to be positively (negatively) correlated with dictators’ generosity.12 It was important for me to include a non-incentivized measure of prosociality, because incentivized measures are potentially susceptible to spillover effects – that is subjects might try to balance their behavior across decisions (hedging or moral balancing effects, e.g., Merritt et al., 2010).

SVO and personal values are widely used in experimental economics and social psychology and have been shown to explain individual differences in DG giving (e.g., Cornelissen et al., 2011, Lönnqvist et al., 2013). These two measures were elicited after the main experimental task. The application of both measures was balanced in each experimental session: half of the subjects completed the SVO measure first, and half of the subjects started with the values questionnaire.

iii) Donation. At the end of the experiment, I gave the subjects the opportunity to make a donation out of their show-up fees. They were told that they could support a UNICEF malaria relief program in Kenya.13 Subjects could donate any amount between €0 and €4 (full fee). In the donation call, I explicitly conveyed that the donated money would actually be transferred to UNICEF and that the subjects could check the transfer receipt afterward (see Appendix F in the supplementary material for details). I expected the donations to be positively correlated with dictator transfers (Barr et al., 2010, Franzen and Pointner, 2013).

iv) Big Five personality. Subjects’ personality profiles were gathered after the SVO and values questionnaires using a 60-item personality measure (Konstabel et al., 2012) that captures the five personality factors neuroticism, extraversion, openness, agreeableness, and conscientiousness conceptualized in the five-factor Model (Costa and McCrae, 1992). Based on previous studies on the relationship between prosocial behavior and personality (e.g., Ben-Ner et al., 2008, Hilbig et al., 2014, Zhao and Smillie, 2015), I expected agreeableness – which describes a person as kind, sympathetic, cooperative, warm, and considerate – to be positively associated with generosity.

In the standard DG, generosity presented the expected significant correlations with social value orientation, self-transcendence values, self-enhancement values, the donation to charity, and the personality factor of agreeableness14. 4.3. Controls

As a last step, I ran additional controls. I look at potential time effects, the characteristics of the recruited subjects, and how they perceived the decision situation. I also check whether the different DG variants evoked different transfer standards. These can be derived from dictators’ beliefs about what other dictators will do and what the receivers expect them to do in the present situation.

Time effects. To estimate the influence of potential time effects, I ran two tobit regressions: I regressed dictator transfers by the day of the week (Tuesday or Wednesday; Tuesday  = 1) and times of day when sessions were conducted (9 a.m. or 11 a.m.; 9 a.m. = 1). Standard errors are clustered for subjects because of repeated decisions in Decision50 and RoleDecision25. Coefficients are not significant (day of the week: β = −0.365, p = 0.456; time of the day: β = −0.191, p = 0.696).

I managed to select rather homogeneous subject samples into the different DG variants. Sex (50% females) and age (average = 23.30 years) of the dictators are well-balanced. Further, a high percentage (89.06%) of the subjects indicated German as their mother tongue. Subjects had moderate experience with previous experiments (on average, 5.64 participations). Past DG experience was ruled out by the recruiting software. A share of 69.27% of the subjects majored in a discipline other than business or economics. 5. Discussion

As I have shown, the probability of how much a dictator’s decision can actually convert into both players payoffs has no robust impact on dictator transfers. Further, neither the standard economic model, the invariance hypothesis, nor the Fehr-Schmidt model can adequately explain the behavioral patterns observed. I will therefore examine how the specific formsof randomization affect behavior in order to assess the suitability of each DG variant as a feasible alternative to the standard DG.

First of all, in the standard DG, individual generosity shows the expected associations with dictators’ social value orientation, personal values, inclination to make a donation, and personality (cf. Cornelissen et al., 2011, Lönnqvist et al., 2013, Benz and Meier, 2008, Hilbig et al., 2014). In this respect, Baseline provides the most convincing results such that the experimenters can be assured that they actually measure what they intend to measure. In Baseline, average transfers (16.9%) were slightly lower compared to what Engel (2011) reports on average generosity in student samples (24.7%). This might be an artifact of the methodological heterogeneity in the analyzed DGs or of the University of Cologne’s subject pool (for a comparable result, see Camerer, 2003).

Beyond a valid baseline, two main results emerge from my data: First, I find no evidence that involving fewer receivers than dictators changes dictator behavior and correlations with alternative measures of prosociality significantly. If there are plausible arguments, unbalanced matching may, therefore, represent a feasible cost-saving alternative for conducting DG studies under resource constraints (e.g., Houser and Schunk, 2009; see also Walkowitz, 2019, for a recent study with prison inmates). With regard to transparency about the applied matching procedure, my results suggest that unbalanced matching can be made transparent to avoid notions of deception. A discussion of the question whether not making it transparent is actually deceptive is beyond the scope of this paper. I also want to emphasize that Receiver50 is only one possible variant for testing the effects of involving fewer receivers. In principle, only two receivers are needed to meet the requirement of a “truly random” matching of dictators and receivers. This variant would be the most efficient test for studying the impact of involving fewer receivers than dictators. I decided to involve twice as many dictators than receivers to maintain comparable incentives across DG variants.

My second main result relates to the impact of role uncertainty. In both DG variants with role uncertainty, dictators were more generous compared to the standard DG.15 Moreover, subjects’ transfers in Role50 and DecisionRole25 did not yield the expected correlations with complementary individual difference measures of prosociality. The characterization of the transfer distributions and my correlational analysis convey that the detected transfer differences are not caused by a plain upward shift in subjects’ transfer level. This calls for caution in interpreting absolute values, because habitually more generous participants cannot be properly singled out. Inflated generosity may become an issue when the researchers look for the true motives behind prosocial behavior. When, for example, the DG adds a control for altruistic preferences to other experimental data, associations between the experimental tasks might be biased. This is of specific interest, because in this case experimenters typically want to elicit a data point from all participants. A DG with role uncertainty offers a very convenient way of doing so, as the receivers are initially not needed. My results confirm and extend the findings of Iriberri and Rey-Biel (2011) and recently also Mesa-Vázquez et al. (2019), who warn urgently against the use of game designs with role uncertainty.

Contrary to the other variants, in Role50 and DecisionRole25, the behavior of other subjects is potentially payoff-relevant for a subject. Although not strategically relevant, there is an inter-player dependence; players may be forced to put themselves into the shoes of another player and into either player role. Dictators in Role50 and DecisionRole25 have higher empirical beliefs concerning the transfers of the other dictators relative to Baseline; empirical expectations and transfers are also highly correlated. Therefore, transfers might reflect a notion of empathy or indirect reciprocity toward other players who might affect a subject’s payoff (Batson et al., 1988). The results from my correlational analysis above, however, speak against this last interpretation. Alternatively, higher transfers might entail an element of fearful or wishful thinking. Because subjects face the threat of ending up as receivers, they might construct the game differently. They evaluate the game outcomes from the receiver’s perspective as well and also express this in their beliefs. Driven by the fear of being exploited, they might see their decision and those of their randomly matched partners as contributions to a “super game,” which resembles a repeated public goods game with an efficiency factor of one (in expectations). In such a game, both players can benefit if they cooperate. They can make a seemingly generous transfer and hope to be matched with an similarly minded dictator in case they are assigned the receiver role. Testing and disentangling these alternative explanations, e.g., by including individual difference measures which grasp related constructs, is an interesting path for future work.

Next to these main results, the findings from Pay50 and Decision50 provide an illustrative case for the benefits of not only considering dictators’ average transfers, but also the correlations with complementary measures of prosociality when testing the validity of DG variants. On the one hand, I find that average dictator behavior in both Pay50 and Decision50 does not differ significantly from the standard DG. However, I detect no consistent correlations of individual generosity and complementary measures of prosociality. Therefore, based on my data, these variants’ application by researchers should be considered carefully.16 If researchers are interested in absolute average levels of generosity in the subject pool, paying only some subjects or decisions might be feasible. Yet, when generosity is tested and quantified against other competing motives, the results might be misleading. Moreover, if the researchers are interested in underlying motivations, or when the DG adds an individual control for altruistic preferences to other experimental data, associations between the exposed behaviors might be biased.

The results from Decision50, together with the findings of DecisionRole25, also add to the discussion on the applicability of the strategy method and how to pay subjects when a series of choices is elicited. Previous works suggest that using the strategy method and paying for one randomly chosen decision is appropriate (e.g., Brandts and Charness, 2000, Engel, 2011, Laury, 2005, Azrieli et al., 2018). The data from my experiment suggest, again, that researchers need to be careful when combining the elicitation of multiple similarly structured decisions with random decision payment. This is particularly important when there is role uncertainty involved (e.g., Andreoni and Miller, 2002, Andreoni and Vesterlund, 2001, Ashraf and Bohnet, 2006, Blanco et al., 2011, Castillo and Cross, 2008, Charness and Rabin, 2002, Engelmann and Strobel, 2004). As shown above, the DG variants Decision50 (which entails two similar decisions and random decision payment), Role50 (with role uncertainty), and DecisionRole25 (which tests a methodological combination) might all not be feasible alternatives to the standard DG due to the missing correlations with complementary individual difference measures of prosociality or inflated generosity. Some studies (e.g., Engelmann and Strobel, 2004) suggest that their results are robust concerning role uncertainty. But, as Iriberri and Rey-Biel (2011) show, Engelmann and Strobel (2004), for example, did not replicate all of their relevant decision problems with role certainty. However, my data also show that individual SVO angles are highly positively correlated with dictator generosity in almost all DG variants and with the other social preference indicators although the applied standard measure for social value orientation also introduces uncertainty about the decision-maker’s actual role and random payment of decisions. Based on my data, my conjecture is that if experimental subjects make longer sequences of decisions under conditions of role uncertainty, the distorting effect of role uncertainty will become smaller.

A noteworthy limitation of my study relates to sample size. Each of the investigated DG variants included 32 usable dictator decisions. Due to the methodological heterogeneity in previous studies and the resulting limited comparability, it was not possible to derive reasonable effect sizes for the comparison of each DG variant with the original version of the DG. Ex ante power analysis showed that n = 32 subjects were sufficient for each DG variant to have a 80 % chance of detecting a medium-sized to large effect of d = 0.72 in transfers (see Cohen, 2013). Economically, such an effect can be considered small, because it implies only a change of 7.2 % relative to pie size. Nevertheless, I must acknowledge that my sample size is too small to rule out the possibility of a true small- to medium-sized effect (d = 0.2 to 0.5) in those DG variants that yielded non-significant results compared to the baseline game (i.e., Receiver50, Pay50, and Decision50). Without further assumptions, I would have needed 128 to 394 usable dictator decisions for each DG variant – about 4 to 12 times the amount that I actually included – to have 80 % power to detect such effects. Assuming that the detected standard deviations in Receiver50, Pay50, and Decision50 represent the populations’ true standard deviations and α = 0.05 and β = 0.2, the ex post calculated minimal detectable effects would be equal to 1.43, 1.33, and 1.34/1.33, respectively. These figures indicate that my study was underpowered. Based on the above assumptions, it would have been necessary to get between 227 and 263 usable dictator decisions for each DG to detect a medium-sized effect, and between 1413 and 1637 decisions to detect a small effect. Due to the high demands on the characteristics of the recruited subjects in this study and given that it was already very challenging to reach the aspired number of subjects, it was not possible to obtain such high numbers of subjects. As described above, based on my data, I came to the conclusion that Pay50 and Decision50 should be handled cautiously anyway, because of the missing correlations with complementary measures of prosociality. In contrast, correlational results for Receiver50 were very similar to those found in Baseline. This implies that if a true generosity difference were present, this would represent a shift in subjects’ transfer levels and habitually generous participants could be properly singled out. Regarding inflated transfers in Role50 and DecisionRole25, detected effect sizes and the confirmation of previous findings (e.g., Iriberri and Rey-Biel, 2011) suggest the existence of true effects.

From an applied perspective, an interesting follow-up study could investigate whether role uncertainty would have an equally pronounced effect in a DG variant similar to Role50 but where all decisions are paid out: experimental subjects could act as dictators and receivers simultaneously and be paid according to their own decisions as dictators, but also receive a transfer from another dictator (cf., e.g., Andreoni and Miller, 2002, Fisman et al., 2007).17 Contrary to Role50, in this game variant, each experimental subject would have partial control over her own earnings. This could mitigate the biasing effect of the above mentioned fear of ending up as a receiver. Contrary to the standard DG, giving in this suggested game variant could be interpreted as pure altruism, because in the initial situation, fairness considerations should not play a role. On the other hand, such a design might trigger strategic considerations that are normally not present in the DG, and experimenters would need to control for dictator beliefs. Subjects could freeride, for example, and underlying motives could become distorted. If such a design would provide similarly valid results as the Baseline DG, this would be a solution for experiments that use the DG as a post-experimental measure of generosity.

As a final remark, my attempt underlines the need for a cohesive study to better understand the impact of the different randomization procedures creating probabilistic incentives. Further evidence is needed to support my findings, as well. For future research, I therefore also suggest focusing on other commonly used and more complex games. Even in a simple setup without strategic interaction, I detected significant influences of randomization-based probabilistic incentives. Similar effects might occur and blur the link between preferences and behavior when the players are strategically connected (e.g., Blanco et al., 2011, Burks et al., 2003).
