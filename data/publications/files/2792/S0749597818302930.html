Abstract

Researchers and practitioners in the area of charitable giving have long lamented the tendency to offer greater aid to one person who is suffering rather than to a large group with the same needs. Demonstrations of such compassion fade are common in the literature, although different explanations for these findings exist. To reconcile both past theory and empirical research, we utilized a dual concern framing (De Dreu, 2006; Pruitt & Rubin, 1985) in conducting a meta-analysis of 41 studies (95 independent samples; 13,259 total sample size) on compassion fade. Results suggest that victim group size negatively affects both helping intent and helping behavior, as well as our proposed mediating mechanisms of anticipated positive affect (self-oriented motivation) and perceived impact (hybrid other-/self-oriented motivation). However, significant effects were not found for empathetic concern (other-oriented motivation). Results also showed that the indirect effects of victim group size on helping are stronger through anticipated positive affect and perceived impact than through empathetic concern. Further, as indicated by supplemental analyses, anticipated positive affect and perceived impact likely operate as predictors of empathetic concern in a serial mediation process through which victim groups size affects helping. Finally, we examined calamity scope (number of victims) and event features (certainty, chronicity, and threat severity) as moderators of the observed relationships between victim group size and helping. Theoretical implications and directions for future compassion fade research emanating from these findings are discussed.

1. Introduction

â€œIf I look at the mass, I will never act. If I look at the one, I willâ€ â€“Mother Teresa

The amount of charitable donations made in the U.S. each year is staggering, reaching approximately $390 billion in 2016 alone, with the number increasing to roughly $410â€¯billion in 2017 (Giving USA, 2018). In considering these estimates, it is intuitive to assume that generosity would be allocated in a manner that benefits the most people in need. However, empirical data suggests this may not be true. In fact, research often indicates that donations are not positively related to the number of individuals in need; instead, a negative relationship emerges, wherein helping diminishes as the number of victims increases (Fetherstonhaugh, Slovic, Johnson, & Friedrich, 1997). As a recent illustration, in the six months following the 2014 Ebola outbreak in West Africa that killed more than 3400 people, the American Red Cross received only $100,000 in individual donations dedicated to fighting the virus (Hicken, 2014). Yet, in a one-month period in 2015, over $1.2â€¯million was raised in individual donations for a child from New York featured in a crowdfunding campaign so that he could visit Harvard (DiGiammarino, 2015).

The above example illustrates compassion fade1â€”a decrease in helping or compassionate intent and behavior as the number of people in need increases (Markowitz et al., 2013, Slovic, 2007). Empirical examples of compassion fade abound (c.f., Erlandsson et al., 2014, Ritov and Baron, 1990), dating to seminal work by Schelling (1968) who provided an initial account of why individuals opt to help one versus many. Of course, these instances of compassion fade defy the basic principles underlying economic valuations of risk (Hammitt and Treich, 2007, Viscusi and Aldy, 2003): funds donated to assist thousands of people dying or in need should exponentially trump aid to one person. Thus, a better understanding of why and when compassion fade occurs represents an important endeavor, given that it represents a case in which moral behavior departs from expected moral principles. In the context of prosocial behavior and charitable giving, this phenomenon is particularly in need of further assimilation and exploration because, as Ariely (2008) laments, â€œ[if compassion fade truly occurs] the attention we will pay to different tragedies around us will not be based on their objective level of tragedy but instead on the way in which they invoke emotions in us. This also means that sometimes the tragedy of one person can overshadow the tragedy of millions.â€

Although the occurrence of compassion fade is well-documented, what is less clear are the underlying psychological mechanismsâ€”or motivationsâ€”for why compassion fade occurs (Cameron & Payne, 2011). Specifically, in the literature on prosocial behavior, motivations for helping others are often categorized as other-interested (i.e., altruistic) or self-interested (i.e., egoistic), with these motivations contrasted against one another. For example, research on other-interested motivations, such as the empathy-altruism hypothesis (e.g., Batson et al., 1991), suggests that empathetic concern and compassion for victims give rise to other-interested motivations focused on improving the welfare of the person in need. However, other models assert that people have an innate tendency to weigh their self-interests when deciding to help (Cialdini, Baumann, & Kenrick, 1981), in that the self-interested observer is likely to offer help only when there is some personal benefit to be gained. Past research also demonstrates that people can be driven by both other- and self-interested motivations (De Dreu & Nauta, 2009), with these hybrid motivations sharing the guiding principle of attaining joint goals in the way of improving the welfare of another person (i.e., other-interested motivations) and improving oneâ€™s own situation (i.e., self-interested motivations). Thus, multiple mechanisms may underlie why compassion fade occurs when people are faced with helping.

Unfortunately, to our knowledge, relatively few studies on compassion fade investigate multiple mechanisms simultaneously (for an exception, see: Erlandsson et al., 2014). Further, the underlying explanatory processes purported to explain why compassion fade occurs have neither been quantitatively accumulated, nor empirically compared. This is an important omission and opportunity for investigation given that philanthropic acts are often posited to occur as the result of many different motivations operating simultaneously (Bekkers & Wiepking, 2011). Moreover, as recently suggested by Cameron (2017), questions remain regarding which motivations might serve as the central antecedent to compassion fade. In sum, if motivations differentially play a role in compassion fade, studies focusing on only one type of motivationâ€”or no motivational mechanisms at allâ€”may yield a narrow and fragmented understanding of this phenomenon.

Toward this end, the purpose of the current research is to conduct a meta-analysis on compassion fade, synthesizing 41 studies (95 independent samples; 13,259 total sample size) that span almost two decades. Consistent with predictions from two of the primary perspectives on compassion fade (Cameron and Payne, 2011, Slovic, 2007), we posit that victim group size decreases helping intent and behaviors of potential donors. Guided by dual concern theory (De Dreu, 2006; Meglino & Korsgaard, 2004; Pruitt & Rubin, 1985), we unify traditional explanations for compassion fade with prevailing motivations for helping espoused in the literature (Crocker et al., 2017, Penner et al., 2005) to position other-oriented motivation (i.e., empathetic concern), self-oriented motivation (i.e., anticipated positive affect), and hybrid other-/self-oriented motivation (i.e., perceived impact) as underlying mediating mechanisms. Our conceptual model is shown in Fig. 1.

Our research makes three key contributions. First, by quantitatively summarizing available findings on compassion fade, we build consensus regarding the degree to which victim group size negatively relates to helping intent and behaviors. In doing so, we disentangle effects for intent versus behaviors and elucidate the strength of these relationships in relation to victim group size. Second, our focus on other-, self-, and hybrid other-/self-motivations derived from dual concern theory (e.g., Korsgaard, Meglino, & Lester, 1996) explicates the process of compassion fade, expanding prior theorizing that has presented motivated accounts to explain differentially helping single victims versus groups (e.g., Cameron & Payne, 2011). Indeed, social behavior is often categorized as occurring on a spectrum ranging from other- to self-driven (Crocker et al., 2017), and the literature on philanthropy commonly argues for two main motives to offer help that operate in isolation and/or together: pure altruism (other-oriented goals) and impure altruism (self-oriented goals). In adopting this motivational view, we integrate theories on psychological biases and limitations in affective information processing (Hamilton & Sherman, 1996) with those tied to the emotion regulation underpinnings of compassion fade (Cameron and Payne, 2011, Erlandsson et al., 2015) to develop a theory-based framework that details how various motivational mechanisms operate in unison to explain the relationship between victim group size and helping. By examining multiple mechanisms, we also advance knowledge regarding the relative magnitude by which different processes influence the effect of victim group size on helping. Such an examination addresses a sizable theoretical gap, given that compassion fade â€œpresents a psychological puzzle because the mechanisms underlying the effect are not fully understoodâ€ (Cameron & Payne, 2011, p. 2).

Finally, because the decision to help is often viewed as a context-specific occurrence, whereby attributions are made about the situation facing victims that foster or hinder help-giving (Batson and Shaw, 1991, Weiner, 1980), we extend prior work on event-contingent characteristics of ethical decision-making (Jones, 1991) and compassion fade by meta-analytically examining situational boundary conditions that may enable or undermine the tendency for people to help. Specifically, because group size often underscores the scope of the hardship facing victims and is a key contingency for helping versus not helping (Schwartz & Clausen, 1970), we examine the size of the group of victims compared to a single victim as a moderating factor. Moreover, the way in which inferences are made about the circumstances facing victims can account for the moral intensity of the situation (Jones, 1991) and decisions to help (Jenni and Loewenstein, 1997, Weiner, 2000). Because such event features might impact the decision to help one versus many people in need, we argue that characteristics of the event constitute important factors the victims bring to bear on the situation that warrant further systematic exploration in order to advance future research. Drawing from literature highlighting that attributions associated with situational characteristics can necessitate the decision to help or not help (Weiner, 1980, Weiner, 2000), we explore three event features common in helping scenariosâ€”certainty, chronicity, and threat severityâ€”as moderators of the relationship between victim group size and helping. Thus, our research addresses continuing calls (i.e., Ein-Gar and Levontin, 2013, Jenni and Loewenstein, 1997) to better understand how boundary conditions associated with victims in need may account for the extent to which victim group size decreases helping intent and/or behavior.

2. Theory and hypotheses

2.1. The relationship between victim group size and helping

Two theoretical perspectives help explain compassion fade. One prominent view is that there are numeracy limitations and biases in the basic affective processing system underlying helping (Hamilton and Sherman, 1996, Slovic, 2007), which we term the affective bias perspective. According to this view, a single victim is depicted with more detailed information than groups, contributing to greater vividness in imagery and eliciting stronger emotional reactions (Hamilton & Sherman, 1996; for a summary of this theoretical perspective, see: Cameron and Payne, 2011, Cameron, 2017). In comparison, multiple victims constrain oneâ€™s capacity for attention and imagery, resulting in blurred and fragmented representations of the victims and weakened affective responses (Dickert and Slovic, 2009, Kogut and Ritov, 2005a). This explanation finds support in other theoretical frameworks. For example, Chaiken (1980) heuristic-systematic model suggests that individuals are more emotionally and mentally involved when processing information about specific targets (i.e., one victim) versus abstract targets (i.e., multiple victims) due to the weakened emotional and cognitive involvement associated with using heuristics and peripheral cues to judge groups rather than individuals. Similarly, Greene and Haidt (2002) suggest that overly affective reactions (i.e., the magnitude of emotions elicited for one victim versus many) often prevent people from making moral judgements that maximally benefit others as a whole. Because of the increased vividness and imagery, it is possible that single victims are viewed as more proximally close and relatable than multiple victims, fostering more emotional affiliation (Balcetis & Dunning, 2010). As elucidated by Cameron (2017), these affective processing limitations are intractable and fixed constraints that bias all people in any prosocial situation, which suggests practical limitations on the likelihood of success in institutional efforts to foster large-scale generosity and helping.

An alternative explanation is the motivated choice perspective, which contends that people regulate their emotions to maximize personal goals while minimizing potential costs that may seem overwhelming, such as the prospect of helping with mass suffering (Cameron and Payne, 2011, Cameron et al., 2016). As detailed in Zaki (2014) motivated reasons to approach or avoid empathy, this motivated choice perspective of Cameron and colleagues is predicated on the idea that potential donors avoid situations that are too potentially costly in the way of emotional burden. That is, a person might expect helping multiple victims to be costlier and less efficacious (i.e., â€˜a drop in the bucketâ€™) compared to the benefits accrued from focusing on a single victim (Cameron, 2017). Indeed, this notion aligns with the cost-reward model of helping (Dovidio, Piliavin, Gaertner, Schroeder, & Clark, 1991) and past work on empathy avoidance (Shaw, Batson, & Todd, 1994), which demonstrate that when helping is foreseen as too costly, potential donors regulate their emotions to avoid feelings that will necessitate helping. Previous work by Cameron and Payne (2011) has generally supported this perspective, linking compassion fade to the strategically downward regulation of oneâ€™s emotions in an attempt to dull affect in the face of mass suffering (e.g., Hoffman, 2000, Slovic, 2007). Importantly, removing such forms of emotion regulation have been found to diminish compassion fade (Cameron & Payne, 2011), suggesting unique opportunities for motivation-based interventions. These practical implications of the motivated choice view are not as actionable under the affective bias perspective, which contends that such interventions are largely futile.

The aforementioned explanations may differ in their rationale as to why compassion fade occurs, a point we address in the sections that follow. Yet, despite the differing views on the mechanisms underlying the effects, the basic premises upon which arguments for compassion fade are based remain the same: victim group size should lessen, rather than increase, helping.

Hypothesis 1:

Victim group size is negatively related to helping (intent and behavior).

2.2. The mediating roles of other- and self-oriented motivations

As noted above, past research suggests that the relationship between victim group size and helping may occur through multiple pathways (Cameron & Payne, 2011). Yet, few studies have systematically examined multiple mechanisms. We address this limitation by providing a framework derived from dual concern theory (De Dreu, 2006, Meglino and Korsgaard, 2006, Pruitt and Rubin, 1985) that summarizes three mechanisms for why people engage in helping in the context of victim group size: other-oriented motivation, self-oriented motivation, and hybrid other-/self-oriented motivation. Examining compassion fade through such a motivational lens focusing on three related yet distinct theoretically-grounded mediating mechanisms helps unify extant perspectives on compassion fade (i.e., Cameron and Payne, 2011, Slovic, 2007).

In terms of other-oriented motivation, a substantial body of work suggests empathetic concern inspires donors to help (Lowenstein & Small, 2007). Empathetic concern is â€œelicited by and congruent with the perceived welfare of someone in needâ€ (Batson, 2011, p. 11) and includes feelings of sympathy, empathy, and concern for the victim(s).2 Notably, empathetic concern reflects selfless (i.e., other-oriented) concern that arouses altruistic motivation aimed at improving the welfare or situation of others as opposed to helping oneself (Batson et al., 1991). This aligns with Meglino and Korsgaard (2006) conceptualization of â€œother orientation,â€ which encompasses situations where people pursue other-oriented goals without careful weighting of personal costs. In other words, empathetic concern represents an other-oriented motivation whereby people focus on helping others without considering the personal costs of such actions. Viewing empathetic concern as an other-oriented motive is consistent with the public goods model, which suggests that donors are primarily motivated by what their gifts accomplish for society (Bergstrom et al., 1986, Roberts, 1984, Warr, 1982).

In linking empathetic concern with helping, it has been argued that helpers display greater empathetic concern toward one victim because one individual elicits stronger affective processing than a group that may be viewed more abstractly (Kogut & Ritov, 2005a). As Slovic (2007) explains, the emotion associated with valuing someone elseâ€™s life that accompanies moral judgements (i.e., helping a victim) is crude and insensitive to numerical gradation. This echoes the aforementioned affective bias perspective (i.e., Slovic, 2007) by suggesting there are biases and limitations to interpreting affective cues when faced with a group of victims, resulting in higher empathetic concern for a single victim. Viewing empathetic concern as an other-oriented motivation also incorporates motivated choice explanations for compassion fade. Through this lens, empathic concern is a moral, other-directed emotion associated with altruistic actions in response to a moral choice (i.e., helping; Eisenberg, 1986, Tangney et al., 2007). However, moral choices are unpleasant events associated with psychological threats that individuals try to repress and minimize, which limits their ability to recognize the magnitude of moral issues (Jones, 1991). This parallels Cameron and Payne (2011) contention that mass suffering (compared to the suffering of one victim) is emotionally exhausting, and people try to avoid such feelings. Thus, compassion fade may occur through an other-oriented motivation pathway whereby the negative relationship between victim group size and helping is mediated by empathetic concernâ€”a strategic coping mechanism to defend against unwanted exhaustion posed by the increasing number of victims. This rationale leads to our next hypotheses:

Hypothesis 2a

Victim group size is negatively related to empathetic concern.

Hypothesis 2b

The negative relationship between victim group size and helping is mediated by empathetic concern.

Beyond empathetic concern, the motivation to help a victim may be derived from self-oriented considerations regarding how helping benefits the individual helper. Numerous theories in the social and organization sciences assume that individuals act in accordance with their self-interests, even when making decisions about who or when to help (Meglino & Korsgaard, 2004). For example, Meglino and Korsgaard (2006) position self-oriented motivations as deriving from the pursuit of self-serving goals that maximize oneâ€™s personal (not othersâ€™) outcomes. Further, building from the empathetic-joy hypothesis, a critical goal of human behavior is that individuals want to gain positive affective experiences (Batson et al., 1991, Diener, 2000). Thus, we also consider anticipated positive affect as a key self-oriented motivation for helping. Anticipated positive affect reflects projected self-focused feelings about how the helper will feel if s/he renders aid (VÃ¤stfjÃ¤ll, Slovic, Mayorga, & Peters, 2014).3 Indeed, a desired result of helping is increased subjective well-being (Dunn, Aknin, & Norton, 2008), which supports Andreoni (1990) â€˜warm glowâ€™ view of philanthropy. According to Andreoni (1990), donors give their time and money not because it helps those in need, but because of the personal benefits realized from giving. Similarly, Erlandsson, Jungstrand, and VÃ¤stfjÃ¤ll (2016) focus on anticipated warm glow as a predictor of helping: when faced with the chance to help, most people imagine how they would feel rather than how they currently feel if they help. Thus, anticipated positive affect can be viewed a positively-valenced equivalent to empathetic concern (Erlandsson et al., 2016); however, the key difference is that anticipated positive affect is a self-directed reaction (rather than other-directed emotion) that results from planning to act in a way to make anotherâ€™s situation better (Batson et al., 1991, Smith et al., 1989).

Importantly, this sense of anticipated positive affect adds meaning to the information encoded about victims, and is processed quickly and rapidly. In terms described by dual-process theories of social perception and cognition (Kahneman, 2003, Slovic, 2007), affective responses are System 1 responses that occur intuitively and automatically compared to System 2 responses that are slower and more deliberate. Because anticipated positive affect is primarily self-oriented and a dominate driver of helping (Crocker et al., 2017), this suggests that anticipated positive affect fits with System 1 processing. Unfortunately, previous research has shown that System 1 processing is limited in its capacity to deal with increasing quantities (Fetherstonhaugh et al., 1997, Slovic and VÃ¤stfjÃ¤ll, 2010). Further, it is often biased by informational factors such as attention, vividness, and social proximity (Lowenstein and Small, 2007, Slovic, 2007), all factors that favor a stronger affective response to single victims over multiple victims. Recent research using physiological indicators has also verified decreased positive affect with increased number of victims (VÃ¤stfjÃ¤ll et al., 2014). Again, this aligns with an affective bias explanation for why compassion fade occurs, but through the lens of self-oriented motivations.

The aforementioned tenets derived from the motivated choice perspective also apply to anticipated positive affect in linking victim group size with helping. In line with this view, we argue that while potential helpers will anticipate feeling more positive affect as the number of victims increases, this will create concerns about the costs of helping (Cameron & Payne, 2011). Potential helpers generally have limited personal experiences of having gratitude expressed to them by a group of victims, making positive affect derived from helping groups more difficult to envision (Logan, 1988). This difficulty in envisioning might be interpreted as an uncertainty individuals seek to avoid. Thus, people are more likely to regulate anticipated positive affect in situations with multiple victims compared to a single victim because the benefits associated with such large magnitude anticipated positive affect are potentially overwhelming. We propose:

Hypothesis 3a

Victim group size is negatively related to anticipated positive affect.

Hypothesis 3b

The negative relationship between victim group size and helping is mediated by anticipated positive affect.

Finally, according to Duncan (2004) economist theory, the third motivation that drives philanthropy is the desire to â€œmake a differenceâ€â€”what we denote as perceived impact. Consistent with Lowenstein and Small (2007), perceived impact is a subjective assessment of the extent to which aid will be impactful for the victim(s). People are more motivated to help when they believe the benefits of helping outweigh the costs (Erlandsson et al., 2014). For example, if a person is asked to donate $100, his/her motivation to help will likely depend, at least in part, on how much of an impact s/he believes the money will have. It is reasonable for a potential donor to conclude that $100 will help one victim more than it would 1000 victims. As Gneezy, Keenan, and Gneezy (2014) suggest, helpers want their donations put to â€˜goodâ€™ use, as when money goes directly to the victim(s) rather than allocated to overhead costs of a charity.

Following important distinctions made by Meglino and Korsgaard (2006) and De Dreau (2006) in terms of how self-interested goals (versus other-interested goals) and rationality are often distinct, we treat perceived impact as a hybrid of other- and self-oriented motivations. De Dreu (2006) foundational tenet based on dual concern theory (Pruitt & Rubin, 1985) posits that what often underlies prosocial motivation is a dual focus on both the outcomes for others and the potential donorâ€™s own interests; that is, other-oriented and self-oriented motivations can co-exist. This is consistent with Meglino and Korsgaard (2006) conceptualization of â€œcollective rationality,â€ wherein personal goals (i.e., maximizing oneâ€™s cost-to-benefit ratio of how to best utilize time, effort, and money in helping) comingle with other-oriented goals (i.e., purely altruistic acts). Perceived impact mirrors this joint optimization because it reflect that donor responsiveness is motivated by the rational understanding of recognitionâ€”internal or externalâ€”that is received by maximizing oneâ€™s cost-to-benefit ratio while simultaneously maintaining a salient focus on helping a victim.

Relating victim group size to perceived impact, research suggests there is less willingness to help as the victimâ€™s reference group increases in size due to perceived minimized influence and difficulty helping multiple beneficiaries (Baron, 1997, Sharma and Morwitz, 2016). That is, potential donors are more responsive to a single victim because rendering aid is viewed as a more tangible and efficient use of helping than helping a group (Duncan, 2004). In line with the affective bias perspective, VÃ¤stfjÃ¤ll, Slovic, and Mayorga (2015) suggest that information about numerous victims overwhelms donors and creates a state of pseudo-inefficacy in terms of the benefit of helping. Our assertion that hybrid other- and self-oriented motivations focused on costs and benefits of helping underlies perceived impact also fits with Cameron and Payne (2011) contention that people help only when compelled to do so in situations that are not costly. In other words, people are more likely to help when they feel that their efforts are maximized for an individual target (i.e., high perceived impact) rather than disbursed across many targets (i.e., low perceived impact). Thus, perceived impact may play a role in enhancing helping for an individual, while an increased number of victims will reduce the perceived impact and foster donorsâ€™ likelihood of rationalizing not helping (i.e., redefining the situation) to reduce remorse (Cameron and Payne, 2011, Dovidio et al., 1991).

Victim group size is negatively related to perceived impact.

Hypothesis 4b

The negative relationship between victim group size and helping is mediated by perceived impact.

Given our focus on simultaneous other-, self-, and hybrid other-/self-oriented motivations for helping, it is necessary to consider if certain motivational pathways will be stronger than others. Specifically, it has long been suggested that potential donors help others primarily out of benefits tied to instrumentality and personal goal fulfillment (Meglino & Korsgaard, 2006). That is, a potential donor is likely to help others only to the degree that it helps him/herself (Batson, 1990). Thus, the idea of pure altruism (i.e., other-oriented motivation) is assumed to be unrealistic orâ€”at a minimumâ€”less compelling (e.g., Cialdini et al., 1981). Further, previous empirical research suggests that self-oriented motivations can supplant other-oriented motivations in helping situations (Batson, Oâ€™Quinn, Fultz, Vanderplas, & Isen, 1983). Consistent with this notion, advertising research has also shown that self-oriented (hedonic) appeals evoke more imagery and detailed attention among audience members than other-oriented (altruistic) appeals (Keller and Block, 1997, MacInnis and Price, 1987). This suggests that motivations with self-oriented or hybrid aspects (anticipated positive affect, perceived impact) are more likely to be prompted by a single victim than by a group of victims, and likely play a stronger role than other-oriented motivations (empathetic concern) when it comes to helping others in need of aid.

Indeed, because empathetic concern involves an emotionally exhausting experience, potential donors may try to regulate or prevent this response (Cameron and Payne, 2011, Lazarus and Folkman, 1984), suggesting an upper boundary on the level of empathetic concern a donor can feel. Shaw et al. (1994) argue that people avoid feelings such as empathetic concern that compel them to help, and work on prosocial helping has shown that other-oriented emotions are more difficult to access than self-oriented affective experiences (Batson, 1990, Sabbagh and Taylor, 2000). Moreover, cost-benefit justifications for the behavior (such as perceived impact) may provide more tangible reasons to refrain from helping groups than empathetic concern. In line with this idea, Erlandsson et al. (2014) demonstrated that perceived impact had a stronger impact on helping than did empathetic concern (sympathy). Combined, we posit that victim group size is more likely to trigger self-oriented and hybrid motivations compared to other-oriented motivations, and demonstrate stronger indirect effects on helping via these mechanisms.

Hypothesis 5:

The effects of victim group size on (a) anticipated positive affect and (b) perceived impact are stronger than the effect on empathetic concern.

Hypothesis 6:

The mediated effect of victim group size on helping is stronger through (a) anticipated positive affect and (b) perceived impact than through empathetic concern.

2.3. Moderator relationships

Studies on compassion fade often imply a numerically-based simplicity in the distinction between whether people help single victims versus groups. However, the literature on behavioral ethics acknowledges that such simplicity ignores the important role of contextual characteristics in influencing peopleâ€™s decisions to respond to ethical issues. For example, Jones (1991) theorizes that any ethical issue is comprised of situational characteristics that reflect the degree of moral intensity involved and will impact the decision to act. Similarly, Weiner, 1980, Weiner, 2000 contends that thoughts, feelings, and behaviors focused on helping others are shaped by attributional aspects that vary by context. Thus, we consider situational characteristics that might serve as boundary conditions to observed effects of victim group size on helping. Specifically, we examine calamity scope (i.e., number of victims in the larger comparison group) and event features (i.e., certainty, chronicity, and threat severity) as moderators of the relationship between victim group size and helping intent and behavior.

2.4. Calamity scope: comparative number of victims

Studies on compassion fade devote little attention to how the size of the comparative group of victims may play a role in determining helping. Previous research suggests that as the number of victims suffering increases, at some point the obvious scope of need portrayed becomes too difficult for donors to ignore without incurring substantial psychological costs (LatanÃ© & Nida, 1981). Similarly, research on bystander intervention (Fischer et al., 2011) has shown that if the need for help is clear and unambiguousâ€”as is arguably the case when a large number of victims are at risk or affectedâ€”the negative effect of victim group size on helping decreases. Research has also found that describing a collective of victims as a unified group can diminish compassion fade (Smith, Faro, & Burson, 2013). In describing this occurrence, Cameron (2017) uses a motivated choice perspective to argue that psychological costs associated with helping a collective might be switched off at the point where the group is viewed as cohesive rather than merely a set of a few disconnected individuals. Larger groups of comparative victims may also mitigate the pitfalls espoused in the affective bias perspective, as the total magnitude of harmful consequences (i.e., large groups of victims) has been discussed as a characteristic of ethical issues that can compel decision makers to employ analytical reasoning (rather than more automatic and biased affective processing) and spur action (Jones, 1991, McMahon and Harvey, 2007). Thus, while the incidence of helping for any entity larger than one is likely to diminish in comparison to that for a single victim, we expect the most notable decrease to occur when comparing one recipient of help to a small group of victims, and for this negative effect to decrease as the number of victims grows.

Hypothesis 7:

The negative effect of victim group size on helping is moderated by the number of individuals in the larger comparison group, such that the effect weakens as number of victims in the larger comparison group increases.

2.5. Event features: certainty, chronicity, and threat severity

In research on interpersonal helping, differences in responding to those in need have been explained in terms of event features and their attributed magnitude of victim need or degree of inferred dependency on help (Berkowitz and Daniels, 1963, Clark and Word, 1974, Weiner, 2000). In general, a victimâ€™s heightened need for help increases the likelihood that a potential donor will offer or intend to offer help (Levitt and Kornhaber, 1977, Zagefka et al., 2012). One explanation is that need for help signals the extent to which the plight facing the victim is a moral issue (i.e., high moral intensity) and interpreted as an ethical dilemma that should be addressed (Jones, 1991, Kelley and Elm, 2003). Thus, we explore three event features that embody a heightened need for helpâ€”certainty, chronicity, and threat severityâ€”to ascertain their moderating effects on the relationship between group size and helping.

Specifically, situations facing victims differ in degree of certainty, or what Weiner, 1980, Weiner, 2000 describes as volitional control attributions. Indeed, not all past studies on compassion fade depict life or death consequences, and not all victims are likely to suffer. Thus, the likelihood that harm will occur is posited as one of the main indicators that reflects moral intensity of an ethical decision, which can make it seem more important to help (Jones, 1991). According to the certainty effect proposed by Kahneman and Tversky (1979), people disproportionately weigh events that are certain to occur relative to those that are uncertain. This effect of event certainty may be more pronounced for a single victim due to the impact of situational tangibility on charitable action (Cryder & Loewenstein, 2012). Specifically, information about a highly certain tragedy is likely to orient a person in the present (Liberman & Trope, 1998), a mindset that makes contextualized details about a single victimâ€”as opposed to a more abstract group of victimsâ€”even more salient. However, increased certainty regarding a group of victims may be more difficult to systematically process and connect with due to the absence of vivid imagery and detail. This suggests that certainty contributes to the likelihood that affective processing breaks down in the face of numerical gradation, a viewpoint aligned with the affective bias perspective.

Another event feature that can influence the relationship between victim group size and helping is chronicity, in that problems facing victims range from those caused by acute loss to those reflective of chronic misfortune. Previous work on perceptual framing used to evaluate the welfare of others suggests that chronic situations may signal greater perceived need for help than an acute misfortune (Lee & Murnighan, 2001). However, because an acute loss (e.g., a car wreck) is viewed as more common, a chronic, ongoing issue (e.g., an incurable disease) may be regarded as a larger deviation from normality that requires substantial aid (Helson, 1964). In terms of compassion fade, this heightened burden to help might facilitate a clearer understanding of the moral imperative at stake with a single victim (Jones, 1991, Weiner, 2000), but it might prove too daunting in the face of multiple victims. In line with this view, compassion fatigue in nurses is often linked to the prevalence of chronically ill patients (Figley, 1995). Further, the motivated choice perspective applies here in that the emotional costs associated with a larger number of chronically ill victims might be too emotionally exhausting for donors, increasing the likelihood that they try to reduce their helping in an effort to evade such overwhelming feelings.

Finally, in regard to threat severity (Rogers, 1983), some scenarios in compassion fade research portray dire circumstances, such as children in Darfur who are suffering from life-threatening diseases (Cameron & Payne, 2011), while others portray far less grave threats, such as not having textbooks for school (Cryder & Loewenstein, 2012). More severe threat appeals typically elicit more adaptive, protective responses (Donovan & Henley, 2000) and trigger stronger donation intentions (Basil, Ridgway, & Basil, 2008). Threat severity not only connotes a heightened level of perceived dependency on help, but also likely enhances the information being processed by potential donors due to the moral intensity associated with the urgency (Jones, 1991, Kelley and Elm, 2003) and global impact attributed to the situation (Weiner, 1980). Thus, information about a severe threat, when coupled with the detailed specificity provided by a single victim at risk (as opposed to a group of victims), may evoke strong affective processing and increase the likelihood of helpingâ€”a prediction in line with the affective bias perspective. Of course, threat severity connotes the strong possibility that the help offered by donors will not be efficacious or timely. Thus, there might be elevated financial and emotional risks associated with threat severity. Because of this, and aligning with the motivated choice perspective, donors may disengage their emotional connection to multiple victims in conditions of threat severity to keep from suffering likely personal losses. Together, the aforementioned arguments suggest that salient event features enhance the perceived needs of a single victim relative to multiple victims, which should strengthen the relationship between victim group size and helping. Thus, we posit:

Hypothesis 8:

The negative effects of victim group size on helping are moderated by certainty, chronicity, and threat severity, such that the effects are stronger when (a) certainty, (b) chronicity, and (c) threat severity are higher rather than lower.

3. Method

3.1. Literature search and inclusion criteria

To identify studies exploring compassion fade, we first searched a variety of electronic databases (including ABI/INFORM, Business Source Complete, Google Scholar, ProQuest Digital Dissertations, PsycINFO, and SSRN) using the following key words: compassion fade, compassion fatigue, compassion collapse, psychological numbing, identifiable victim effect, identifiability bias, and identified victim. We also conducted a manual search of articles appearing in the following journals beginning in 19974: Journal of Applied Psychology, Journal of Behavioral Decision Making, Journal of Consumer Psychology, Journal of Consumer Research, Journal of Experimental Psychology, Journal of Experimental Social Psychology, Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, Journal of Risk and Uncertainty, Judgment and Decision Making, and Organizational Behavior and Human Decision Processes. The reference list of each article found was reviewed to identify additional articles. Finally, to identify possible studies missed by these efforts, we contacted authors of published manuscripts for file-drawer studies. This process yielded 87 potentially relevant studies available through August 2018.

Studies were included if they met several criteria. First, although compassion fade research encompasses work exploring psychophysical numbing, victim identifiability, magnitude insensitivity, and proportion dominance effects (Dickert et al., 2015, Markowitz et al., 2013), we only included empirical studies where one versus many victims were examined. This is consistent with meta-analytic best practices that caution against â€˜comparing apples to orangesâ€™ (Eysenck, 1978, Sharpe, 1997) and enabled us to maintain consistency in compiling effect sizes. Second, studies had to include a single victim condition in comparison to a two or more victims condition rather than conditions that required participants to jointly evaluate the number of victims simultaneously. Third, studies were either experimental or quasi-experimental with victim group size manipulated. Fourth, given our interest in the relationships between victim group size, helping, and the underlying mediating mechanisms, studies had to examine victim group size in relation to empathetic concern, anticipated positive affect, perceived impact, or helping intent and/or behavior. Finally, we excluded studies that did not report statistics for calculating a zero-order effect size for victim group size. In total, 41 studies (including six unpublished manuscripts) representing 95 independent samples (k) and a sample size of 13,259 met these criteria and were included. Coding information per study is provided in Appendix A.

3.2. Coding procedures

Each study was independently coded by two of the authors using a coding scheme for recording effect sizes, sample sizes, and reliabilities. All disagreements (interrater agreementâ€¯=â€¯97.4%) were discussed until consensus was reached. Per previous meta-analyses (i.e., Sitzmann, Brown, Casper, Ely, & Zimmerman, 2008), when a study reported statistics for multiple operationalizations of a construct, the effect sizes were averaged together to create a composite effect size using Hunter and Schmidt (2004) formula to account for within-study correlations. For example, if a study included effects of victim group size on both sympathy and feeling for the victim (which fit with our definition of empathetic concern), those effects were averaged together while accounting for the correlation between sympathy and feeling for the victim.

To match our focal criteria of helping intent or helping behavior, studies had to measure willingness to help or actual help, respectively; other intentions or behaviors (e.g., blame, punishment) were excluded. Helping intent measures were those that examined individualsâ€™ willingness to help via time or money without actual help being given. Helping behavior included outcomes that involved any actual help given, such as actual monetary donations, donations of goods (e.g., candy), or giving advice. Moreover, with respect to mediators, empathetic concern was coded as any other-focused emotion measures that highlighted the victim as the target of the felt emotion, including compassion, sympathy, and feeling upset or concern for the perceived welfare of the victim(s), anticipated positive affect was coded as expected positive feelings or emotions for the observer in regard to donating or helping, and perceived impact was coded as any measure of perceived impact of donation or aid, utility (i.e., financial utility associated with helping/aiding the victim or victims), or efficacy of the help.

Because studies exploring the effects of victim group size on helping varied in terms of how many victims comprise the larger group compared to the single victim, we coded the number of victims in the larger group condition as a between-study moderator. Importantly, for some studies this variable could not be coded because only an abstract description of the larger comparison group was provided rather than an explicit number (c.f., Ein-Gar & Levontin, 2013).

Finally, moderators tied to event featuresâ€”certainty, chronicity, and threat severityâ€”were coded independently by seven doctoral students and faculty trained in a meta-analysis who were blind to hypotheses.5 Raters were provided with verbatim excerpts from each studyâ€™s materials detailing the victimsâ€™ plight. They then rated the predicament faced by the victims on a 1 (strongly disagree) to 5 (strongly agree) scale per dimension. Prior to rating victimsâ€™ plights, raters were told that certainty was defined as the likelihood that the victim(s) will experience the event/problem described, chronicity was defined as the likelihood that the event/problem will continue or recur after help has occurred, and threat severity was defined as the degree to which the event/problem is associated with serious and/or negative consequences.

3.3. Meta-analytic calculations

We employed random effects meta-analytic techniques prescribed by Hunter and Schmidt (2004). We checked for potential outliers within our dataset using the sample-adjusted meta-analytic deviancy (SAMD) statistic (Huffcutt & Arthur, 1995). This procedure uses a bootstrapping technique where the overall sample-size weighted, reliability-corrected correlation between victim group size and the outcome variable is calculated kâˆ’1 times to understand if one sample is biasing the analysis. We ran this analysis for our two outcome variables and three mediators, and detected no outliers. Thus, we proceeded with subsequent analyses.

We transformed reported statistics such as means, standard deviations, t tests, and F tests into Cohenâ€™s d and the associated point biserial correlation (r). Using point biserial correlations facilitates interpretation and meaningful comparison across effect sizes (Hunter & Schmidt, 2004). We estimated the population effect size (Ï) by calculating sample-weighted means of correlations corrected for unreliability in observed correlations due to measurement error in the outcome. Because very few studies used multi-item measures or reported reliabilities, we imputed reliability across the outcomes (imputed ryyâ€¯=â€¯0.84) from the sample-weighted mean reliability of nine outcomes (i.e., four outcomes from Erlandsson, VÃ¤stfjÃ¤ll, Sundfelt, & Slovic, 2016, two outcomes each from Cameron and Payne, 2011, Han, 2013, and one outcome from Lee and Feeley (2018); ryyâ€¯=â€¯0.79, 0.82, 0.88, 0.83, 0.88, 0.81, 0.87, 0.87, and 0.88. respectively). We also reported the number of effect sizes (k) and cumulative sample size (N) for each effect size.

To interpret the correlations, we calculated 95% confidence intervals (CIs) and 80% credibility interval (CVs) for each mean-corrected correlation. CIs estimate variability in the mean-corrected correlation, while CVs estimate variability in the individual-corrected correlations across the studies. Mean corrected correlations were judged to be significant (pâ€¯<â€¯.05) when the 95% CI did not include zero. Further, moderators were deemed likely to be operating when the 80% CV included zero (Whitener, 1990). Variability in effect sizes due to artifacts (%SE) was also examined to indicate the presence of moderators, with less than 75% of variance in the correlations due to artifacts indicative of possible moderators.

To explore the influence of moderators in explaining the effect of victim group size on helping intent and helping behavior, a weighted least squares (WLS) regression approach was employed (Lipsey & Wilson, 2001), using the Fisher-z-transformed corrected correlations weighted by the inverse of each effect sizeâ€™s variance (Hedges & Olkin, 1985). This approach is recommended for continuous moderators (Steel & Kammeyer-Mueller, 2002), and allowed us to control for year of publication and publication status (0â€¯=â€¯not published, 1â€¯=â€¯published) as study characteristics while examining number of victims in the larger comparison group, certainty, chronicity, and threat severity as moderators. Due to the different number of studies included per moderator and potential issues pertaining to multicolinearity,6 moderators were tested separately. For each regression, the total R2 and a beta (Î²) for each moderator is calculated. If the beta is significant, the variability in effect sizes is significantly explained by the moderator. Further, the sign of the beta is interpreted dependent upon the sign of the mean-corrected correlation. For example, if the mean-corrected correlation between victim group size and helping intent is negative, a positive beta indicates that the relationship becomes less negative at higher levels of the moderator, and negative beta indicates that the relationship becomes more negative at higher levels of the moderator. The standard errors for the WLS regression results were adjusted following Lipsey and Wilson (2001), to rely on number of effect sizes rather than sample size.

5. Discussion

Over the last twenty years, a burgeoning interdisciplinary stream of research has explored the occurrence of compassion fade in response to helping a single individual compared to a larger group. However, the multiplicity of possible mechanisms investigated in relation to this phenomenon has hindered the development of a cogent knowledge base. The present meta-analysis is a systematic accumulation of research on compassion fade that serves to integrate this body of work. Overall, our meta-analytic results indicate that as victim group size increases, helping intent and behavior substantially declines. Our findings also suggest that the relationship between victim group size and helping is mediated by self-oriented (anticipated positive affect) and hybrid other-/self-oriented (perceived impact) motivation more than other-oriented motivation tied to empathetic concern. Additionally, we provide insight into possible boundary conditions by documenting variation in the effects of victim group size on helping due to the scope of the calamity (i.e., number of victims in the larger comparison group) and event features (i.e., certainty and threat severity). As discussed below, these findings have important theoretical and practical implications for compassion fade scholarship and the charitable donations industry.

5.1. Theoretical implications

Our primary meta-analytic findingâ€”that victim group size significantly reduces helping intent and behaviorsâ€”substantiates compassion fade as a reality; however, this effect was more pronounced for helping behaviors than intent to help. In part, this may be because in situations where an individual is asked to actually give aid, the consequences of not giving become more salient. This aligns with recent work guided by construal level theory (CLT; Trope and Liberman, 2003, Trope and Liberman, 2010), suggesting that more concrete donation appeals are more likely to elicit actual help (e.g., monetary contributions) than abstract requests for help (e.g., willingness to donate time; MacDonnell & White, 2015; see Ein-Gar & Levontin, 2013, for similar results). Actions are viewed more concretely by people in comparison to intentions, the latter of which are construed at higher, more abstract levels, and perceived as less tangible (Vallacher & Wegner, 1987). Therefore, potential donors in the studies comprising our meta-analysis may have interpreted helping behavior more concretely than intent, which may have exacerbated the compassion fade effect. Extending this finding, future research might consider how the nature of the request for help (i.e., intent versus action) requires perspective taking at different levels of abstraction in order to differentially influence consequences (Longmire & Harrison, 2018).

Meta-analytic results from the current study also suggest that compassion fade occurs more often through hybrid other-/self-oriented motivations (i.e., perceived impact) and, to a lesser extent, self-oriented motivations (i.e., anticipated positive affect) than through other-oriented motivations (i.e., empathetic concern). Although this contradicts common views on the importance of empathy-related feelings as a catalyst for helping (Batson, 1991), it solidifies a burgeoning viewpoint that empathetic concern is not necessary for a connection between a single individual and increased helping (Cryder, Loewenstein, & Scheines, 2013). It should also be noted that both the direct effect of victim group size on perceived impact and the indirect effect through this mechanism were stronger than those effects associated with empathetic concern. In terms of helping a single victim, this suggests future research should focus less on how to increase the degree to which people care about a victimâ€™s plight (i.e., empathetic concern), and more on how to increase perceived impact in order to increase generosity.

Importantly, support for the alternative meta-analytic model suggests that empathetic concern still plays a central role in compassion fade. As a reminder, results from this model showed that the effect of victim group size on empathetic concern occurred entirely through anticipated positive affect and perceived impact, with empathetic concern in turn affecting helping. This aligns with traditional egotistic theorizing on helping, which asserts that purely other-oriented motivations actually originate from motives with a self-oriented emphasis (e.g., Batson et al., 1983, Nelson, 1999, Shaw et al., 1994). Similarly, Zaki (2014), argues that individuals experience empathetic concern only after self-oriented or self-relevant goals/motives occur, with Goetz, Keltner, and Simon-Thomas (2010) further emphasizing that self-oriented goals serve as antecedents of empathetic concern. This aforementioned research underscores the likelihood that empathetic concern operates in a complicated manner in the context of numerous victims versus a single victim. Focusing on delineating this complex relationship, Cameron, 2017, Cameron et al., 2016, Cameron and Payne, 2011 continue to extend the motivated choice perspective by repeatedly demonstrating that people attempt to regulate their empathetic concern downward when faced with mass suffering (compared to a single victim) in order to avoid financial and psychological costs. Results from our alternative model closely align with this idea: as a result of diminished personal affective returns (anticipated positive affect) and a less than advantageous cost/benefit ratio (perceived impact), the empathetic concern and subsequent helping of potential donors diminished as victim group size increased. Similarly, Cameron et al. (2018) recently argued that higher- and lower-level goals, and their relative prioritization, make experiencing empathetic concern a rationally-bounded decision process. An important take-away from their framework and our alternative model is that diminished empathetic concern via lowered anticipated positive affect and perceived impact can be explained from a motivated choice perspective, but not an affective bias explanation. This aspect of our findings broadens the motivated choice perspective and solidifies it as a more viable explanation for compassion fade.

Our findings also shed light on the moderating effect that contextual factors might have on helping in response to one versus many victims in need. As predicted, the number of victims in the larger comparison group moderated the negative relationship between victim group size and helping behavior, such that the relationship weakened as the comparison group increased. It is possible that as the comparison group becomes larger, the needs of the group become too difficult to ignore without the donor experiencing psychological costs in terms of cognitive dissonance or an identity-based moral dilemma (Cameron and Payne, 2012, Jones, 1991). Another explanation is that as the comparison group grows, the individual differences within the group may become muted while the overall cause becomes more salient. Thus, large groups may be viewed as more entitative, and perceived similarly to a single victim in terms of affective processing capacity and motivated self-interests for helping (Smith et al., 2013, VÃ¤stfjÃ¤ll et al., 2014). It is also possible that large groups may evoke a more utilitarian perspective by which donors more thoughtfully consider how their actions may do the greatest amount of good for the greatest number of people. Such utilitarian decision strategies are often employed in the public policy domainâ€”where the objective is to help as many people as possibleâ€”so that government agencies and not-for-profit organizations avoid inefficient allocation (i.e., low perceived impact) of funds (e.g., Baron & Szymanska, 2011).

Our results also delineate how event features serve as boundary conditions; yet, effects varied for intent versus behavior. Supporting our initial predictions, the negative relationship between victim group size and helping intent was stronger when event features were more certain and higher in threat severity. This extends Berkowitz (1972) research demonstrating that people aid others who are perceived as more dependent upon their helping by showing that need dependency is a more effective generator of helping intentions for single victims versus groups. Thus, perceived need in the way of certainty and threat severity might operate as an imagery enhancer for one victim, but may not scale up in the context of multiple victims.

Importantly, while the above rationale aligns with the affective bias perspective, a motivated choice explanation is also plausible. Jones (1991) posits that the moral intensity of a situation is higher when probability of effect is present, defining probability as a joint function of degree to which the event will occur (i.e., certainty) and likelihood of serious cause (i.e., threat severity). Providing evidence that these two constructs are inter-related event features, we found very high correlations between certainty and threat severity (see Appendices B and C). Because of this, although we initially only hypothesized threat severity from a motivated choice perspective, it is likely that tenets of motivated choice could be applied to both event features. Specifically, because people discount the impact of events that have lower probability (i.e., lower certainty) or lower magnitude (i.e., lower threat severity), there is less moral urgency in those situations (Jones, 1991). Conversely, high moral urgency situations signal moral intent and moral recognizability that likely heighten the anticipated emotional, psychological, and financial costs associated with helping multiple needy victimsâ€”a motivated choice situation that results in stronger compassion fade effects when events reflect high certainty or high threat severity.

Finally, running counter to expectations, when donors evaluated a victim event that was certain to occur (high certainty) or had dire consequences (high threat severity), helping behaviors for one victim versus a group of victims declined. This finding is consonant with prior empirical work focusing on the importance of emphasizing prevention as opposed to a cure (e.g., Jenni & Loewenstein, 1997). That is, the urgency of behavioral actions targeted at a single victim may be higher when the problem can be prevented or is minor rather than merely be delayed or managed. Another possibility is that certainty and threat severity act as a type of common fate (cf., Campbell, 1958) that enhances the perception of tangible groupness in large aggregates of victims when there is a call to action in terms of similarly tangible behaviors (rather than merely intent). Similarly, in line with the motivated choice perspective, the personal costs and emotional toll associated with behaviors (i.e., donations) for many victims may be less burdensome when considered relative to the severity of the common fate facing multiple victims. Of course, we should note that although certainty and threat severity each diminished the negative effect of victim group size on helping behavior, donors were still more likely to help a single victim than a group of victims, even when certainty or threat severity was high. Thus, these characteristics reflect a plateau for compassion fade in terms of behaviors rather than reversing the effect.

5.2. Practical implications

Several practical implications emerge from our results. First, our mediation results suggest that appeals focusing on how good helping can make one feel (i.e., anticipated positive affect) or that highlight the difference helping can make (i.e., perceived impact) will be more persuasive for helping single victims than messages eliciting empathetic concern. With this in mind, attempts to elicit help from donors should be most effective when they emphasize the uplifting affective benefits of rendering aid along with details about the efficacy of that aid in addressing the problem. Also, appealing to the self-interested or hybrid other-/self-interested goals of donors may have a greater persuasive effect when the message features a single victim.

The observed mitigating effects of calamity scope yield practical insights as well. Because larger groups may be viewed as less amorphous than small groups, it is important for weighty causes with a large number of victims to evoke a singular unifying message that helps activate the utilitarian motives of potential donors. For example, charitable causes that highlight in-group characteristics within a donorâ€™s community may be more successful in soliciting aid than causes in which a large group of victims are being described differentially (Kogut & Ritov, 2007). As such, our results suggest ways that charitable organizations can tailor their marketing in an effort to increase the likelihood of helping numerous victims in need.

Finally, our results suggest that practitioners developing promotional messages meant to elicit prosocial intent toward a group in need will face resistance when the dilemma involves a certain or a highly threatening situation. However, these messages will be more positively received if actual behaviors are required. Fitting with the motivated choice view, interventions focused on increasing approach motives while reducing avoidance motives, such as interventions with a compassion-oriented focus, have been shown to increase pro-social behavior (Klimecki et al., 2013, Weng et al., 2013). Similarly, Cameron (2017) recommends mindfulness training, which has well-established benefits (cf., Eby, Allen, Conley, Williamson, Henderson, & Mancini, in press), to encourage detailed attention to the present moment and acceptance of potential negative consequences associated with helping. Interventions focused on enhancing peopleâ€™s self-efficacy in helping might also reduce avoidance of helping groups by diminishing perceptions of low perceived impact relative to costs (Sharma & Morwitz, 2016).

6. Limitations and future research

The implications of our research must be couched in the context of its limitations. In testing our meta-analytic path model, we collapsed helping intent and behavior into general helping rather than separating these outcomes because of the limited number of studies providing relationships between each type of helping and our mediators of interest. Although this could be viewed as a limitation, we did not expect differential relationships for helping behavior versus intent, and the observed bivariate effect sizes for helping intent versus helping behavior were similar. Regardless, sampling bias associated with the small number of studies (k), especially in testing the meta-analytic path model, should be considered.

The results are also subject to the limitations of meta-analytic techniques. Unfortunately, we could not include all studies and constructs because of a lack of information necessary for calculating effect sizes. Thus, our work should be viewed as a theoretical integration of the most commonly studied compassion fade variables, rather than an exhaustive list of all mediators and moderators of the relationship between victim group size and helping. Further, because meta-analysis culminates study-level effects, we were unable to tease apart effects due to mediators from effects due to moderators, precluding examination of moderated mediation. Although it is common for meta-analyses to examine the effects of moderators separately from mediation (Butts et al., 2013), many of the moderators examined could influence the mediation process of how victim group size affects helping. Future compassion fade work should consider such relationships, as well as additional contextual moderators and individual differences.

While our meta-analysis revealed mediators of the relationship between victim group size and helpingâ€”as well as an alternative explanation for how those mediators may relate to empathetic concernâ€”we cannot address issues associated with causality because our analyses utilized correlations. Future research that explicitly manipulates our key variables and examines their impact on the victim group sizeâ€“helping relationship using statistical techniques such as experimental-causal-chain design (Spencer, Zanna, & Fong, 2005) would provide a more in-depth understanding of compassion fade. In determining causality, it would also be beneficial to investigate the differences between empathetic concern, empathy, and compassion. Although it has been argued that these constructs are derived from a similar theoretical framework (Zaki, 2014), key differences have been posited (for examples, see Bloom, 2017, Goetz et al., 2010).

Although we limited the scope of our meta-analysis by only including studies comparing responses to a single victim versus a group, we recognize that compassion fade could encompass other numerically-based constructs such as magnitude insensitivity (Frederick & Fischhoff, 1998) and proportion dominance (Bartels, 2006, Slovic, 2007). While operational constraints and our desire for conceptual parsimony prohibited including studies examining these phenomena, we acknowledge that the literature would benefit from a more comprehensive review that accounts for variations in methodological approaches used to explore compassion fade. Moreover, we recognize that many studies exploring compassion fade (including the ones in our database) manipulate both victim group size and identifiability simultaneously, and the latter is inherently linked to the degree of affect processing evoked (Baesler and Burgoon, 1994, Nisbett and Ross, 1980). A more rigorous understanding of compassion fade might result from orthogonally manipulating victim group size along with identifiability and other contextual event features to tease apart important effects associated with each. This would help mitigate limitations we faced in testing moderating features together because of suppression, illuminating which event features matter most when considering many of them at once.

Another limitation is the generalizability of our meta-analytic results. Compassion fade studies often employ between-subjects research designs that feature separate response elicitations where options are presented in isolation and evaluated separately. With this design, subjects do not make explicit trade-offs between numbers of victims as they would in a joint evaluation, where subjects simultaneously consider multiple options together. According to theorizing by Hsee and Zhang (2010), peopleâ€™s behavioral responses to a situation can vary depending on if they evaluate only that situation (separate evaluation) or also another in unison (joint evaluation). Separate evaluation is more influenced by affective factors than joint evaluations (Bazerman et al., 1998, Gino et al., 2010), thus exaggerating compassion fade effects for the former. Further, separate evaluations do not adequately reflect the realistic settings in which people make donation decisions (Kahneman, 2013). Employing more within-subjects research designs (i.e., VÃ¤stfjÃ¤ll et al., 2014) and joint evaluation modes (i.e., Erlandsson et al., 2016) in research on compassion fade would better mirror real charitable giving contexts.

Beyond the limitations above, given the paucity of compassion fade research accounting for cultural differences in potential donors (see Kogut, Slovic, and VÃ¤stfjÃ¤ll (2015) for a notable exception), future research should consider this issue. In particular, cultural dimensions indicative of nurturing (e.g., high femininity) might dampen the tendency among helpers to exhibit greater responsiveness to a single victim versus a larger group, given that these cultures are more consensus-oriented and caring (Hofstede & Hofstede, 2001). Another cultural consideration is humane orientation, which captures the degree to which a culture promotes and rewards individuals for being altruistic and generous (House, 1998). Because these cultural values emphasize caring for those in need, they might influence empathetic concern in ways undetected by merely examining victim group size as a predictor. Supporting this possibility, past research has shown that a sense of environmentalism (versus non-environmentalism) reverses the identifiable victim effect for helping animals in need (Markowitz et al., 2013).

Finally, it is important to note that most studies on compassion fade assess intended helping, willingness to donate money, or helping behavior in a laboratory context. Notable exceptions include Galak et al., 2011, Lesner and Rasmussen, 2014, and Sudhir, Roy, and Cherian (2016). For example, Galak et al. (2011) examined actual lending to borrowers, and found evidence of decreased lending as group size increases. Lesner and Rasmussen (2014) found that a campaign letter focusing on one victim did not result in larger donations than an appeal focusing on a group. Conversely, Sudhir et al. (2016) found compassion fade effects for donation rates, donations per mailing, and donation amount per donor. While experimental studies have contributed to our understanding of compassion fade, additional research examining actual contributions in real world settings is sorely needed.

7. Conclusion

This meta-analysis represents the first attempt, to our knowledge, to quantitatively inventory the empirical literature on compassion fade, explicate potential mediators, and explore boundary conditions of observed effects. Our results yield important insights about how compassion fade operates and the conditions under which victim group size negatively affects helping. Yet, our conclusions are accompanied by new, thought-provoking questions that provide a sound springboard for future research on compassion fade.
