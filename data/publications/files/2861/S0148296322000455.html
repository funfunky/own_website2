Disgust predicts charitable giving: The role of empathy

Author links open overlay panelEugene Y.ChanaFelixSeptiantob
https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jbusres.2022.01.033Get rights and content

Abstract

This research investigates the influence of disgust, as an emotion, on charitable giving. We propose that feeling disgusted decreases consumers’ engagement in charitable behavior (e.g., giving money and time) because it lowers empathy—and empathy is an important determinant in charity-giving decisions. Across three experimental studies in which we manipulate incidental or integral disgust, we illustrate a negative effect of feeling disgusted on charitable giving, with empathy playing an explanatory role. The effect of disgust mainly arises when disgust is paired with an other-focused (not self-focused) ad appeal. The findings shine light on the links between disgust, empathy, and charity. They offer implications for non-profit organizations that depend on the goodwill of donors while at the same time their very cause or even promotional materials may elicit disgust.

1. Introduction

Disgust is a strong and peculiar emotion that discourages individuals from coming into contact with substances that might be infected or diseased (Gomez and Spielmann, 2019, Oaten et al., 2009, Rozin et al., 2008). It may have an evolutionary basis since disgust helped human ancestors solve adaptive problems by avoiding spoiled foods (Curtis et al., 2004, Griskevicius and Kenrick, 2013), avoiding sexual partners who would lower reproductive success (Fessler & Navarrete, 2003), and avoiding members of the out-group who were potential carriers of disease to which in-group members were not yet immune (Faulkner et al., 2004, Inbar et al., 2009, Terrizzi et al., 2010).

Disgust is also a relevant emotion in everyday human behavior. People can feel disgusted upon seeing a dirty bathroom or seeing rotten food. Many charitable contexts also elicit disgust. Charity appeals for sick or starving children often present kids in physically filthy (and thus disgusting) environments. Similarly, donations to help people with certain diseases, especially physical and/or skin illnesses, elicit feelings of disgust from the physical ailment(s). In one example that was controversial but received accolades from the advertising industry, including an award at Cannes, a campaign to promote awareness about female genital mutilation featured an image of the Union Jack flag in the shape of female genitalia (Sanghani, 2015). Such imagery can elicit disgust.

We examine how disgust is relevant to charity decisions. While disgust has been investigated in other social marketing settings (Clayton et al., 2017, Deckha, 2008, Galoni and Noseworthy, 2015, Lerner et al., 2004, Leshner et al., 2011, Morales et al., 2012), there is limited understanding of its role in charitable giving contexts, with limited exceptions (Allred and Amos, 2018, Mazur and Gormsen, 2020, Sherman et al., 2001). The lack of research is noteworthy given many charitable appeals and contexts elicit disgust. Notably, while the few studies have found that disgust is associated with reduced charitable behavior (Allred and Amos, 2018, Mazur and Gormsen, 2020, Sherman et al., 2001), the underlying process driving the effect remains unclear.

The current research thus seeks to provide a nuanced understanding of why disgust reduces charitable giving. Drawing on links between disgust and dehumanization (Fiske et al., 2004, Haslam, 2006), we posit that disgust discourages individuals from engaging in charitable behavior by reducing empathy. However, we further argue that disgust might also increase charitable giving in cases where empathy is not essential. As such, the findings of this research provide a nuanced understanding of how disgust influences charitable behavior and the mediating role of empathy in this regard.

2. Theoretical development

2.1. Disgust in marketing communications

While commercial marketing communications typically endorse positive emotions (Cavanaugh et al., 2015, Septianto et al., 2019), social marketing has long embraced the use of negative emotions (Duhachek et al., 2012, Morales et al., 2012). One reason might be that while commercial marketing promotes products and services, social marketing aims to foster a desired behavior. Interestingly, consumers seem to be more accepting of such negative emotions (e.g., elicited by graphic imagery) when the emotions are used by non- than for-profit groups (Parry, Jones, Stern, & Robinson, 2013). In this regard, it is hoped that the negative emotions will motivate people to make positive behavioral changes (Marteau, Hollands, & Fletcher, 2012). Negative emotions “work” by drawing people’s attention to heightened risks of unwanted behavior (Marteau et al., 2012, Sheeran et al., 2014). Consequently, the vast majority of studies on social marketing have examined the conditions under which negative emotions (e.g., fear, shame, and disgust) are effective in promoting positive or compliant behaviors, thus fostering well-being (Duhachek et al., 2012, Morales et al., 2012).

In our research, we focus on disgust. This is a human emotion linked to a distinct set of physiological and psychological responses including a distinct facial expression (Ekman and Friesen, 1975, Rozin et al., 2008). Disgust is typically elicited by exposure to stimuli such as feces, vomit, blood, and rotten meat. Evolutionary psychologists argue that disgust has an adaptive function, as feeling disgusted has helped the human species survive (Pinker, 1997). More recent research suggests at least two distinct types of disgust—physical and moral (Chapman and Anderson, 2014, Ottaviani et al., 2013). That is, disgust can be elicited by physical or moral impurity; there may even be a third type of disgust, namely sexual disgust elicited by exposure to sexual impurity. Regardless, the examination of different types of disgust is beyond the scope of the present research as we focus on physical disgust.

In the case of disgust, prior research has examined the potential of this emotion for social marketers in the contexts of reducing drug, tobacco, and alcohol consumption (Clayton et al., 2017, Deckha, 2008, Leshner et al., 2011). Morales et al. (2012) found that incorporating disgust within a fear appeal is effective because disgust motivates individuals to expel the disgusting object and also distance themselves from that object (Galoni and Noseworthy, 2015, Lerner et al., 2004). Clayton et al. (2017), likewise, found that disgusting content in anti-tobacco advertisements reduces craving and increases intentions to quit among smokers.

However, some studies have argued that disgust might lead to potential backlash (Allred and Amos, 2018, Mazur and Gormsen, 2020, Sherman et al., 2001). For example, individuals with a higher disgust sensitivity (those more likely to experience disgust) report more negative attitudes toward organ donations (Mazur and Gormsen, 2020, Sherman et al., 2001). In a similar vein, Allred and Amos (2018) demonstrated that while disgust elicited by child victim imagery evokes empathy, it leads to a lower intention to donate. As such, even though disgust seems to have negative influences on charitable behavior, there is limited understanding of why this might be the case—and when feeling disgusted might lead to positive, prosocial behavior. The current research seeks to extend previous studies by examining the influence of disgust on charity giving and further, shine light on the underlying mechanism and boundary condition.

2.2. Dehumanization, empathy, and charity

A recurring finding within the disgust literature is that individuals feeling disgusted dehumanize other individuals (Buckels and Trapnell, 2013, Fiske et al., 2004, Haslam, 2006, Hodson and Costello, 2007, Sherman and Haidt, 2011), with dehumanization defined as the denying of internal states to others (Waytz, Gray, Epley, & Wegner, 2010). This explains why disgust predicts the dehumanization of immigrants (Hodson & Costello, 2007). In particular, disgust may be linked to animalistic (vs. mechanical) dehumanization by reducing other human beings to the category of animals (vs. objects; Haslam, 2006). It seems that people who are disgusted think of other human beings as less human and instead more as animals or objects. Dehumanizing others can serve a psychological benefit, as separating oneself from other (dehumanized) individuals leads to a perceived lower chance of coming into contact with and being possibly infected by them.

In turn, dehumanization lowers empathy for other persons (Cheung and Chan, 2000, Karanika and Hogg, 2020, Kemmelmeier et al., 2008), explaining negative correlations between disgust and empathic concerns (Stevenson et al., 2015, Stone and Potton, 2014). Empathy involves taking into consideration other persons’ mental states (Davis, 1994, Zaki, 2014). Thus, when individuals do not see others as human beings, they may be less likely to consider others’ mental states and exhibit less empathy. As empathy is a key antecedent to prosociality (Bagozzi and Moore, 1994, Cialdini et al., 1987, Pavey et al., 2012, Stürmer et al., 2006), greater disgust, via lowering empathy, might, we propose, reduce one’s willingness to help others. For example, dehumanization decreases one’s ability to recognize others in need via lower levels of empathy (Andrighetto et al., 2014, Čehajić et al., 2009), while efforts in building social networks and cohesion after intra-ethnic conflicts have not only relied on rehumanizing others but also the promotion of empathy (Halpern & Weinstein, 2004). Formally, we thus hypothesize that:

H1a: Disgust reduces individuals’ charitable giving.

H1b: The negative effect of disgust on giving is explained by empathy, such that disgust decreases empathy.

3. Study 1

The objective was to test Hypotheses 1a and 1b. We experimentally manipulated disgust to assess the cause-and-effect of the disgust emotion, such that manipulating disgust should decrease empathy and reduce giving. We measured time that participants are willing to volunteer for a non-profit group as the dependent variable.

Moreover, although we predicted the mediating role of empathy, disgust may reduce charitable giving through other mechanisms. For example, disgust correlates with fear (Van Overveld, De Jong, Peters, Cavanagh, & Davey, 2006), which may also explain lower charitable giving. However, this is unlikely as fear has also been shown to increase altruism (Chan, 2017), although that was in a non-disgusting context. Alternatively, disgust leads to political conservatism (Inbar, Pizarro, Iyer, & Haidt, 2012), which also reduces altruism (Van Lange, Bekkers, Chirumbolo, & Leone, 2012), possibly due to the view that charitable recipients are responsible for their own plight (Clarkson et al., 2015). As such, to rule out the possibility of fear and conservatism as alternate explanations, we measured both fear and conservatism.

3.1. Participants

Students who were Australian citizens (N = 198; Mage = 20.41 years old; S.D. = 2.34) received course credit for about 15 min of their time. There were 101 men (Mage = 20.55 years old; S.D. = 2.11) and 97 women (Mage = 20.38 years old; S.D. = 2.49). The sample was mainly Caucasian (77%), followed by Asian (15%), other (5%), and Indigenous (2.5%). Under half of the sample (35.8%) self-identified as religious, agnostic (32.8%), atheist (20.7%), or other (10.6%). All but one student were single; that one student was married. On political views, the majority supported the two major political parties in the country, namely the right-leaning Liberal-National Coalition (39.8%) and the left-leaning Labor Party (25.7%). Others supported a third party (15.2%) or were independents (19.2%).

3.2. Procedure

We randomly assigned participants to either the experimental (i.e., disgust) or control group. Following Johnson et al. (2016), in the experimental condition we asked participants to recall or describe a disgusting event. In the control group we asked them to write about what they did during the day before. Two blinded research assistants read all the responses and (1) removed those persons writing about moral (and not physical) disgust, and (2) removed those in the control condition who wrote about a disgusting event (moral or physical). Four participants fit either of these criteria, reducing the dataset to 194 valid responses.

Then, all participants were presented with information about the “[name of university] Tutoring Centre,” which was fictitious but supposedly its mission was to offer tutoring and support services for students struggling with their academic coursework. We mentioned that the center was currently helping out a student named “Alex G.,” a second-year student who was going through “a tough time at home” and thus failed his recent Introductory Psychology exam, and that without the center’s help, Alex G. may not succeed academically. This was to make sure that the potential recipient of any altruistic act was a person and identifiable. The description further stated that the center recently lost 5% of its funding because of university cutbacks, and that the center is not covered by student fees, thus they were asking students if they would be able to devote a few hours to act as an administrative assistant, “freeing up some funds to pay for tutors to help students like Alex G.” No photo or description of Alex G. was provided beyond the fact that he was a psychology sophomore. Given the scenario, all participants had the opportunity to volunteer up to 10 h in the next semester. Students indicated how many hours they wished to volunteer. Instructions stated that “a staff member would e-mail them later in the semester” (see Appendix A).

After, all participants responded to the following two questions to measure their empathy towards Alex G.: “How much empathy do you feel for Alex G. given his predicament?” and “How much do you care about Alex G.’s academic success?” These questions were measured on 9-point scales from 1 = “Not at All” to 9 = “Very Much.” To measure conservativism, we asked all participants to identify their political ideology on two distinct 9-point scales, from 1 = “Left-leaning/Liberal” to 9 = “Right-leaning/Conservative.” In Australia, voting is compulsory for all citizens, thus students (who were also Australian citizens, confirmed in a pre-screener) would have familiarity with political issues. We measured fear using the PANAS (Watson, Clark, & Tellegen, 1988). While the PANAS is used to measure affect generally, it has items “scared” and “afraid” that can measure fear.

We also asked participants if they had ever heard of the center (yes, no). As a manipulation check, they completed the Disgust Sensitivity Scale (DSS; Haidt, McCauley, & Rozin, 1994; see Appendix B) to verify that the writing scenario did make people feel disgusted. We instructed all participants to complete the DSS based on their feelings at the moment. During debriefing, they were debriefed in full.

3.3. Results

No student had ever heard of the [university] Tutoring Center, which was expected as it was fictitious. Thus, no participants were excluded based on this criterion.

3.3.1. Disgust manipulation check

Averaging the items on the DSS (α = 0.86), an independent samples t-test revealed that those in the disgust condition scored higher (M = 6.87, S.D. = 2.54) than those in the control (M = 5.12, S.D. = 2.87), t(1 9 2) = 4.54, p < .001, d = 0.56 (95% C.I.: 0.36, 0.94).

3.3.2. Volunteering intentions

An independent samples t-test revealed that those in the disgust condition volunteered less time (M = 3.27, S.D. = 4.71) than those in the control (M = 5.71, S.D. = 4.98), t(1 9 2) = 3.48, p < .001, d = 0.50 (95% C.I.: 0.21, 0.78).

3.3.3. Empathy

Averaging the two empathy items (r = 0.91, p < .001), an independent samples t-test revealed that those in the disgust condition scored lower (M = 6.13, S.D. = 1.46) than those in the control (M = 6.49, S.D. = 0.88), t(1 9 2) = 1.99, p = .048, d = 0.28 (95% C.I.: 0.004, 0.57).

3.3.4. Political ideology

Averaging the items on political ideology (r = 0.89, p < .001), an independent samples t-test revealed that students who were in the disgust condition scored similarly (M = 4.47, S.D. = 2.44) as those in the control (M = 4.76, S.D. = 1.78) (p = .340).

3.3.5. PANAS

We averaged the positive (α = 0.93) and negative affect (α = 0.91) items. An independent samples t-test revealed that students who were in the disgust condition scored similarly on positive mood (M = 6.32, S.D. = 1.87) as those in the control condition (M = 6.22, S.D. = 2.12) (p = .725). Another independent samples t-test revealed that students in the disgust condition scored similarly on negative mood (M = 4.31, S.D. = 2.33) as those in the control (M = 4.00, S.D. = 2.18) (p = .310). Looking at the item “scared” in particular, there was no difference between those in the disgust (M = 4.34, S.D. = 2.11) and the control conditions (M = 4.57, S.D. = 2.62) (p = .497). Similarly, looking at the item “afraid,” there was no difference between those in the disgust (M = 4.42, S.D. = 2.10) and the control conditions (M = 4.48, S.D. = 2.49) (p = .854).

3.3.6. Mediation analysis

We used PROCESS Model 4 (Hayes, 2017) to determine if the disgust manipulation would reduce empathy to then reduce participants’ time willing to volunteer (5,000 bootstrap samples; 95% C.I.). Disgust was coded as 1 (control as 0). We conducted two analyses, the first a parsimonious model including only empathy as our mediator. The indirect effect was estimated between -0.25 and -0.03, thus mediation was significant. See Fig. 1. We conducted another mediation analysis to see if fear (scared and afraid averaged; r = 0.74, p < .001) and conservatism might also mediate the effect as mediation effects are possible even without main effects, in addition to empathy (thus, three mediators in a parallel analysis). The indirect effects were estimated to include 0 (fear: -0.06 to 0.06; political conservatism: -0.13 to 0.21), thus the indirect effects were insignificant, whereas the indirect effect via empathy remained significant, estimating to lie between -0.22 and -0.02.

3.3.7. Additional analysis

As previously discussed, four participants mentioned moral and not physical disgust and thus were removed from the primary analyses. But, to be thorough, we included these four participants’ responses in our analysis for volunteering intentions. An independent samples t-test revealed that those in the disgust condition volunteered less time (M = 3.29, S.D. = 4.72) than those in the control (M = 5.72, S.D. = 5.00), t(1 9 6) = 3.51, p < .001, d = 0.49 (95% C.I.: 0.21, 0.78). Thus, there was no difference in our results. This could be explained by two possible reasons. First, four (out of 198) is a small select group and thus unlikely to sway the “main” responses of the 198. Second, both physical and moral disgust, while they have different antecedents and are elicited by different stimuli (see General Discussion), have similar functions and psychological processes. Thus, even an elicitation of moral disgust also potentially reduces charity giving.

3.4. Discussion

The findings from Study 1 demonstrate the likely impact of disgust on charitable giving and the mediating role of empathy, thus confirming our hypotheses. Crucially, we also rule out two other possibilities for the effect—namely that disgust might reduce giving through fear or conservatism.

4. Study 2

In Study 1, we tested the possible role of empathy in disgust’s impact on charitable giving through measurement-of-mediation approach (Spencer, Zanna, & Fong, 2005). While Study 1 was useful (since we manipulated disgust to test causal impact on empathy), there might still be other factors that could explain the relationship, especially if our empathy measurement co-varies with an omitted variable. While it was possible to use reverse mediation techniques, there are criticisms of this approach as well (Lemmer & Gollwitzer, 2017). One way to avoid these limitations is by manipulating empathy. If empathy were the true mediator, then manipulating it should moderate the impact of disgust on charitable giving. Specifically, we previously argued that disgust increases dehumanization, but this should reduce charitable giving when empathy is not manipulated, replicating our findings thus far. When empathy is heightened, this should “stem” the lower empathy that typically results from dehumanization, thereby stemming the lower charitable giving as well. Formally:

H2a: Disgust should increase dehumanization, but this should only then lower charitable giving when empathy is not heightened.

H2b: When empathy is heightened, disgust should still increase dehumanization, but it should not reduce charitable giving.

4.1. Participants

A total of 220 Britons from Prolific (Mage = 37.93 years old; S.D. = 12.91) participated. There were 80 men (Mage = 37.84 years old; S.D. = 12.42), 139 women (Mage = 38.05 years old; S.D. = 13.24), and 1 non-binary participant (age = 28.00). The sample was Caucasian (77.3%) followed by African American (8.6%), Asian (8.2), and other (6%). Less than half were agnostic (20.5%) or atheist (11.4%), while 49.1% were religious and 19.1% self-identified as other. For relationship status, 45.7% of our sample were married, 42.5% were single, and another 1.8% were widowed. On political views, the majority supported the Conservative Party (24.1%), Labour (39.5%), another party (11.9%), or identified as independents (24.5%).

4.2. Procedure

This study employed a 2 (disgust: salient, control) × 2 (empathy: heightened, non-heightened [control]) between-subjects design. We manipulated incidental disgust using the same procedure as described in Study 1. We then informed all participants that they had the opportunity to win an additional £5 bonus, with five participants chosen randomly to receive this bonus, but they could earmark any amount of this (from £0 to £5) for World Vision. We presented a picture of a young girl in Africa who we mentioned was in need of food and water (see Appendix C). We also manipulated empathy. In the heightened empathy condition, we asked participants to “think about this girl, and why it’s important to empathise with her plight.” In the non-heightened empathy (control) condition, no such statement was provided. The photo of the girl was provided in both conditions. Our dependent variable was earmarked amount of the bonus to World Vision.

We then measured dehumanization. There are numerous ways and measures to do so. We followed Kteily, Bruneau, Waytz, and Cotterill (2015) approach. The authors measured dehumanization by presenting images depicting human evolutionary progression, and asked their participants to classify where they would put various cultural groups (e.g., Americans and Arabs) according to the “Ascent of Man.” (See Appendix D.) Here, we asked participants where they would rate the little girl in the World Vision stimuli on this “Ascent of Man Scale.” The original scale had 5 points, but we adapted it to a 9-point scale to be conceptually consistent with our use of a 9-point measure of DSS (next). Consequently, lower scores meant greater dehumanization. Finally, as in Study 1, participants also completed the DSS based on their feelings at the moment as our manipulation check for disgust. About one day later, after the study’s conclusion, five participants were randomly chosen and paid, with the amount earmarked subtracted and donated to World Vision.

4.3. Empathy manipulation pre-test

We conducted a separate pre-test for our empathy manipulation. We recruited 100 participants from Prolific (Mage = 39.11 years old; S.D. = 11.12; 55 men, 40 women), and they saw the same image of the girl for World Vision as in our main study, along with the same additional sentence in the empathy manipulation. However, participants did not earmark an amount to donate. We simply asked them how much empathy they felt for the girl and how much they cared about her success in life, based on our two measures in Study 1 (separate 9-point scales). Averaging the two empathy items (r = 0.94, p < .001), participants in the empathy condition scored higher (M = 6.78, S.D. = 2.13) than those in the control (M = 5.81, S.D. = 1.99), F(1, 98) = 5.52, p = .020, d = 0.46 (95% C.I.: 0.07, 0.86). Thus, our manipulation of empathy was successful.

4.4. Results

4.4.1. Disgust manipulation check

Averaging the statements on the DSS (α = 0.87), a 2 × 2 ANOVA revealed only a main effect of our disgust manipulation, with the disgust condition increasing disgust (M = 6.27, S.D. = 1.82) compared to no such manipulation (M = 5.27, S.D. = 2.46), F(1, 216) = 9.66, p = .002, d = 0.42 (95% C.I.: 0.15, 0.69). There was no main effect of empathy manipulation (p = .730) and no two-way interaction (p = .713). Hence, only our disgust manipulation affected disgust.

4.4.2. Earmarked donation amount

A 2 × 2 ANOVA revealed a main effect of disgust manipulation, with the disgust condition lowering earmarked amount (M = 2.80, S.D. = 2.10) compared to no manipulation (M = 3.54, S.D. = 1.91), F(1, 216) = 11.55, p = .001, d = 0.46 (95% C.I.: 0.19, 0.73). There was a main effect of our empathy manipulation, with the heightened empathy condition increasing earmarked amount (M = 3.31, S.D. = 2.02) over non-heightened empathy (M = 3.20, S.D. = 2.02), F(1, 216) = 4.07, p = .045, d = 0.27 (95% C.I.: 0.006, 0.54). Importantly, there was also a two-way interaction, F(1, 216) = 7.49, p = .007, d = 0.37 (95% C.I.: 0.10, 0.64).

When disgust was not incidentally elicited, heightened empathy did not affect earmarked amount (M = 3.41, S.D. = 2.01) relative to non-heightened empathy (M = 3.62, S.D. = 1.86) (p = .545). However, when disgust was elicited, earmarked amount was already low on average, but heightening empathy increased the earmarked amount (M = 3.22, S.D. = 2.05) compared to non-heightened empathy (M = 1.85, S.D. = 1.93), F(1, 83) = 8.38, p = .005, d = 0.63 (95% C.I.: 0.19, 1.07). See Fig. 2. Looking at the data in a different way, in the non-heightened empathy condition, eliciting disgust lowered earmarked donation amount, F(1, 108) = 17.58, p < .001, d = 0.80 (95% C.I.: 0.41, 1.19). In the heightened empathy condition, eliciting disgust did not lower earmarked amount (p = .623).

4.4.3. Dehumanization

On this measure, lower scores mean greater dehumanization. A 2 × 2 ANOVA revealed a main effect of our disgust manipulation, with the disgust condition reducing scores (M = 3.29, S.D. = 2.71) compared to no such manipulation (M = 5.27, S.D. = 3.51), F(1, 216) = 17.15, p < .001, d = 0.56 (95% C.I.: 0.29, 0.83). There was no main effect of our empathy manipulation (p = .950) and no two-way interaction (p = .738).

4.4.4. Moderated mediation analysis

Finally, we conducted a moderated mediation analysis to see if eliciting disgust would result in greater dehumanization, and if this would then reduce earmarked donation amount primarily when empathy was not experimentally heightened. See Fig. 3 and Table 1. We used Model 15 of the bootstrapping protocols (5,000 bootstrapped samples; 95% C.I.), with the presumed moderator (empathy manipulation) acting on the effect of the presumed mediator (dehumanization) and dependent variable (earmarked amount), with the independent variable being disgust. Disgust was coded as 1 (control as 0), and heightened empathy was coded as 1 (control as 0). The index of moderated mediation was significant, as it was estimated to lie between -0.55 and -0.007. In the non-heightened empathy condition, the indirect effect of disgust on earmarked amount via dehumanization was significant, estimating to lie between 0.23 and 0.75. In the heightened empathy condition, the indirect effect was no longer significant, as it was estimated to lie between -0.10 and 0.55.

4.5. Discussion

We provide further evidence for our hypotheses, this time by manipulating empathy to show that doing so moderates the effect of disgust on charitable giving. Specifically, we replicate our finding that disgust reduces charity—but only when empathy was not made salient. When it was, this stemmed the lower giving when consumers feel disgusted. Thus, manipulating empathy can essentially act as an “intervention”. Feeling disgusted results in dehumanization, which reduces empathy. Thus, an intervention that leads consumers to feel more empathy should thus prevent the typical lower charitable giving when potential donors are feeling disgusted and dehumanizing target recipients.

5. Study 3

While we posited that disgust decreases empathy and giving, there might be some conditions under which disgust might be less relevant and influential for empathy. In particular, the negative effect of disgust on giving might not emerge in cases when empathy is not reduced. Namely, while the focus of charitable behavior is typically on those in need (i.e., other-focused), social marketers can also employ self-focused ad appeals to encourage giving.

We propose that self-focused versus other-focused ad appeals is a moderator that influences the effect of disgust on giving. Disgust should reduce giving in the case of other-focused ad appeals because empathy is lower. Indeed, in our studies so far, our target recipient is “other” compared to the individual (or potential donor). However, in the case of self-focused ad appeals, empathy should not be reduced because the focus is on the self, while empathy is an other-focused construct. Rather, in self-focused ad appeals, there tends to be a preference to protect oneself from threats and negativity, including disease and illness (Galoni et al., 2020, Schaller and Neuberg, 2012, Tybur et al., 2013). Note that we do not expect the self-protection motive to be elicited in other-focused ad appeals because this motive is less relevant in this context.

In sum, even though disgust is associated with lower empathy and an increased self-protection motive, the ad appeal will determine which motive would emerge. In the other-focused ad appeal condition, disgust should reduce empathy, thus decreasing charitable giving. In contrast, in the self-focused ad appeal condition, disgust should increase self-protection motive, leading to an increased charitable giving. Formally stated:

H3a: Disgust reduces charitable giving in the other-focused ad appeal condition, but increases giving in the self-focused ad appeal condition.

H3b: The positive effect of disgust on giving for self-focused ad appeals is explained by self-protection motive.

Taken together, Study 3 extends the literature in two meaningful ways. First, in the prior studies, we elicited incidental disgust using an unrelated task (e.g., recalling and writing about a disgust-related event). While these examinations provided empirical evidence for our hypotheses, it is difficult for marketers to extrapolate such findings in real contexts given the origin of the emotion is external to the charitable context. Following others (Cavanaugh et al., 2015, Septianto et al., 2019), we thus examined the effect of integral disgust—that is, by manipulating disgust within an advertisement, or within the charitable context. Second and more importantly, we examined a boundary condition of our hypotheses. Specifically, although disgust reduces charitable giving because it lowers empathy in the other-focused ad appeal condition, it can also elicit self-protection motive and increase charitable giving in the self-focused ad appeal condition.

5.1. Participants

A total of 239 American citizens from Mechanical Turk (Mage = 38.98 years old; S.D. = 10.56; Litman, Robinson, & Abberbock, 2017) participated in exchange for a small amount of monetary compensation. There were 142 men (Mage = 37.30 years old; S.D. = 10.28) and 97 women (Mage = 41.45 years old; S.D. = 10.52). The sample was mainly Caucasian (78.7%), followed by African American (11.7%) and Asian (6.7%), and others (2.9%). Most held an undergraduate degree (62.3%), followed by a post-graduate degree (28.5%), completed high school (7.1%), and technical or vocational training (2.1%). Over half of the sample (69.5%) self-identified as religious, atheist (12.6%), agnostic (9.6%), or other (8.3%). More than half of the sample were married (83.3%) followed by single (16.3%), and other (0.4%). On political views, the majority supported the Democrats (54.4%), followed by Republicans (38.5%), and others/independents (7.1%).

5.2. Procedure

This study employed a 2 (disgust: salient, control) × 2 (ad appeal: self-focused, other-focused) between-subjects design; hence, we developed four advertisements consistent with this design. We used “friend” as the operationalization of “other”. Disgust was manipulated within the image using the context of skin cancer (Morales et al., 2012). Ad appeal was manipulated by altering the potential impact of skin cancer (Agrawal, Menon, & Aaker, 2007), such that in the self (other) condition, participants read: “Skin exposure causes skin damage and leads to skin cancer. Picture yourself (your friend) if you (they) got this – how would it feel? Think of yourself (your friend)!”

Participants then read a brief paragraph stating that, “In the U.S., more than two people die every hour as a result of skin cancer. Since 1979, the Skin Cancer Foundation has made tremendous strides in helping people understand the importance of skin cancer prevention, early detection and prompt, effective treatment.” Following Sharma and Morwitz (2016), participants were asked how much they would like to allocate from the total payment (50 cents) for donation to the Skin Cancer Foundation in 10-cent increments (0, 10, 20, 30, 40, or 50 cents).

To measure empathy, participants responded to two questions (r = 0.68, p < .001): “How much empathy do you feel for a person who gets skin cancer?” and “How much do you care about a person who gets skin cancer?” These questions were measured on 9-point scales from 1 = “Not at All” to 9 = “Very Much.” We chose to use slightly different wording for our empathy questions (compared to Studies 1 and 2) to avoid method invariance. To measure self-protection motive, participants rated the extent to which they had “a desire to protect themselves” (Griskevicius, Goldstein, Mortensen, Cialdini, & Kenrick, 2006), measured on a 9-point scale from 1 = “Not at All” to 9 = “Very Much.”

As manipulation checks, we asked participants the extent to which they felt “unclean,” “disgusted,” “dirty,” “revolted,” “scared,” “anxious,” “afraid,” and “nervous” (Galoni et al., 2020). The first four items were averaged as a measure of disgust (α = 0.93), whereas the last four items were averaged as a measure of fear (α = 0.94). Note that we used these measures because of potential concerns related to how the PANAS (used in Study 2) might show poor performance when used with emotion manipulations (Harmon-Jones, Bastian, & Harmon-Jones, 2016). Participants also indicated the extent to which their thoughts about the ad message made them focus on (a) themselves and (b) their family (Agrawal et al., 2007). Items were measured on a 9-point scale, from 1 = “Not at All” to 9 = “Very Much.” At the end of the study, participants completed demographic questions and were debriefed.

5.3. Results

5.3.1. Disgust manipulation check

As expected, a two-way ANOVA (disgust × ad appeal) examining the levels of disgust revealed only the main effect of disgust, F(1, 235) = 6.90, p = .009, d = 0.34 (95% C.I.: 0.08, 0.60), such that participants in the disgust condition (M = 6.35, S.D. = 1.81) reported higher levels of disgust than those in the control condition (M = 5.68, S.D. = 2.11). In addition, examining the levels of fear, we found non-significant main effects of disgust, ad appeal, and the interaction effect (ps > 0.100).

5.3.2. Ad appeal manipulation check

The two-way ANOVA (disgust × ad appeal) only showed significant main effects of ad appeal, such that participants in the self-focused condition (M = 7.21, S.D. = 1.56) thought more of themselves when reading the ad message than did those in the other-focused condition (M = 6.68, S.D. = 1.99), F(1, 235) = 5.15, p = .024, d = 0.29 (95% C.I.: 0.03, 0.55). But, participants in the other-focused condition (M = 7.28, S.D. = 1.41) thought more of their friend when reading the ad message than did those in the self-focused condition (M = 6.81, S.D. = 1.82), F(1, 235) = 4.98, p = .027, d = 0.29 (95% C.I.: 0.03, 0.54).

5.3.3. Donation amount

We conducted another two-way ANOVA to examine participants’ donations. Results revealed a significant main effect of ad appeal, F(1, 235) = 5.99, p = .015, d = 0.31 (95% C.I.: 0.06, 0.57), but a non-significant main effect of disgust (p = .966). There was a significant interaction effect, F(1, 235) = 9.46, p = .002, d = 0.40 (95% C.I.: 0.14, 0.65). As can be seen in Fig. 4, when evaluating an other-focused ad appeal, participants in the disgust condition (M = 24.92, S.D. = 15.36) reported lower donation amounts than those in the control condition (M = 30.66, S.D. = 13.02), F(1, 235) = 4.88, p = .028, d = 0.28 (95% C.I.: 0.03, 0.54). Conversely, when evaluating a self-focused ad appeal, participants in the disgust condition (M = 35.08, S.D. = 12.92) reported higher donations than those in the control (M = 29.50, S.D. = 15.45), F(1, 235) = 4.58, p = .033, d = 0.27 (95% C.I.: 0.02, 0.53).

5.3.4. Empathy

We conducted a two-way ANOVA (disgust × ad appeal) to examine empathy. There was a non-significant main effect of disgust (p = .119) but a significant main effect of ad appeal, F(1, 235) = 9.25, p = .003, d = 0.39 (95% C.I.: 0.13, 0.65). There was also a significant interaction, F(1, 235) = 4.91, p = .028, d = 0.28 (95% C.I.: 0.03, 0.54). Specifically, when evaluating an other-focused ad appeal, participants in the disgust condition (M = 5.96, S.D. = 1.96) reported lower empathy than those in the control condition (M = 6.74, S.D. = 1.40), F(1, 235) = 7.17, p = .008, d = 0.34 (95% C.I.: 0.09, 0.60). However, when evaluating a self-focused ad appeal, there was no difference in empathy between the disgust (M = 7.04, S.D. = 1.30) and control conditions (M = 6.91, S.D. = 1.65) (p = .647).

5.3.5. Self-protection motive

We conducted a two-way ANOVA (disgust × ad appeal) to examine self-protection motive. Results revealed a significant interaction effect, F(1, 235) = 4.24, p = .041, d = 0.26 (95% C.I.: 0.01, 0.52). Specifically, when evaluating a self-focused ad appeal, participants in the disgust condition (M = 7.29, S.D. = 1.55) reported a higher self-protection motive than those in the control condition (M = 6.50, S.D. = 2.10), F(1, 235) = 5.73, p = .017, d = 0.31 (95% C.I.: 0.05, 0.57). However, when evaluating an other-focused ad appeal, such effects did not emerge among participants in the disgust (M = 6.86, S.D. = 1.99) and control conditions (M = 7.03, S.D. = 1.46) (p = .608).

5.4. Discussion

The results of Study 3 provide support for our prediction using integral disgust, which we elicited within an advertisement. More importantly, we identified a boundary condition on the negative effect of disgust on charitable giving by examining the role of self-focused versus other-focused ad appeals. Participants in the disgust condition donated less when evaluating an other-focused ad appeal, consistent with the results of Studies 1 and 2. But, when the ad appeal was self-focused, those in the disgust condition reported higher donations. Also, we established the mediating roles of empathy in the other-focused ad appeal condition and self-protection motive in the self-focused ad appeal condition.

6. General discussion

Across three experimental studies, we observe the negative influence of disgust on charitable giving. Further, we shine light on the underlying mechanism (empathy) and the moderating role of ad appeal (self-focused, other-focused). In Study 1, we manipulate incidental disgust (using a writing task) to show that it impacts empathy (but not fear or conservatism) to then reduce one’s willingness to volunteer. In Study 2, we manipulate empathy to show that it moderates the impact of disgust on reducing charitable giving. Finally, in Study 3, we manipulate integral disgust in an advertising context, reporting that disgust has a negative effect on giving only in the case of other-focused (but not self-focused) ad appeals.

6.1. Theoretical implications

Our findings offer several theoretical implications. Prior work has mostly studied disgust in social marketing contexts (Clayton et al., 2017, Deckha, 2008, Leshner et al., 2011, Morales et al., 2012), with limited attention in the context of charitable giving. Hence, we contribute to the literature on disgust and prosocial behavior (Allred and Amos, 2018, Mazur and Gormsen, 2020, Sherman et al., 2001) by establishing the underlying mechanism. In particular, drawing upon prior research suggesting that disgust can lead people to dehumanize others (Buckels and Trapnell, 2013, Hodson and Costello, 2007, Sherman and Haidt, 2011), that dehumanization lowers empathy for other persons (Cheung and Chan, 2000, Karanika and Hogg, 2020, Kemmelmeier et al., 2008), and the role of empathy in charitable giving (Bagozzi and Moore, 1994, Pavey et al., 2012, Stürmer et al., 2006), we offer empirical evidence on the mediating role of empathy (Studies 1–3). Also, we rule out fear and conservatism as rival explanations (Studies 1 and 2) and test our predictions by both mediation and moderation approaches (Studies 2 and 3; Spencer et al., 2005).

Our findings seem to be in contrast to the findings of Allred and Amos (2018), who showed that even though disgust reduces donation intentions, it also evokes empathy. We expect this difference to occur because Allred and Amos (2018) examined disgust elicited by imagery of children suffering from ailments, a stimulus that has been shown to elicit distress and other negative emotions (Griffin, Babin, Attaway, & Darden, 1993). Rather, our findings are consistent with previous work (Allred and Amos, 2018, Mazur and Gormsen, 2020, Sherman et al., 2001) showing that disgust lowers giving of money and time.

Importantly, we identify the condition under which disgust discourages charitable behavior. Because disgust reduces giving via empathy, we expect that the negative effect of disgust might not emerge in the case when empathy is not essential. Moreover, because disgust has an evolutionary function to protect oneself (Curtis et al., 2004, Griskevicius and Kenrick, 2013), when the ad appeals emphasize the relevance to the self (e.g., the potential impact of skin cancer to oneself), disgust can encourage charitable behavior. As such, we contribute to the literature by showing the moderating role of self-focused versus other-focused ad appeals and shedding light on the underlying mechanisms.

6.2. Practical implications

From a practical standpoint, the results of this research offer implications for non-profit organizations. This is because disgust can be elicited. For example, disease concerns can make people disgusted (Galoni et al., 2020, Huang et al., 2017, Lee et al., 2010). More importantly, the use of integral emotions that are applicable in the marketing context can make it beneficial for marketers to employ such emotional appeals in their marketing communications. For example, charities that help the sick or poor may elicit disgust, which may actually in turn reduce giving—contrary to their goals. Our research highlights these important possibilities.

Our findings also suggest that increasing empathy mitigates the effects of disgust, consistent with the broader literature showing the importance of empathy in giving (Cialdini et al., 1987, Pavey et al., 2012, Stürmer et al., 2006). Indeed, in Study 2, when we increase empathy, the effect of disgust on lower charitable donations dampens. Thus, inducing empathy might be particularly useful for some non-profits (those that might elicit disgust) and for potential donors (those feeling disgusted). We also show that disgust appeals can be useful when combined with self-focused (but not other-focused) ad appeals. These options provide understanding for marketers on how to develop effective charitable communication strategies when using disgust in the emotional appeal or in situations when disgust as an emotion is salient, such as during COVID-19 when disease concerns are salient (Galoni et al., 2020).

6.3. Limitations and future research

First, while we rule out fear and conservatism, there may be other explanations that we do not test. There may be different routes through which people are encouraged to give (Septianto, Sung, Seo, & Tugiman, 2018) and hence a more comprehensive study of disgust and how it affects charitable giving is needed. Second, while we rule-in dehumanization in Study 2, we only used a single-item measure (Kteily et al., 2015); other (multi-item) measures may better capture this construct.

Third, we focus only on physical disgust. It would be interesting to assess if our effects also apply to moral disgust. For starters, the eliciting stimuli differ. Feces and seeing skin diseases elicit physical disgust but not moral disgust. By contrast, exposure to incest generally elicits moral disgust. There are two reasons why physical disgust may be more relevant to charity research, though. First, charity appeals are more likely to present physically than morally disgusting stimuli. Indeed, sick children in Africa, hospitals, and the homeless are more common in charity appeals, which are less likely to contain stimuli that elicit moral disgust. Second, moral disgust can vary not only as an individual difference that can also be measured by disgust sensitivity, but also by culture. For example, some cultures find it disgusting when women drive cars or kids sleep in their parents’ bed, while other cultures do not. Yet physical disgust is elicited by common stimuli across cultures. This makes physical disgust likely pertinent. We have no reason to believe that our effect would be different when examining moral disgust because the two types of disgust have similar functions (Lee and Ellsworth, 2013, Russell and Giner-Sorolla, 2013); it is just that their antecedents and variations differ. Future research should explore the conditions under which these types of disgust might lead to similar versus divergent effects on charitable giving.

Fourth, future research can explore other boundary conditions that might prompt disgust to increase giving. There might be contexts where empathy is not needed, thus attenuating the negative effect of disgust on giving. One example is funding for symphonies and art galleries as donations to such organizations, however not-for-profit and dependent on the goodwill of donors, may not depend on empathy but rather a bond of identification (Bhattacharya, Rao, & Glynn, 1995). Indeed, in charitable giving situations that are less dependent on empathy, higher disgust may have little effect on actual giving. That said, our findings might further apply to charitable contexts in which empathy is involved. In addition, another potential moderator is comparing different types of donations. In our research, we demonstrate that disgust can reduce charitable giving for both money and time donations. However, MacDonnell and White (2015) suggested that time (vs. money) donation is construed more abstractly (at a high construal level). As disgust is associated with a high construal level (Chowdhry, Winterich, Mittal, & Morales, 2015), it is plausible that disgust’s effect might be stronger if the dependent variable is time donation (volunteering) rather than money donation because there is a construal-fit between disgust and time donation.

6.4. Conclusions

In summary, disgust reduces empathy and charitable giving. Although there are limitations and boundary conditions to consider, we offer empirical evidence for the posited relationships, thus broadening understanding of the various impacts of disgust and providing practical implications for charitable organizations that may elicit disgust due to their very nature. We hope that these findings prove fruitful for further research.
