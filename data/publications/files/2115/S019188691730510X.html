Personality, equity sensitivity, and discretionary workplace behavior

Highlights

•

•

•

•

Abstract

The present study sought to utilize an equity based approach to understand the relationships between broad personality traits (i.e., Honesty-Humility, Conscientiousness, and Agreeableness) and two important workplace behaviors: organizational citizenship behaviors (OCBs) and workplace deviance (WD). We hypothesized that the relationships between these traits and OCB/WD could be somewhat accounted for by the association between these traits and individual differences in equity sensitivity – one's input/output orientation. Using two samples (Ns = 182 and 206) we found evidence that (a) Honesty-Humility and Conscientiousness are associated with equity sensitivity, (b) equity sensitivity predicts levels of OCB and WD, and (c) Honesty-Humility and Conscientiousness predict OCB and WD through their association with equity sensitivity. Although equity sensitivity provided clarity as to these relationships, direct effects remained for Conscientiousness on WD in one of the samples, and Honesty-Humility retained a direct effect on WD in both samples. Agreeableness was not robustly associated with equity sensitivity, but did have a direct negative effect on WD, indicating additional important mechanisms linking traits to WD. Overall, however, evidence emerged that individual differences in equity sensitivity (Benevolence versus Entitledness) can help to understand the associations between broad personality traits and workplace behavior.

Workplace Deviance (WD) and Organizational Citizenship Behaviors (OCBs) are two critical sets of organizational behaviors that have been studied extensively in the organizational sciences, as they play a significant role in the attainment of organizational goals and are central to the understanding of individual workplace behaviors (Rotundo & Sackett, 2002). Workplace deviance (WD) is defined as “voluntary behavior that violates significant organizational norms and, in so doing, threatens the well-being of the organization or its members, or both” (Bennett & Robinson, 2000, p. 349). WD includes negative workplace behaviors such as theft, coming in late, or rude or aggressive acts towards co-workers. On the other hand, OCBs are generally more positive discretionary behaviors that support the broader social and organizational success, such as helping others, volunteering, and attending non-mandatory functions (Borman and Motowidlo, 1993, Lee and Allen, 2002, Organ, 1997). WD has been demonstrated to have many potential damaging and costly effects on organizations (Bennett & Robinson, 2000), whereas OCBs are proposed to positively impact employee productivity and organizational performance (Bolino et al., 2002, Podsakoff and MacKenzie, 1994).

Given the importance of WD and OCB, research has focused on understanding the personality traits that predict such behaviors (e.g., Borman et al., 2001, Lee et al., 2005, Organ and Ryan, 1995). For instance, within the HEXACO model, research has found Honesty-Humility and Conscientiousness to be consistently negatively associated with WD and similar behaviors (e.g., Lee et al., 2005, Louw et al., 2016), whereas research on the Big Five has found Conscientiousness, Agreeableness, and Emotional Stability to be the strongest predictors of WD (e.g., Berry, Ones, & Sackett, 2007). A similar constellation of traits has been proposed to be central to understanding OCB, although in the positive direction, with Conscientiousness and Agreeableness consistently predicting OCB (Borman et al., 2001, Ilies et al., 2006).

Despite our increasing knowledge of the traits that predict OCB and WD, there are gaps in our understanding of the theoretical mechanisms through which personality may impact these behaviors. In other words, our understanding of “why” certain traits may predict OCB and WD remains under-represented. In the present study, we propose that the relationship between broad personality traits and these behaviors can be explained by the fact that individuals with certain personality traits have different preferences surrounding input/outcome ratios, and differing tolerances for under-reward and over-reward in the workplace – otherwise known as differences in “equity sensitivity” (Huseman, Hatfield, & Miles, 1987). Benevolents are more oriented towards inputs and tolerant of being under-rewarded, while Entitleds are more outcome oriented and intolerant of under-reward. Recent research has indicated that three traits that are central correlates of WD and OCB – namely Honesty-Humility, Conscientiousness, and Agreeableness – are also robust correlates of equity sensitivity, with individuals high on these traits being more Benevolent, and individuals low on these traits being more Entitled (Woodley, Bourdage, Ogunfowora, & Nguyen, 2016). Combined with the fact that equity and justice frameworks have been central in understanding WD and OCB, we believe that individual equity sensitivity may enable an understanding of why certain broad personality traits are associated with WD and OCB. This is consistent with research proposing that equity sensitivity might help to understand the relations between certain traits and workplace behaviors (Woodley & Allen, 2014).

In the present study, we test the idea that Honesty-Humility, Conscientiousness, and Agreeableness predict WD and OCB because of their association or shared variance with equity sensitivity. The implications of this study are two-fold: it builds knowledge by enhancing our understanding of the complex associations between individual differences and workplace behaviors, and it may also provide valuable insight to organizations on how different individuals may react to various organizational phenomena, such as injustice and reward.

1. Personality and WD/OCB

Research has found that personality plays a substantive role in predicting OCB and WD. Within the HEXACO model, the two most prominent predictors of WD have been Honesty-Humility and Conscientiousness. Honesty-Humility describes individual differences in tendencies towards manipulativeness, willingness to take advantage of others, greed, and perceived superiority (Lee & Ashton, 2004). Research has repeatedly demonstrated Honesty-Humility to be robustly and negatively correlated with WD and related behaviors (e.g., Marcus et al., 2007, Zettler and Hilbig, 2010). These same studies also demonstrate Conscientiousness to robustly and negatively relate to WD, while studies utilizing the Big Five or Five-Factor Model have shown meta-analytic negative associations for Conscientiousness, Agreeableness, and Emotional Stability with WD (e.g., Berry et al., 2007). On the other hand, studies have found that Conscientiousness and Agreeableness have the most robust positive relationships with OCB (Borman et al., 2001, Organ and Ryan, 1995). In sum, there seems to be a strong personality basis in predicting WD and OCB, with prominent roles for Honesty-Humility, Conscientiousness, and Agreeableness.1

2. The equity basis for WD/OCB

Both WD and OCB are noted as being discretionary behaviors that are rooted in social exchange and responses to the environment. One's perceptions of fairness and justice in the workplace predicts both WD and OCB, with organizational justice perceptions being positively related to OCB (Colquitt, Conlon, Wesson, Porter, & Ng, 2001) and negatively related to WD (Fox, Spector, & Miles, 2001).

Of particular relevance to the present paper is equity theory and the idea of distributive justice. According to equity theory (Adams, 1965) individuals judge fairness based on the ratio of inputs (e.g. effort, time, energy) to outputs (e.g. rewards) that they receive relative to a referent other. In the context of WD and OCB, if an individual feels they are under-rewarded relative to their inputs, they may reduce their effort to decrease inputs or perhaps steal (i.e., engage in WD) to increase their outputs. Similarly, an individual who feels that they are being treated well and rewarded beyond what they deserve may increase their inputs – for instance, by engaging in OCB – to better match their outputs.

Rather than all individuals having the same preferences surrounding the appropriate ratio of input-outcomes, Huseman et al. (1987) propose that individuals differ in their preferred ratios, with some individuals being more input-oriented and others more outcome-oriented. Variation on this preference is labeled “equity sensitivity.” On the high end of the continuum are Benevolents – these individuals focus on what they can “give” in a situation, and are more tolerant of under-reward. On the other end of the continuum are Entitleds – individuals who are focused on outcomes, and prefer to receive more relative to their inputs than others do.

An individual's equity sensitivity should impact the extent to which they engage in OCB and WD. Indeed, Blakely, Andrews, and Moorman (2005) found that when perceptions of organizational justice were low, Entitleds exhibited substantially lower levels of OCBs, whereas Benevolents performed more OCBs than Entitleds regardless of justice perceptions. Similarly, research indicates that Benevolents seem to engage in more citizenship behaviors in a team environment than do Entitleds (Akan, Allen, & White, 2009).

Conversely, WD has also been found to be a potential means by which employees can control their input/output ratios, particularly in instances of perceived unfairness or mistreatment. A number of studies have shown that individuals who perceive greater unfairness tend to engage in more WD (e.g., Aquino, Galperin, & Bennett, 2004). It is proposed that this perceived unfairness or injustice is related to WD because employees who feel unfairly treated may reduce their cooperative behaviors to avoid exploitation and to even the score (Lind, 2001), or engage in sabotage (Ambrose, Seabright, & Schminke, 2002).

Despite the prominent relationship between perceived injustice and WD, research on the relationship between Equity Sensitivity and WD is limited. However, it has been found that following contract breach, entitled individuals responded more adversely as evidenced by an increasing display of interpersonal deviant acts (Restubog, Bordia, & Tang, 2007).

Together, we predict that equity sensitivity will be positively related to OCB and negatively related to WD, such that those who are more Benevolent will engage in more OCB and less WD, and those who are more Entitled will engage in less OCB and more WD.

3. Personality, equity sensitivity, and WD/OCB

To this point, we have demonstrated that both broad personality traits and equity sensitivity have a role to play in predicting OCB and WD. However, we propose that traits such as Honesty-Humility, Conscientiousness, and Agreeableness are associated with OCB and WD at least partially because these personality traits are associated with variations in equity sensitivity. Indeed, a recent study by Woodley et al. (2016) found that across three studies and both self and peer-reports of personality, Honesty-Humility and Conscientiousness were robustly associated with equity sensitivity, such that individuals high on these traits tend to be more Benevolent, and individuals low on these traits tend to be more Entitled. Agreeableness demonstrated similar patterns with equity sensitivity, although somewhat less robustly. While the finding with Honesty-Humility was novel, other studies have also found Conscientiousness and Agreeableness to relate to equity sensitivity (e.g., Bing and Burroughs, 2001, Scott and Colquitt, 2007). These findings were consistent with the nature of these traits for a number of reasons. First, individuals low on Honesty-Humility tend to believe they are superior to others and are very status and outcome oriented, whereas those high in Honesty-Humility are higher on reciprocal altruism (Ashton & Lee, 2007). Moreover, research indicates that agreeable individuals are more flexible, trusting, and lenient with others (Lee & Ashton, 2004), whereas those high (versus low) in Conscientiousness are generally input oriented and willing to work hard even when outcomes (e.g., pay) are low (Burnett, Williamson, & Bartol, 2009).

Together, these findings indicate that individuals high on these traits will be more input-oriented and willing to tolerate unequal treatment, whereas those low on these traits will attempt to gain more outputs than others. As such, there seems to be a basis to each of these traits that is rooted in their responses to and preferences around equity.

Consistent with these findings, we believe that Honesty-Humility, Conscientiousness, and Agreeableness will be positively related to engaging in OCBs through increased tendencies towards benevolence (high equity sensitivity), whereas low levels of these traits will lead to increased WD due to their association with increased entitlement (low equity sensitivity).

In short, we test the following hypotheses:

Hypothesis 1

(a) Honesty-Humility, (b) Conscientiousness, and (c) Agreeableness will be positively associated with equity sensitivity.

Hypothesis 2

Equity sensitivity will (a) positively predict OCB and (b) negatively predict WD.

Hypothesis 3

(a) Honesty-Humility, (b) Conscientiousness, and (c) Agreeableness are positively related to OCB through equity sensitivity.

Hypothesis 4

(a) Honesty-Humility, (b) Conscientiousness, and (c) Agreeableness are negatively related to WD through equity sensitivity.

4. Method

4.1. Participants and procedure

4.1.1. Student sample

The first sample consisted of 182 employed undergraduate students from a Canadian university. Of these, 77.5% were female and the mean age was 20.67 (SD = 0.42). The majority of participants were employed in either customer service or sales and had a mean tenure of 1.86 years (SD = 2.21). Students were awarded course credit in exchange for participation.

Measures were collected over two time points, exactly one week apart. This time lag was designed to reduce any priming effects that the individual difference measures may have on the participants' responses to the outcome measures. Personality and equity sensitivity measures were completed at Time 1, and OCB and WD measures were completed at Time 2. Such temporal separation has been proposed as one way to decrease common method bias (Podsakoff, MacKenzie, Lee, & Podsakoff, 2003).

4.1.2. Employee sample

Our second sample was employee-based where participants were recruited through an online crowdsourcing website known as StudyResponse, based out of Syracuse University. Participants completed an online questionnaire through Qualtrics, and were compensated $10.00 USD for their time.

This sample consisted of 206 employees, and of these participants, 100 were male (48.5%) and 106 were female (51.5%). Participants' ages varied from 26 to 69 (M = 45.60, SD = 10.98) with an average tenure of 10.78 years (SD = 8.28) in their current organization. Participants came from a wide variety of occupations such as accounting, administration, engineering, finance, legal, maintenance, and sales. Measures were completed at a single time point.

4.2. Measures

4.2.1. Personality

In both samples, personality was assessed using the 100-item HEXACO-PI-R (Lee & Ashton, 2004), which contains 16 items per personality factor. Participants responded using a 5-point Likert scale (1 = strongly agree, 5 = strongly disagree). Cronbach's alphas ranged from 0.80 (Openness to Experience, student sample) to 0.88 (Extraversion, employee sample).

4.2.2. Equity sensitivity

Equity sensitivity was measured using the Equity Preference Questionnaire (EPQ) (Sauley & Bedeian, 2000) which consists of 16 items. Respondents are asked to indicate the number that best represents their agreement with each listed item from 1 (strongly disagree) to 5 (strongly agree). Studies have shown the EPQ to have superior convergent and content validity when compared to Huseman et al.'s (1985) equity sensitivity instrument (Shore & Strauss, 2008). The Cronbach's alpha was 0.89 for the student sample and 0.83 for the employee sample, and the samples did not significantly differ in scores on the EPQ (M = 3.70 vs. M = 3.73; F(1, 380) = 0.19, p = 0.660).

4.2.3. Workplace deviance

WD was assessed with Bennett and Robinson's (2000) Workplace Deviance scale. This scale consists of 19 items on a scale from 1 (Never) to 7 (Daily), and measures the extent to which they have engaged in each of the listed behaviors at work in the last year. The Cronbach's alpha was 0.86 for the student sample and 0.96 for the employee sample. WD scores were not significantly different across the two samples (M = 1.84 vs. M = 1.77; F(1, 362) = 0.52, p = 0.473).

4.2.4. Organizational citizenship behavior

OCB was measured using the 16-item Lee and Allen (2002) scale. Responses were provided on a 7-point Likert scale rating from 1 (Never) to 7 (Always). The Cronbach's alpha was 0.92 and 0.95 for the student and employee sample, respectively, and scores did not differ between samples (M = 4.73 vs. M = 4.79; F(1, 361) = 0.28, p = 0.597).

4.3. Analyses

Path analysis using Mplus (v. 7.4; Muthén & Muthén, 1998–2012) was employed to assess associations between the exogenous personality variables (i.e., Honesty-Humility, Agreeableness, Conscientiousness) and endogenous workplace outcomes (i.e., OCBs, WD). Path analysis is a subset of structural equation modelling that allows for the simultaneous modelling of related regression relationships (Muthén & Muthén, 1998–2012). For this analysis, maximum likelihood (ML) estimation and bootstrapping were used to calculate the indirect effects, bootstrapped standard errors, and 95% confidence intervals for all relationships. Estimates are considered significant if zero does not fall within bias corrected confidence intervals. The strength of each relationship is presented as a β weight, or partial regression coefficient, representing the weight after each variable has been standardized to have a mean of zero and an SD of one to eliminate unit measure differences (Streiner, 2005).2 In the follow up analyses to test direct effects (i.e., if there remains a relationship between a trait and OCB/WD after accounting for shared variance with equity sensitivity), due to the number of parameters in the model, we were only able to specify the relationships for one trait at a time. For example, we tested a model adding in only the direct effects of Honesty-Humility on OCB/WD. In the next model we removed these effects and examined only the added direct effects for Conscientiousness, then Agreeableness.

5.1. Correlation analyses

5.1.1. Personality and equity sensitivity

Honesty-Humility was positively correlated with scores on the EPQ in both the student sample (r = 0.46, p < 0.001) and employee sample (r = 0.66, p < 0.001), such that individuals higher in Honesty-Humility scored more towards the Benevolent end of the scale. Similarly, in both samples, Conscientiousness was also positively correlated with scores on the EPQ (r = 0.39, p < 0.001 and r = 0.51, p < 0.001 in the student and employee samples, respectively). Finally, in the employee sample, Agreeableness was positively correlated to scores on the EPQ (r = 0.36, p < 0.001) but this relationship was not significant in the student sample. Together, this supports Hypotheses 1a and b, with mixed support for Hypothesis 1c. Although not hypothesized, in the employee sample, Extraversion (r = 0.33, p < 0.001) and Openness (r = 0.44, p < 0.001) both positively correlated with EPQ scores.

5.1.2. Hypothesized correlates of OCB and WD

Consistent with Hypotheses 2a and b, in both samples the EPQ was positively correlated with OCB (r = 0.40, p < 0.001 and r = 0.34, p < 0.001 for the student and employee samples), and negatively correlated with WD (r = − 0.48, p < 0.001 and r = − 0.34, p < 0.001). In the employee sample, Honesty-Humility (r = 0.14, p < 0.05), Agreeableness (r = 0.25, p < 0.001), and Conscientiousness (r = 0.18, p < 0.05) were all positively correlated with OCB, but were not significantly related in the student sample. In terms of WD, Honesty-Humility (r = − 0.34, p < 0.001 and r = − 0.35, p < 0.001 for the student and employee sample), Conscientiousness (r = − 0.16, p < 0.05 and r = − 0.38, p < 0.001 for the student and employee sample), and Agreeableness (r = − 0.25, p < 0.001 and r = − 0.22, p < 0.001 for the student and employee sample) were negatively correlated with WD.

5.2.1. Primary model

Consistent with Hypotheses 1a and b, in both the student and employee samples respectively, Honesty-Humility (β = 0.44, 95% CI [0.32, 0.55] and β = 0.49, 95% CI [0.36, 0.61) and Conscientiousness (β = 0.35, 95% CI [0.22, 0.47] and β = 0.30, 95% CI [0.17, 0.42]) had significant paths to equity sensitivity. The path from Agreeableness to equity sensitivity was not significant in either sample, contrary to Hypothesis 1c. In terms of outcomes, equity sensitivity was positively associated with OCB (β = 0.41, 95% CI [0.24, 0.56] and β = 0.35, 95% CI [0.21, 0.47]) and negatively associated with WD (β = − 0.49, 95% CI [− 0.61, − 0.36] and β = − 0.35, 95% CI [− 0.44, − 0.26]), supporting Hypotheses 2a and b.

Finally, the indirect effects involving Honesty-Humility and Conscientiousness were significant in the expected directions in both the student and employee samples, respectively. Specifically, Honesty-Humility had a significant positive indirect effect on OCB through equity sensitivity (β = 0.18, 95% CI [0.09, 0.28] and β = 0.17, 95% CI [0.10, 0.25]) and a significant negative effect on WD through equity sensitivity (β = − 0.22, 95% CI [− 0.31, − 0.13] and β = − 0.17, 95% CI [− 0.23, − 0.12]). Similarly, Conscientiousness also exhibited a positive indirect effect on OCB (β = 0.14, 95% CI [0.08, 0.22] and β = 0.10, 95% CI [0.05, 0.17]) and negative effect on WD (β = − 0.17, 95% CI = − 0.24, − 0.11] and β = − 0.11, 95% CI [− 0.17, − 0.05]) through equity sensitivity. Agreeableness did not have a significant indirect effect on either OCB or WD.

5.2.2. Direct effects

When direct effects are added for Honesty-Humility, in both the student and employee sample, Honesty-Humility is a significant negative predictor of WD (β = − 0.21, 95% CI [− 0.36, − 0.05] and β = − 0.21, 95% CI [− 0.36, − 0.06]), such that both the indirect and direct paths are significant. On the other hand, the direct paths from Honesty-Humility to OCB (β = − 0.05, 95% CI [− 0.21, 0.11] and β = − 0.15, 95% CI [− 0.35, 0.04]) were not significant after accounting for the relationship with equity sensitivity. Similarly, the direct paths for Conscientiousness with OCB (β = − 0.04, 95% CI [− 0.19, 0.19] and β > 0.01, 95% CI [− 0.15, 0.15]) in both samples, and WD (β = 0.05, 95% CI [− 0.11, 0.20]) in the student sample, were not significant after accounting for equity sensitivity. However, in the employee sample Conscientiousness retained a direct negative effect on WD (β = − 0.27, 95% CI [− 0.37, − 0.16]). Finally, when direct effects were added in for Agreeableness, Agreeableness had a significant negative direct path to WD (β = − 0.17, 95% CI [− 0.24, − 0.11] and β = − 0.11, 95% CI [− 0.17, − 0.05]) in both samples.

6. Discussion

The present study was aimed at increasing our understanding of how and why certain personality traits impact important workplace behaviors. Previous research has found traits such as Honesty-Humility, Agreeableness, and Conscientiousness to relate to workplace behaviors such as OCB and WD (e.g., Berry et al., 2007, Borman et al., 2001, Lee et al., 2005). Inspired by recent findings that several of these traits are associated with an individual's workplace input/output orientation, or equity sensitivity, we hypothesized that exchange and equity based views may help to understand why individuals with certain broad traits may engage in OCB and WD. This is consistent with past theory, which has noted that OCB and WD can be viewed as discretionary behaviors that are rooted in exchange (i.e., they are means of increasing or decreasing one's inputs or outputs in response to situations).

In general, we found robust support for a number of our hypotheses. Specifically, echoing the results of Woodley et al. (2016), we found that individual differences in Honesty-Humility and Conscientiousness (and to a lesser extent Agreeableness) are associated with different views and preferences around equity in the workplace. Namely, individuals higher on these traits tend to score more towards the Benevolent end of equity sensitivity, such that they seem to be willing to give more or be more input-oriented/focused, whereas individuals low on these traits are more likely to be Entitled, or focused on what they can get (i.e., are output oriented). Moreover, we found that the association between these traits and equity sensitivity helped to understand the relationship between Honesty-Humility and Conscientiousness and WD and OCB. Together, these findings indicate that Honesty-Humility and Conscientiousness are important in understanding individual's norms around equity, exchange, and their propensity to engage in discretionary workplace behaviors.

While the results were quite clear for Honesty-Humility and Conscientiousness, the hypotheses involving Agreeableness received much less support. In terms of why this might be the case, we offer some thoughts. First, while Agreeableness has been found to be related to equity in the past literature, this typically accounts for a much smaller percentage of variance in equity than does Honesty-Humility and Conscientiousness (Woodley et al., 2016). Second, in the employee sample, Agreeableness was correlated with equity sensitivity, but not after accounting for the other traits in the path model. Follow up analyses on the employee sample including only Agreeableness did yield support for the hypotheses, with Agreeableness positively predicting equity sensitivity (β = 0.38, 95% CI [0.25, 0.50]) and having a positive indirect effect on OCB (0.13, 95% CI [0.06, 0.21]) and negative indirect effect on WD (− 0.13, 95% CI [− 0.19, − 0.08]). However, these findings did not generalize to the student sample. As such, it appears that Agreeableness does impact OCB and WD through its association with equity, but overall the associations between HEXACO Agreeableness and equity seem to be less robust, and not incremental to variance explained by other traits.

The results of the models including direct effects show that while the associations with equity sensitivity are important in understanding these relationships between broad traits and workplace behaviors, there remains some unaccounted for direct effects. For instance, Conscientiousness was still related to WD in the employee sample, and Honesty-Humility had significant indirect and direct paths to WD in both samples. As such, future research should simultaneously examine additional mechanisms that could explain these associations. For instance, some studies have shown moral disengagement to help explain the relationship between traits and unethical or counterproductive behaviors (Ogunfowora, Bourdage, & Nguyen, 2013). Finally, Agreeableness was not robustly associated with WD or OCB through its association with equity sensitivity, but did have direct relationships with WD, and, in one sample, a correlation with OCB. This indicates that differences in equity sensitivity are not particularly helpful in understanding the association of this trait with OCB/WD. Future research should examine additional mechanisms that may link Agreeableness to these behaviors.

6.1. Limitations, implications, and future directions

Although the present study demonstrated some interesting findings that inform the nature of the association between broad personality traits and workplace behaviors, there are a number of limitations and future directions. For instance, although the student sample used a time-lagged study design to try to reduce common method bias, both samples used solely self-report data. Nonetheless, past research has shown some of the relationships to be robust across rating sources, including the finding by Woodley et al. (2016) that many of the associations between personality and equity sensitivity generalize when using peer reports of personality.

In addition, although the present study focused on two classes of particularly important workplace behavior (OCB and WD), the fact that these prominent broad personality traits are associated with individual differences in equity sensitivity can inform how these traits would relate to additional outcomes, particularly those associated with exchange. For example, equity sensitivity has been shown to impact reactions to psychological contract breach (Shih & Chuang, 2013), attitudes such as organizational commitment, job satisfaction, and turnover intention (King & Miles, 1994), and procedural and interactional justice perceptions and reactions to management explanations (Kickul, Gundry, & Posig, 2005). These relationships may indicate that individuals low in Honesty-Humility and Conscientiousness may have similar reactions and perceptions in the workplace. Future research can use these findings to investigate further situations and their association with attitudinal and behavioral outcomes for individuals with these traits.

Finally, these findings also demonstrate some potential practical implications. First, these findings add to the body of literature indicating the importance of hiring individuals high in Honesty-Humility and Conscientiousness (e.g., for a review see Ashton and Lee, 2007, Lee et al., 2008). For instance, individuals high on these two traits tend to be more likely to be viewed as ethical leaders (De Vries, 2012). In addition, from the perspective of a manager, understanding that personality is associated with norms around equity (and subsequent workplace behaviors) may help to understand how and why certain individuals may react to decisions in the workplace, and how better to frame those conversations and decisions. For instance, when dealing with employees who are low in Honesty-Humility and Conscientiousness, it may be particularly important to frame the benefits of that decision for the individual. In line with this, decisions around certain areas may be differentially important. Kickul and Lester (2001) found that entitled individuals respond more negatively to “unfairness” in certain areas, specifically, tangible extrinsic outcomes (i.e., pay; benefits) as well as breaches surrounding growth and development. On the other hand, benevolents were more concerned with breaches surrounding autonomy and control. As such, managers may be mindful that decisions in certain areas may be particularly sensitive for some individuals.
