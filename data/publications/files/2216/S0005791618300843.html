Psychopathy subfactors distinctively predispose to dispositional and state-level of sadistic pleasure

Abstract

Background and objectives

Sadistic pleasure — the enjoyment of harm-infliction to others — can have devastating interpersonal and societal consequences. The current knowledge on non-sexual, subclinical forms of sadistic pleasure is poor. The present study therefore focussed on the personality correlates of sadistic pleasure and investigated the relationship between the different subcomponents of psychopathy and both dispositional and state-level sadistic pleasure.

Method

N = 120 males drawn from a community sample filled out questionnaires to assess their level of psychopathy and dispositional sadism. Then, participants engaged in a bug-grinder procedure in which they were led to believe that they were killing pill bugs. The positive affect they reported after ostensibly killing the bugs served as measures of sadistic pleasure. The bug-grinding task was repeated a second time after installing either a positive victim attitude combined with giving human names to the bugs, or a negative victim attitude combined with labeling the bugs with numbers.

Results

Although the Self-centred Impulsivity component of psychopathy had some relevance to sadism, it was the Coldheartedness subscale that showed the strongest relationship to sadistic pleasure. Specifically, increased Coldheartedness was uniquely related to more positive affect, along with less guilt after bug grinding.

Limitations

Drawbacks of the study include the unique reliance on a male, community sample, and the potential impact of demand characteristics, including a suggestion that the participant put at least some bugs into the grinder.

Conclusions

Our findings underscore the differential predictive value of psychopathy components for sadistic pleasure. Coldheartedness can be considered especially disturbing because of its unique relationship to harm-infliction of the most irreversible nature (i.e. killing), and gaining pleasure out of it.

1. Introduction

Sadism refers to the pleasure that can arise from the physical or emotional suffering of others (Davies & O'Meara, 2007; Holt & Strack, 1999; MacCulloch, Snowden, Wood, & Mills, 1983; Webber, Schimel, Martens, Hayes, & Faucher, 2013). The vast majority of sadism research focusses on sexual sadism, with an estimated 2–5% of people obtaining sexual gratification from inflicting pain on others (Arndt, Foehl, & Good, 1985; Crépault & Couture, 1980; Hunt, 1974). This might have contributed to the common belief that sadism is a rather rare phenomenon. In everyday life though, enjoyment of cruel behavior is suggested by the popularity of violent movies, violent video games, and brutal sports (Baumeister & Campbell, 1999; Buckels, Jones, & Paulhus, 2013). Such subclinical forms of sadism have been reported by around 7% of student samples (O'Meara, Davies, & Barnes-Holmes, 2004). Actual prevalence may be somewhat higher, given that striving for social desirability may bias responses toward understating how much one enjoys performing or witnessing aggression (Vigil-Colet, Ruiz-Pamies, Anguiano-Carrasco, & Lorenzo-Seva, 2012; Riggs, Murphy, & O'Leary, 1989). Some authors even claim that most individuals have the capacity to indulge in the rush of pleasure that is evoked by sadistic behavior (Baumeister & Campbell, 1999). Sadistic pleasure is mostly assessed at a trait level through self-report, but some recent studies using the so-called bug grinding paradigm have measured state sadistic pleasure using self-reported affect measures after participants were led to believe they killed bugs by putting them into a grinder (Martens, Kosloff, Greenberg, Landau, & Schmader, 2007; Martens, Kosloff, & Jackson, 2010).

One personality constellation that has been linked to sadism is psychopathy. Psychopathy is characterized by deficits in emotional function and antisocial behavior. Although there is some debate about the underlying factor structure of the psychopathy construct, there is good support for the triarchic model of psychopathy (Patrick, Fowles, & Kreuger, 2009) This model identifies boldness (including dominance, emotional stability and venturesomeness), disinhibition (deficient inhibitory control), and meanness (callousness and aggressive resource seeking) as core psychopathic features. The triarchic model has also been supported in psychopathy research using non-clinical samples that typically relies on assessment instruments excluding reference to criminal and antisocial behavior (Drislane, Patrick, & Arsal, 2014). One example is the Psychopathy Personality Inventory (PPI, Lilienfeld & Widows, 2005), which consists of the three factors of Fearless dominance, Self-centred impulsivity, and Coldheartedness. Around 25% of prisoners are assumed to be psychopathic (Assadi et al., 2006; Hare, 1991). Taxometric studies suggest that psychopathy is a dimensional construct existing on a continuum, rather than a qualitatively distinct category (Edens, Marcus, Lilienfeld, & Poythress, 2006; Guay, Ruscio, Knight, & Hare, 2007; Marcus, John, & Edens, 2004). Using self-report information or evidence from the crime scene, 83% of psychopaths were found to have committed sadistic acts (Porter, Woodworth, Earle, Drugge, & Boer, 2003). Psychopathic traits in male offenders have been linked with increased self-reported sadistic behavior (Hart, Forth, & Hare, 1991; Holt & Strack, 1999) and cruelty to animals (Kavanagh, Signal, & Taylor, 2013). Some recent studies focused on psychopathic traits in community sample, and also evidenced a positive relationship with self-reported sadism (Međedović & Knežević, 2018; Plouffe, Saklofske, & Smith, 2017).

While there is some evidence on the relationship between psychopathy and sadistic pleasure, not much is known about the underlying mechanisms that drive this relation. One candidate mechanism is that of victim attitude (that is, the perpetrator's evaluative feelings about the victim). The subcomponent of victim attitude that has been studied most extensively is that of perceived similarity. Increasing incidental similarity between a participant and a confederate (e.g., by informing participants that they share their first name, birthday, or fingerprint) has been shown to increase preparedness to comply with requests or donate money (Burger, Messian, Patel, del Prado, & Anderson, 2004) and reduce aggression (Konrath, Bushman, & Campbell, 2006). Martens et al. (2007) showed that participants who perceived themselves as more similar to bugs killed fewer bugs during the self-paced extermination task. Another subcomponent that might be related to animal-related victim attitudes is ecological usefulness. One study using social wasps for example, found that lowering the ecological value of a specific wasp made it a target of increased aggression by the other wasps (Bohm, Ruth, & Schramm, 2016). These findings imply that a negative victim attitude likely engenders increased bug killing and possibly sadistic pleasure.

The current study had two main goals. The first one was to examine the relationship between sadistic pleasure and psychopathy. Sadistic pleasure was assessed on a dispositional level using self-report, and operationalized on a state-level as increased positive affect following bug killing. In order to increase specificity, we also assessed the levels of negative affect, including shame and guilt. The latter emotions have been shown to be related to killing behavior (see Martens et al., 2007). Our trait measure of psychopathy offered multiple subfactors, so we were able to investigate which of them accounted for substantial variance in killing — thereby to illuminate what aspects of psychopathy contribute to sadism. We hypothesize that particularly the Coldheartedness factor of psychopathy will be related to increased sadistic pleasure, given this factor is characterized by lack of empathic concern (Berg, Hecht, Latzman, & Lilienfeld, 2015).

The second goal was to investigate the impact of experimentally inducing a positive attitude combined with anthropomorphism or negative attitude combined with numerical labelling toward pill bugs. We expected psychopathy to correlate positively with dispositional sadistic pleasure, with number of bugs killed, and with positive affect following bug grinding, while a negative relation was expected with guilt. Next, the negative attitude condition/numerical labelling was generally expected to cause increased killing behavior when compared to the positive attitude/anthropomorphism condition. Finally, increased psychopathy levels were expected to moderate the relationship between attitude condition and both killing behavior and affect. Specifically, we expected that increased psychopathy would lead to increased bug killing and subsequent positive affect, along with decreased guilt in participants assigned to the negative attitude condition.

2. Method

2.1. Participants

A total of N = 120 males, aged between 17 and 75 (Mage = 32.22, SD = 16.53), participated in the study. Age was positively skewed in this sample, and there was no difference in age between the positive and negative attitude groups, t=.49, p=.48. Only male participants were recruited because men generally exhibit higher levels of sadism (Freiman & Widiger, 1989; Fuller, Blashield, & Miller, 1992; Gay & Fiester, 1991) and psychopathy (Coid, 1998; Coid, Yang, Ullrich, Roberts, & Hare, 2009; Cooke, 1996; Hare, 1970, 1991; Lilienfeld & Andrews, 1996; Moran, 1999) than women. This allowed us to avoid confounding gender effects as well as possible floor effects. Participants were recruited by advertising flyers and through an online subscribing internet portal. Only males with a basic comprehension of the Dutch or English language were included. Accordingly, the experiment was performed in Dutch (N = 83) or English (N = 37). Sixty-seven participants (55.8%) had a Dutch nationality, 37 (30.8%) were German, and 16 (13.3%) were of other nationalities. The majority of the participants were students (59.2%). Other participants were employed (25%), or unemployed (15.8%). With respect to the educational level of the sample, 0.8% was regarded as low, 57.5% was regarded as average and 41.7% was regarded as high. The majority was single (73.3%). A priori power analyses based on correlations with an effect size = 0.30, α = 0.05, power of 0.8 (Buckels et al., 2013) and anticipated dropout of 10%, indicated a required sample size of at least N = (6 + (Z1-β+Z1-α/2)2 * (1-ρ2/ρ2))/(1-0.1) = 94.75 to detect a relationship between psychopathy and sadistic pleasure. To detect a between-subjects effect of attitude manipulation on sadistic pleasure with an effect size = 0.68 (Marten et al., 2007), α = 0.05, power of 0.8 (Buckels et al., 2013) and anticipated dropout of 10%, we required a sample size of at least N1 = N2 = (Z1-β+Z1-α/2)2 * (2/d2))/(1-0.1) = 38.

2.2. Measures

2.2.1. Sadistic pleasure

2.2.1.1. Dispositional sadistic pleasure

The Short Sadistic Impulse Scale (SSIS; O'Meara, Davies, & Hammond, 2011) is a 10-item self-report inventory, derived from the 49-item Sadistic Attitudes and Behaviors Scale SABS (Davies & Hand, 2003), which measures a dispositional tendency to enjoy hurting others. Sample items are “I would enjoy hurting someone physically, sexually, or emotionally” and “I have humiliated others to keep them in line”. Items have to be rated on a Visual Analogue Scale (VAS) ranging from 0 (not at all applicable) to 100 (absolutely applicable). Previous studies have supported the reliability and concurrent validity of the SSIS, with an internal consistency estimate of α = 0.86 and a negative relationships with empathy, r = −0.27 (O'Meara et al., 2011).

2.2.1.2. State-level sadistic pleasure

To measure sadistic pleasure on a state level, participants were led to believe they ground pill bugs, after which their affect level was measured. Based on the faux-bug-killing paradigm of Martens et al. (2007), we developed a modified plastic coffee grinder with a metal tube attached to its top (see appendix A). There was a funnel at the end of the tube into which the pill bugs could easily be dropped. Within the grinder, the mechanism that activated the cutting blades was removed and replaced with a sound electrode that simulated the sound of bugs being ground up. The original electromotor was retained to further simulate a grinding experience by producing a small vibration. Underneath the grinder, a small box was secured in order to collect the bugs. On the side of the grinder, a red activation button was attached, simulating the grinding upon pressing.

2.2.2. Affect

Participants’ affect was assessed with the Profiles of Mood States-Short Form (POMS-SF; McNair, Lorr, & Droppleman, 1971) and the State Shame and Guilt Scale (SSGS; Marschall, Sanftner, & Tangney, 1994). The POMS is an adjective checklist to assess transient, distinct mood states. Thirty-two items had to be rated on a 5-point scale ranging from 0 (not at all) to 4 (extremely). Two POMS-SF subscales that are relevant for the current study are the positive (i.e. vigour) subscale, and the negative affect scale. Internal consistency estimates of the POMS-SF total- and subscales were comparable to those of the original POMS and ranged between α = 0.76 and 0.95 (Curran, Andrykowski, & Studts, 1995). SSGS items (e.g. “I feel remorse, regret”; “I cannot stop thinking about something bad I have done”) had to be rated on a 5-point scale ranging from 1 (not feeling this way at all) to 5 (feeling this way very strongly). Internal reliability estimates of the SSGS vary between α = 0.79 and 0.86 (Dorahy et al., 2013; Ghatavi, Nicolson, MacDonald, Osher, & Levitt, 2002), and the 5-month stability coefficient was x = 0.53 (Tilghman-Osborne, Cole, Felton, & Ciesla, 2008). To be able to assess how the participants felt about the pill bugs, we developed two additional questions (based on Buckels et al., 2013) on how negative and loathsome the participants found the pill bugs. Both items had to be rated on 100 mm VAS scales.

2.2.3. Psychopathy

Psychopathy traits were assessed with the Psychopathic Personality Inventory-Revised (PPI-R, Lilienfeld & Widows, 2005). The PPI-R is self-report questionnaire consisting of 154 items that have to be scored using a 4-point Likert scale (1 = false, 2 = mostly false, 3 = mostly true, and 4 = true). Factor analysis has indicated two main factors; Fearless dominance (FD) and Self-centred impulsivity (SCI). Seven out of eight subscales of the PPI load on these two scales, i.e. fearlessness, social potency, and stress immunity; and Machiavellian egocentricity, carefree nonplanfulness, blame externalization and impulsive nonconformity, respectively. The eighth factor of Coldheartedness did not load on either of these two factors (Benning, Patrick, Hicks, Blonigen, & Krueger, 2003). Satisfactory internal validity (PPI-R Total, α = 0.91; PPI-FD, α = 0.91; PPI-SCI, α = 0.89, PPI-Coldheartedness, α = 79), construct validity (correlates ranging from r = 0.18-0.68 with other psychopathy measures), and external validity has been reported for PPI-R factors (Uzieblo, Verschuere, Van den Bussche, & Crombez, 2010). Additionally, test-retest reliability proved to be high (PPI-total, r = 0.93; PPI-R FD, r = 0.91; and PPI-R SCI, r = 0.90 Sandler, 2007).

2.2.4. Positive/negative attitude condition

Participants were assigned to either the positive or the negative attitude condition. These conditions were constructed to install positive versus negative attitudes about pill bugs. Participants read bogus scientific articles including a general description of pill bugs, plus information about their reproduction habits, social behavior and ecological value. In the positive attitude condition, the given information aimed to induce a positive view of the bugs, such as by emphasizing their similarity to humans and their high ecological value, whereas the negative attitude article aimed to induce a negative view, depicting their dissimilarity to humans and ecological harm. Both articles were formulated specifically for the purpose of this study (see appendixes B and C). To check the validity of the two attitude manipulations, 20 volunteers (10 female, 10 male) with mean age of M = 35.9 years, SD = 18.0, rated on a 100 mm Visual Analogue Scale ranging from `extremely positive' to `extremely negative' how they perceived pill bugs after reading the articles. The pill bugs were rated significantly more negatively after reading the negative article than after reading the positive article, M=77.15, SD=18.5 vs M=26.45, SD=24.1, respectively, t (19)=6.79, p < .001.

2.3. Procedure

Participants were told that the study was about personality and behavior toward animals. It was explained that the study aimed to investigate the role of exterminators who deal with animals, and that it involved engaging in a bug extermination task. In the first part of the experiment, after signing informed consent, participants filled out the POMS to measure baseline mood state, the PPI-R and the SSIS on a PC. In the meanwhile, the experimenter set up the bug grinder experiment in another room. We selected common pill bugs (i.e., porcellio scaber) with segmented exoskeletons, dark brown in color and measuring approximately 1 cm in length. Easy accessibility and manageability, and their neutral appearance allowed compatibility with both positive and negative (manipulated) attitudes.

Each bug was placed into a separate plastic cup. A tray with eight cups was placed next to the bug grinder. As part of the cover story, the participant was informed that a grinding apparatus would be used because poison sprays and chemicals are not permitted within the university buildings for health and safety reasons. The following instructions were given: “For this task, I want to ask you to put at least four1 pill bugs, separately, into the funnel. However, you can also deposit more than four bugs. After this, press the red activation button. You have approximately 1 min for this task. I will be waiting outside if you have any questions.” A minimum of four bugs was decided in order to avoid floor effects and ensure sufficient variance of the amount of ground up bugs. Participants were instructed not to touch the bugs but to use the plastic cups to put the bugs into the grinder. After the participant completed the bug grinder task, the experimenter re-entered the room and gave the participant the POMS to assess post current state (post 1), as well as the negativity and disgust rating scale to record their perception of the pill bugs (see also Buckels et al., 2013).

For the second part of the experiment, participants were randomly assigned to one of the two attitude conditions. It was explained that they had to redo the bug grinding, but this time after carefully reading a scientific article about pill bugs. The experimenter explained that he would ask questions about the article later in the study to ensure attentive reading. After reading either the positive of negative article, the participants performed a non-demanding distraction task of 3 min on the computer in which they were to indicate how often a square on the computer screen turned red.

Next, the participants were given a piece of paper on which they had 3 min to write down all the facts they remembered from the pill bug article. After that, participants in the positive attitude condition were asked to think of a suitable name for each of the bugs and write those on the plastic cups, to increase anthropomorphization (see also Buckels et al., 2013). In the negative attitude condition participants had to write numbers on the plastic cups that held the pill bugs. After doing so, participants were asked to again complete the bug grinding (i.e. using the same instruction; put in at least four bugs, with a minute time frame). Next, participants filled out the POMS again (post 2) and the bug rating scale, followed by an exit interview, in which they were thoroughly probed for suspicion. In the debriefing, participants were fully informed about the study's purpose. It was stressed that they had not actually killed any bugs. Participants were shown that the bugs they had inserted into the grinder were all still alive. The participants received either 15€ in gift vouchers or university participation points after completing the experiment. The pill bugs were safely released after the study. This study was approved by the ethics committee of Maastricht University (ECP-138 16-03-2014).

2.4. Statistical analyses

Nine participants expressed suspicion about the cover story and were removed from all analyses except in analyses of baseline measures. Variables violating the normality assumption were examined using non-parametric tests. Intercorrelations between all study variables were assessed using Pearson's or Spearman's correlations. The impact of psychopathy factor scores on bug killing and affect was assessed with regression analyses. Three psychopathy subscales were entered as predictors, controlling for baseline level of the dependent variables.

With regard to affect, regression analyses showed that psychopathy coldheartedness factor significantly predicted increased positive affect, decreased negative affect and guilt; while psychopathy total and factor FD score predicted decreased guilt (Table 4).

3.3. Effect of attitude manipulation

ANOVA with condition and baseline variable level as predictors showed a main effect of condition on bug valence after the second grinding task, implying that participants assigned to the negative attitude condition felt significantly more negatively toward the bugs, M=7.58, SD=15.62, than those assigned to the positive attitude condition, M=-14.75, SD=18.77; F(1, 108)=54.75*, p= < .001, d=.70. Overall, these findings show that our attitude manipulation articles were effective in inducing a distinct attitude, valence and disgust rating of pill bugs, as well as grinding behavior.

4. Discussion

Sadistic cruelty may be fairly uncommon in everyday human society, but when it does occur, it is often so destructive to victims that it is important to understand despite its relative rarity. Ethical and practical constraints make it difficult to study genuine interpersonal cruelty in the laboratory. The present investigation did however adapt a simulated bug-killing procedure developed by Martens et al. (2007) to test hypotheses about personality and attitudinal factors that might predispose people to engage in sadistic behavior — and to derive pleasure from doing so.

The present investigation focused primarily on subclinical psychopathy, a trait marked by antisocial behavior and disregard for others’ suffering. We examined three separate aspects of psychopathy. The first factor has been labelled as Fearless Dominance, reflecting lack of fear, self-rated effectiveness at influencing and dominating others, and relative immunity to stress. It does not appear to be what makes psychopaths into sadists. Its correlation with self-reported sadism was near zero (0.04), and it failed to correlate with killing bugs or with feeling good after killing. Its sole impact was a reduction in feeling guilty after killing bugs, and even that only on the first trial.

The second aspect of psychopathy we studied is known as Self-Centred Impulsivity. This aspect combines externalizing blame, an egocentric and Machiavellian approach to interpersonal relations, a carefree indifference to making plans, and an impulsive style of action that refuses to conform to conventional norms and expectations. Our results suggest some relevance to sadism, including a significant correlation with dispositional sadism, and a conditional increase of number of bugs killed (i.e., only following a negative attitude in the 2nd grinding task). However, it was unrelated to number of bugs killed during the 1st grinding task, as well as being unrelated to affect after killing bugs.

The third aspect of psychopathy was Coldheartedness. This captures one of the core aspects of psychopathy, namely the lack of sympathy for victims and the indifference to their suffering (which may facilitate finding sadistic pleasure). It did not correlate with the measure of dispositional sadism — but it did predict killing more bugs (on the first trial). Consistent with the label, people scoring higher on Coldheartedness reported less guilt and less negative affect in general after killing bugs, as well as more positive emotion.

Our findings do provide some challenge to the predictive utility of the SSIS as a self-report measure of sadistic tendencies (O'Meara et al., 2011). People scoring high on that scale did not respond like sadists in our procedure. They did not show elevated rates of killing bugs (though there were weak trends in that direction). Even more important, the sadism scale yielded significant negative correlations with self-reported pleasure after killing bugs — whereas the definition of sadism calls for positive correlations. The sadism scale was unrelated to guilt and showed nonsignificant trends toward more negative affect after killing (thus, again in the wrong direction). To be sure, there are many possible manifestations of sadism, but at least on these animal-related behavioral and emotional measures of animal-related sadistic pleasure it was not a successful predictor of sadism as here operationalized. This seems to be in contrast to Buckels' et al. (2013) findings. They did find the SSIS to correspond to a behavioral preference for bug-grinding compared to other non-appealing tasks such as cleaning toilets, although a different operationalization was used there. Future studies should further address this possible lack of correspondence between dispositional and state-level measures of sadistic pleasure.

Our attitude/anthropomorphism manipulation successfully instilled either positive or negative attitudes toward the bugs. The negative attitude/numbering caused people to kill more bugs than participants in the positive attitude/naming condition, consistent with the view that holding a negative attitude toward victims increases violence toward them. It did not, however, change the affective reactions to killing bugs. Put another way, inducing a negative attitude toward bugs led to more killing but not more enjoyment of killing. The positive attitude/naming manipulation did not exert a therapeutic effect on psychopathy or its subfactors. Instead, the negative manipulation increased killing behavior of higher scoring SCI factor psychopathy individuals. One clinical implication might therefore be to screen people with increased self-centred impulsivity on negative attitude toward (possible) victims, as this may further increase their sadistic behavior toward them.

Around 24% of our sample voluntarily chose to grind more bugs than suggested in the instructions, and this increased up to 42% after a negative attitude manipulation. In addition to this, around 38% reported increased positive affect after the bug grinding. This clearly demonstrates that the presence of both sadistic behavior and sadistic pleasure is not restricted to criminal populations or sexual disorders, but rather should be regarded as a continuous phenomenon that is also present in the general population. So our data support the notion of ‘everyday sadism’ (e.g. Buckels et al., 2013; Paulhus, 2014).

The current study contributes to a rather new field of study, that is, everyday, non-sexual sadism and sadistic pleasure, and the relationship between psychopathy and sadism. Drawbacks of the study include the unique reliance on a male, community sample. Also, in order to avoid floor effects, our instructions suggested that participants should grind up a minimum of four bugs (though not all complied). In some other studies participants were not given any suggestions and hence it was left up to them to decide how many bugs to grind (Buckels et al., 2013; Martens & Kosloff, 2012). The number of bugs ostensibly killed in those studies was quite similar to what we found. Therefore, we doubt that the suggestion to grind four bugs had much impact on our results. Still, the suggestion to kill four bugs was a demand characteristic, and omitting the suggestion might have increased the ecological validity of the sadistic tendency assessment. (Then again, some sadists operate in raiding parties, heavily outnumbered guard details, or killing squads, in which there are suggestions and even orders to harm or kill people.) That said, demand characteristics, or simply a wish to comply with the experimenter's suggestion, may have impacted our results, and we advise future researchers not to provide any suggestions as to the number of bugs they should grind.

One other drawback concerns the two-fold nature of the attitude manipulation (i.e. article content and bug-labelling). It could be argued that this approach actually manipulated two variables at a time, which makes it impossible to know whether the effects were driven by positive attitudes or by anthropomorphism. Future studies should be careful to only manipulate one variable at a time. Finally, future studies should investigate to which degree animal (i.e., bug)-related sadism corresponds to human-related sadism in order to evaluate whether generalizations of the current findings to broader sadistic and aggressive tendencies are warranted. Another promising avenue for future studies would be to analyse how the different subfactors of psychopathy interact in predicting the outcomes in a sufficiently powered sample, as implied by the triarchic model (Patrick & Drislane, 2015).

This study tested and found support for a positive relationship between the personality constellation of psychopathy and sadism. Our findings indicate that within the concept of psychopathy, its Coldheartedness component could be considered especially disturbing because of its unique predictive value for sadistic behavior of the most irreversible nature (i.e. killing), given the co-occurring enjoyment. The Coldheartedness/sadism relationship seemed quite robust, as it was found to be unchangeable following positive victim information, highlighting its potential therapy resistant nature. Enjoyment of harm infliction might serve as a perpetuating factor of aggression for those with increased Coldheartedness. Our results do not fit the conceptualization of psychopathy as a coherent, unitary, one-dimensional construct (Marcus, Fulton, & Edens, 2013). Instead, they support the triarchic conceptualization of psychopathy in which meanness constitutes a separate concept (Patrick, Fowles, & Kreuger, 2009). We thus postulate that Coldheartedness constitutes a central concept that calls for further emphasis (cf. Berg et al., 2015).

General information

Pill bugs are warm-blooded crustaceans. The animals are approximately one and a half centimeters long, half a centimeter wide and black-grey or brown in color. Pill bugs are crustacean isopods; a suborder of arthropods. In the Netherlands, 36 known living species of pill bugs reside. Woodlice originate from the ocean. They carry two large antennae on the head along with a few small aerials in-between. These aerials sensitize pill bugs to painful stimuli. Some scientists believe that these aerials allow pill bugs to sense emotional signals. Pill bugs live in a range of habitats, such as forests or meadows. Even gardens, from which many people may know pill bugs, are suitable habitats.

Reproduction

Prior to mating, the male pill bug seduces its female counterpart by giving ‘presents’ such as food or shelter. Male pill bugs recognize mating-prepared females by their scent. After fertilization, males search for safe places to store the eggs. The young are very sensitive to dehydration and predation. During the entire fertilization and nesting period, the male resides with the female, protecting the nest against enemies. After mating, males stay with the same females for the rest of their lives, nurturing the offspring.

Social behavior

Pill bugs are social animals that often live in large groups. They show such strong ties to their communities, that surviving independently is impossible. For this reason, pill bugs are sometimes referred to as a superorganism. Allied with group members, pill bugs protect eggs and offspring from harm. This alliance allows pill bugs to quickly notice and scare off enemies. Populations of pill bugs maintain a firm division of labor and intensive communication in which they may read emotional signals from their fellow species.

Ecological use

Pill bugs consume dead produce of nature, such as fallen leaves and branches. They contribute to the natural maintenance process in which biological remainders are removed. On top of that, pill bugs are effective against antagonists.

Appendix C. Negative Attitude Bug Article

General information

Pill bugs are cold-blooded crustaceans. The animals are approximately one and a half centimeters long, half a centimeter wide and black-grey or brown in color. Pill bugs are crustacean isopods; a suborder of arthropods. In the Netherlands, 36 known living species of pill bugs reside. Pill bugs originate from the ocean. Even though pill bugs have left the ocean, they've remained their gills in the form of adjusted ‘legs’ on the rear body. The body consists of hard, overlapping plates (segments) which immunize them to pain. Pill bugs live in a range of habitats, such as forests or meadows. Even gardens, from which many people may know pill bugs, are suitable habitats.

Reproduction

Male pill bugs mate with multiple females several times a year. After fertilization, the male will seek for new mating-prepared females. The males are viewed as unattractive partners, as scientists have often reported cases of rape where the female is hurt and thus unable to resist. After mating the female molts, creating a brood pouch in which several dozen eggs are deposited. The female carries the eggs for several weeks, followed by birth of the nymphs who remain in the pouch for several days. Pill bugs are mainly known for eating the nymphs; 90% of the newborn will not survive the first week. After this critical first week the offspring are abandoned without receiving parental guidance in searching for food and shelter.

Social behavior

Pill bugs are solitary animals. When they do group with others, this is mainly due to the fact that the food supply is controlled by a group of others. After joining, pill bugs will claim the food and leave when the food storage is depleted. At birth, offspring are also at risk of being eaten by others. Pill bugs find each other's company for the sole purpose of safety. Massive grouping may cause infections that can be transmitted to other animals and plants. Moreover, a territorial battle may occur, in which male pill bugs penetrate and murder the female to guarantee their place in hierarchy. For insect exterminators, pill bugs are known as extremely persistent pests; once they are in your garden they are extremely difficult to remove since they are good at hiding and multiply rapidly.

Ecological use

Pill bugs are classified as harmful insects due to the stalking, impairing and polluting of crops and plants. In addition, they may spread dangerous diseases when eaten by animals who serve as food to humans (e.g., chicken).

Closer inspection of Table 3 revealed that if the number of bug kills was zero on the first trial in the positive condition, it was always zero on the second trial.
