Personality and Individual Differences

Volume 140, 1 April 2019, Pages 52-56

Destroying things for pleasure: On the relation of sadism and vandalism

Author links open overlay panelStefanPfattheicheraJohannesKelleraGoranKnezevicb
https://doi.org/10.1016/j.paid.2018.03.049Get rights and content

Abstract

Vandalism, the intentional act to destruct or to deface a property not one's own, has received surprisingly little attention in psychological research so far. The present contribution addresses this gap and applies a basic personality perspective to vandalism. Here, we focused on one specific manifestation of vandalism, that is, destroying things for pleasure. In the present study (N = 221), we assessed individual differences in sadism, the dark triad of psychopathy, narcissism, and Machiavellianism as well as the HEXACO personality model. Multiple regression analyses revealed that individual differences in sadism predict vandalistic acts that are executed for pleasure. This relation was shown to be independent of the dark triad and the HEXACO dimensions. As such, the present contribution suggests that sadistic orientations might play a role in vandalistic acts that are executed for pleasure. Implications for research on sadism and vandalism are discussed.

1. Introduction

In July 2017, the summit of the 20 major national economies took place in Hamburg, Germany. The summit was accompanied by political protests and extreme forms of damage to property – cars were burned and shops were completely destroyed. These acts of vandalism were first solely ascribed to radical left-wing groups. Later in the reportage about the summit, however, prominent media channels came to the conclusion that radical left-wing actions played only a minor role. Instead, it was frequently reported that a central force in the acts of vandalism were non-political individuals who destroyed property for pleasure (Kempkens, Levy, Stark, Tieg, & Widmann, 2017).

Surprisingly, empirical psychological research on vandalism is still in its infancy. The vast majority of psychological research in the area of violence is focused on violence towards human beings (i.e., aggression). In fact, the number of journal articles listed in PsycINFO referring to “vandalism” is 402 (with a major focus on vandalism at schools, often non-quantitative), whereas the number of journal articles referring to “aggression” is 9941 (date: November 28, 2017). The present research addresses this gap and applies a basic personality perspective to vandalism. Specifically, the question is asked which basic personality orientations predict vandalistic acts that are executed for pleasure. To address this question, we include a broad personality model (the HEXACO) and dark personality traits (i.e., the dark tetrad) in our analyses, with a specific focus on sadistic orientations.

Vandalism can be defined as the intentional act to destruct or to deface a property not one's own (Goldstein, 1996). This definition includes a large number of different behavioral manifestations of vandalism, varying in social and monetary costs. Examples include broken windows, destruction of school property, (racial) graffiti, littering, burning cars, or destroying artworks (Goldstein, 1996). The large number of different behavioral manifestations of vandalism is accompanied by a large number of discussed motives driving different forms of vandalism (see, e.g., Cohen, 1971; Coffield, 1991). These motives range from curiosity, excitement, and creativity to hostility, frustration, and revenge (Goldstein, 1996). Since there exists a huge diversity in vandalistic acts and motives, one must specify which type of vandalism is the target of discussion or research. In the present contribution, we examined vandalistic tendencies that are executed for pleasure. In fact, recent (non-quantitative) research identified pleasure (Allen & Greenberger, 1978; Coffield, 1991) or enjoyment (Csikszentmihalyi & Larsen, 1978) as one goal of vandalism. From this motivational perspective, vandalistic acts serve an instrumental function to approach pleasure.

In the present work, we were particularly interested in the relation of sadistic orientations and vandalistic acts that are executed for pleasure. We argue below that part of the strategies (everyday) sadists use to approach pleasure (i.e., exercise control by means of violating subjects) can also be applied when vandalizing objects. The very essence of sadism is that sadists are motivated to dominate and to control other individuals by harming them because they experience pleasure through their cruelty (Cooke, 2001; Dietz, Hazelwood, & Warren, 1990; Međedović, 2017; O'Meara, Davies, & Hammond, 2011). Sadism emerges not only in the clinical but also in the subclinical context (Chabrol, Melioli, Van Leeuwen, Rodgers, & Goutaudier, 2015), for instance, when killing bugs or harming an unknown person (Buckels, Jones, & Paulhus, 2013); when engaging in trolling, a practice that reflects destructive behavior towards others on the internet (Buckels, Trapnell, & Paulhus, 2014); punishing cooperative interaction partners (Pfattheicher, Keller, & Knezevic, 2017; Pfattheicher & Schindler, 2015); or playing violent video games (Greitemeyer, 2015; Greitemeyer & Sagioglou, 2017). Sadistic motivations are clearly released across a myriad of different contexts.

Conceptualizing vandalism as destroying things for pleasure, it seems possible that sadistic motivations might play a role. When harming other human beings to approach pleasure, a sadistic individual is in a state of dominance and provided with a sense of control. We argue that the same tendencies can also be applied to (material) objects; that is to say, when destroying things, one can also exercise control and engage in violent acts (cf. Fisher & Baron, 1982). From this perspective, people who are motivated to control and harm other individuals for pleasure might also be motivated to exercise control by means of destroying objects for pleasure.

At this point we want to emphasize the conceptual distinction between sadism and destroying objects for pleasure; that is, destroying objects for pleasure must not be equated with sadism. Sadism reflects the tendency to pursue pleasure by dominating and hurting subjects (not objects), maybe primarily because the latter are sentient creatures that can feel pain. As objects cannot be harmed and then feel pain, destroying objects for pleasure is not sadism per se. We merely argue that sadistic tendencies (i.e., pleasure through destructiveness, and dominance in particular) can be applied to objects as well and that individuals who apply these tendencies to subjects also apply these tendencies to objects.

Overall, in the study reported below, we tested the assumption that (everyday) sadism is related to vandalistic acts that are executed for pleasure. We also tested whether sadistic motivations in particular, and not other dark tendencies like the dark triad of psychopathy, narcissism, and Machiavellianism, are related to vandalism. In addition, we included the broad personality model of the HEXACO (Ashton & Lee, 2007) in our analysis.

2. Study

2.1. Method

The study was conducted online via Amazon Mechanical Turk (cf. Buhrmester, Kwang, & Gosling, 2011). We first assessed individuals' dispositions to everyday sadism (Buckels et al., 2014) as well as individuals' proneness to psychopathy, narcissism, and Machiavellianism (Jones & Paulhus, 2014). Then participants completed the HEXACO (Ashton & Lee, 2007) and items assessing vandalism. All responses on the scales were given on a 7-point Likert scale ranging from “I completely disagree” to “I completely agree.” Participants also responded to two attention check items during the course of the study (“This is an attention check item. Please answer: I completely agree.”) Means, standard deviations, and Cronbach's alphas of all scales are displayed in Table 2. Data and syntax of the study are available on the Open Science Framework (OSF; see https://osf.io/d3beq).

2.1.1. Sadism

Everyday sadism was measured using the 18-item Comprehensive Assessment of Sadistic Tendencies scale (CAST; Buckels et al., 2014). The CAST consists of three subscales including items assessing verbal sadism (“I enjoy making jokes at the expense of others”), direct sadism (“I enjoy physically hurting people”), and vicarious sadism (“In video games, I like the realistic blood spurts”).

2.1.2. Dark Triad

Subclinical psychopathy was measured using the 64-item Self-Report Psychopathy Scale III (SRP III; Paulhus, Neumann, & Hare, 2015), Machiavellianism was assessed using the MACH IV (Christie & Geis, 1970), and narcissism was assessed using the Narcissistic Admiration and Rivalry Questionnaire (NARQ; Back et al., 2013).

2.1.3. HEXACO

To assess the HEXACO personality model, we used the 60-item version of the HEXACO Personality Inventory-Revised (HEXACO-60; Ashton & Lee, 2007). The HEXACO-60 consists of 10 items for each of the six HEXACO scales.

2.1.4. Vandalism

We generated six items to assess individuals' tendency to vandalize things for fun. The items can be found in Table 1. All six items were highly inter-correlated (all rs felt between 0.41 and 0.74, all ps < .001); accordingly, Cronbach's alpha was sufficiently high (α = 0.88). Applying an exploratory factor analysis (EFA, principal component analysis), all criteria for the establishing of the relevant number of factors converge to a clear one-factor solution. The first factor explains 63.93% of the variance (eigenvalue 3.78), the second one 11.91% of variance (eigenvalue 0.72). Thus, only one factor had an eigenvalue >1 (Guttman-Kaiser criterion). Parallel analysis suggested the retention of one factor, using as the criterion 99th percentile for eigenvalues obtained on 10,000 randomly generated data sets (the eigenvalues for the first two factors based on the randomly generated sets were 1.37 and 1.21). The smallest averaged squared partial correlation was 0.06 – the first three being 0.32, 0.06 and 0.12 – leading to the conclusion that Velicer's minimum average partial test also points to a one-factor solution. Scree plot is provided on the Open Science Framework (see https://osf.io/d3beq); factor loadings are displayed in Table 1.

2.1.5. Sample

Using G*Power (Faul, Erdfelder, Buchner, & Lang, 2009), a power analysis was conducted to detect at least a small-to-medium effect (r = 0.20), using an alpha-level of 0.05 (two-tailed). Power was set to 0.80 (Cohen, 1992). This power analysis revealed a required sample size of N = 191. We obtained data from 255 individuals of which 221 correctly respond to the two manipulation check items and completed all items (final N = 221; Mage = 37.33; 59.27% women).

3. Results

3.1. Analytic strategy

Given that the dark tetrad of sadism, psychopathy, narcissism, and Machiavellianism, along with Honesty-Humility from the HEXACO, share substantial variance (see Table 2), their zero-order correlations with vandalism are difficult to interpret. Therefore, we applied multiple regression analyses to control for the respective other constructs. Additionally, to account for the multidimensionality of the constructs (e.g., narcissism is composed of admiration and rivalry; Back et al., 2013) we ran all analyses both with the total score as well as using the subscales, while controlling for the other constructs. Given that these analyses result in far too much information to be reported in this article, they can be found on the Open Science Framework (see https://osf.io/d3beq). It turned out that only direct sadism significantly predicts vandalism when all other subscales were also included in the analyses. Accordingly, in the main analyses reported in the following, only the subscales of sadism are reported. For the other constructs, the total score was included in the analyses.

A multiple regression analysis including only the dark tetrad into the regression model revealed that direct sadism and psychopathy independently predicted vandalism (see Table 3, Model 1). Please note, however, that when psychopathy is decomposed to its subscales, only direct sadism remained as a significant predictor (see OSF for this analysis and the Discussion section regarding this point).

4. Discussion

Past research has largely neglected the empirical analysis of vandalism. The present research addresses this remarkable gap by applying a personality perspective to vandalism. In doing so, we focused on a specific form of vandalism, that is, destroying things for pleasure. In our well-powered study, we show that vandalism was correlated with the dark tetrad and with Honesty-Humility, Agreeableness, and Conscientiousness from the HEXACO personality model. Additional regression analyses revealed that direct sadism was the central and strongest predictor of vandalistic acts that are executed for pleasure.

The present work has several implications and contributes to the existing literature in meaningful ways. First, the present contribution creates a solid foundation for future research on vandalism. Basically, we show that the motivation to destroy things for pleasure varies by individual, and we explain a substantial amount of variance in this variation. Future research can extend the present contribution by (a) examining other forms of vandalism, and (b) testing whether sadistic orientations also play a role in these others forms.

The present work further extends research on sadism. Specifically, it is shown that sadistic tendencies not only play a role when it comes to violence against human and non-human creatures, but also when it comes to violence against objects. As violence against objects is also of physical nature, it makes sense that only direct (i.e., physical) sadism predicts vandalism whereas verbal and vicarious sadism only played a minor and non-significant role. Beyond, we have argued that when destroying things, one can also exercise control and engage in violent acts to approach pleasure – reflecting sadistic tendencies. Nonetheless, destroying objects for pleasure and sadism are distinct orientations. Sadism, per definition, reflects the tendency to pursue pleasure by dominating and hurting subjects (not objects). What the study suggests is that those who apply sadistic tendencies (i.e., pleasure through destructiveness, and dominance in particular) to subjects also apply these tendencies to objects.

Additionally, we contribute to previous research discussing the role of sadism in the basic personality spectrum. There are claims to integrate sadism (and the dark triad) into the HEXACO personality model, reflecting low levels of Honesty-Humility, Emotionality, Agreeableness, and Conscientiousness (e.g., Book et al., 2016; Book, Visser, & Volk, 2015; Hodson et al., in press; Muris, Merckelbach, Otgaar, & Meijer, 2017). In contrast, other research shows that sadism can predict unique variance in a variety of outcomes beyond the HEXACO and the dark triad (Chabrol, van Leeuwen, Rodgers, & Séjourné, 2009; Greitemeyer, 2015; Greitemeyer & Sagioglou, 2017; Međedović & Petrović, 2015; Pfattheicher et al., 2017; van Geel, Goemans, Toprak, & Vedder, 2017). Basically, what we argue for is that when it comes to explaining specific destructive behavior, for instance, vandalism that is executed for pleasure, it is not meaningful to apply a broad personality perspective. This is because when one wants to learn about the specific motivation behind (destructive) behavior, one needs to assess motivations in a specific way (e.g., sadistic orientations) rather than in a global way.

In the present case, the negative relations between (a) Honesty-Humility, Agreeableness, Conscientiousness and (b) Vandalism suggest that the absence of the former three fosters the presence of the latter. However, referring to the absence of a certain trait renders this explanatory account conceptually pretty vague and ambiguous (cf. Pfattheicher et al., 2017; Pfattheicher & Böhm, 2018). We argue that the current study can help to overcome the conceptual vacuum (reflected in a reference to the absence of the respective traits) by way of reference to the construct of sadism. That is, we see our study as a contribution that addresses some of the conceptually underdeveloped areas of the personality space (typically referred to as low levels or absence of a certain basic trait). To the best of our knowledge, there are no contributions available that precisely conceptualize what the opposite pole of agreeableness (or Honesty-Humility, conscientiousness) specifically represents. The current results suggest that sadism is a significant element of this area of the personality space.

One can further discuss collinearity issues of psychopathy and sadism. Our main hypothesis was a positive relation between sadism and vandalism. This hypothesis is supported both by zero-order correlations and multiple-regression analyses, so the results do not depend on the applied analysis. Overall, we conclude that the problem of controlling for shared variance of psychopathy and sadism seems not to be an issue for our main hypothesis. The case is different for psychopathy which is a strong and significant predictor when looking at zero-order correlations (see Table 1); it is a significant predictor in multiple regression analyses when sadism was controlled for. However, it is not a significant predictor in multiple regression analyses when psychopathy is decomposed to its subscales. In the latter case, removing shared variance of the subscales of psychopathy probably removes the essence of psychopathy in these subscales (cf. Sleep, Lynam, Hyatt, & Miller, 2017). Then these “reduced” subscales of psychopathy (which likely no longer represent psychopathy) are not correlated with vandalism any more. What we conclude is that psychopathy could play a role in vandalism (as indicated by its positive zero-order correlation with vandalism); however, it is unclear which specific aspect of psychopathy explains this relation (as indicated by the unclear results in multiple regression analyses).

In critically reflecting on the current work, we want to acknowledge that it remains unclear why exactly individuals with sadistic tendencies are more motivated to vandalize things for pleasure as compared to their low sadistic counterparts. It seems possible that sadistic orientations are necessarily person-focused, that is, individuals with sadistic tendencies vandalize in order to harm and to dominate another human being who owns the destroyed property. However, it seems also possible that sadistic orientations can be applied to objects without a strong focus on the owner as well (i.e., exercise control and dominance by means of violence). Future research can examine this interesting issue. In case of the G-20 summit at Hamburg, some cases of vandalism reflect a focus on the person (i.e. the owner), for example, when protesters destroyed luxury cars. However, other instances of vandalism seemed less person-focused, such as the destruction of bus stop shelters or shops.

Furthermore, we want to acknowledge that our study is based on self-reported motivations and behavioral tendencies in a sample of modest size. The study did not include a behavioral measure of vandalism. In this regard, a field study assessing vandalism along with dark personality traits would prove a promising approach for future research. We also want to highlight that the vandalism measure, although reporting good internal consistency, has been developed specifically for this study and therefore no further validity information is currently available.

Moreover, our study is restricted to one specific form of vandalism, that is, destroying things for pleasure. Vandalism is a relatively broad construct that includes a broad variety of different behavioral manifestations, driven by different motivational orientations (Goldstein, 1996). In this regard, the present contribution picked only one piece of the bigger puzzle of vandalism. Additionally, the present study has neglected boundary conditions of the sadism-vandalism relation. For instance, it could be possible that the nature of the object itself (e.g., valuable or not valuable) might make a big difference on the purposes the act of destroying it serves. It is also possible that being observed by others (vs. acting without observation) affects vandalistic acts. Indeed, antisocial behavior is shown less likely when individuals are observed (e.g., Steinmetz & Pfattheicher, 2017). As such, the findings of the present study are dealing with a degree of indeterminateness and complexity.

To conclude, our approach to examine the associations between vandalism (specifically, destroying things for fun) and basic dark personality orientations reflects a step in a new direction and constitutes a contribution to research on violence in general and vandalism in particular. As such, the present work opens a new avenue of research on vandalism.
