            ABSTRACT

            Corruption is widely recognized as one of the economic problems. While there has been a lot of attention to this issue, statistical analysis of corruption using happenstance data is often fail actually to observe the behavior of corrupt activity itself. Naturally, corruption is hard to observe in the field, since everyone engaged in it has good reasons to remain silent.             1. INTRODUCTION

            Corruption is widely recognized as one of the economic problems. Corruption is both pervasive and significant around the world. In some developing countries, such as Zaire and Kenya, it probably amounts to a large fraction of the Gross National product (Shleifer and Vishny, 1993). The explicit cost of corruption in Indonesia during the period 2001-2009 was about $7.86 billion. Mauro (1995) finds statistical evidence for the growth-reducing effect of corruption. Long and Rao (1995) show that the value of a firm decreases significantly after the corrupt behavior of managers discovered. Gupta et al., (1998) find out that the effect of corruption increases poverty. Easterly (2001) explained how different types of corruption hamper the whole economy. Nevertheless, case study and statistical analysis based on happenstance data of corruption often fail to determine the behavior of corrupt activity itself. Real-world observations, even though authentic, might have conflicting interpretations as we cannot exclude influences other than those we want to observe. The major advantage of laboratory experiments is that different influences can be controlled for (Frank and Schulze, 2000). Naturally, corruption is hard to observe in the field since everyone engaged in it has good reasons to remain silent. In an alternative laboratory experiment, we can observe behavior in simulated bribery scenario directly.

            Furthermore we can identify which variables influence corrupt behavior by arranging the experimental environment. According to Lianju and Luyan (2011), bribery is one of the most important manifestations of corruption, and its purpose is (through the exchange of money and power) to get reciprocity benefits. Therefore conducting an experiment based on a bribery situation can be considered as a good model to understand corruption. According to Abbink et al. (2002), there is three essential characteristics of corruption. First, there is the reciprocity feature: both briber and bribee can exchange benefits with one another. However, since bribery is illegal, enforceable contracts on corrupt acts cannot be made.

            The exchange favors must rely on trust and reciprocity between the briber and bribee. Second, corruption imposes negative externalities on the public. Third, bribe giving and bribe taking is liable to severe penalties in case of discovery. Therefore, corruption is inherently risky. This paper is designed to see how reciprocity and risky characteristics of corruption work on the individuals' behavior. The analysis of corrupt behavior in this paper is done through experimental economic approach performed at the Faculty of Economics and Business, Universitas Gadjah Mada, Indonesia, where students were used as the subject. The experiment is based on Giamattei (2010) and consists of several types of a simple sequential game. In the entire game, one player (representing the potential briber) can decide whether they want to transfer money to second player (representing potential bribee) or not. The bribe aims to induce a decision that benefits the briber. This option, however, is costly for the bribee. With this feature alone, we can obtain a simple reciprocity game which allows us to study the impact of trust and reciprocity. When a transfer has been made, player two can choose whether to report the bribe, induce opportunistic behavior, or induce reciprocity behavior.

            Regarding player two's opportunistic behavior, player one can choose whether to report player two or do nothing. By comparing these treatments, we can examine the corrupt behavior. All treatments are played in ten round games between the same players with randomized matching. Two interesting results emerged. First, as opposed to the prediction, opportunistic behavior is not preferable when the subjects are highly risk-averse. Secondly, people who are bribed are more likely to reciprocate. Also, gender and (study) major differences did not seem to matter in bribery. The article goes as follow. Section B explores the literature on corrupt behavior with particular stress on the use of laboratory experiment. Section C discusses the design of the experiment. Section D provides results and discussion. Finally, section E concludes.

            2.LITERATURE REVIEW

            According to Falk & Fehr (2003), Laboratory experiments enable us to test ideas and theories that may be impossible to test naturally in the real world. It also allows us to simulate what can happen in the real world into a human-made laboratory environment, where it is nearly impossible to be done in the real world due to ethical value. Experimental economics made its early debut through work of Vernon Smith in 1962, entitled "An Experimental Study of Market Behavior." As time goes by, it became more popular and had significant development in the 1970s. Nowadays we can find a lot of study using experimental economics approach, such as those of Atlas (2008), Atlas and Putterman (2011), Chesney et al., (2009), Fielder (2009), Fielder and Haruvy (2009), (Haruvy, 2011), and Spann et al. (2010).

            Numbers of studies have been done on corruption; however, using a laboratory experiment to determine the behavior of briber and bribee is not yet very common. Dusek et al. (2004) categorize experiments on corruption and corruptibility as those involving bilateral settings and unilateral settings. In one of the first investigations, Frank and Schulze (2000) focused on individual tendencies to engage in corruption in procurement and whether economists are more likely to accept bribes. Later on, Schulze and Frank (2003) extended their analysis to state how intrinsic motivations are affected by threats of penalties. Marwell and Ames (1981) conducted a laboratory experiment on free riding: individuals were asked to allocate a given financial endowment to private use and contributions to a public good. Carter and Irons (1991) used an ultimatum bargaining game, where the 'proposer' offers a division of a given amount of $10 between him and the 'responder.' Frank et al. (1993) conducted a prisoner's dilemma experiment and found that economics majors defect significantly more often (60 percent) than non-majors (39 percent). This paper mostly inspired by previous studies that were conducted specifically to analyze corrupt behavior like Abbink et al. (2002), Frank and Schulze (2000), Lambsdorff and Frank (2007) and Giamattei (2010).

            Frank and Schulze (2000) did a simple experiment about the principal-agent problem. The agent has an incentive to favor a third party at the expense of the principal and in exchange for some compensation. However, they have no punishment in their game and only focused on different attitudes towards the conflict of self-interest versus social concerns, not towards risk. Participants decide how much bribe they get and how much damage they cause to others; the briber is experimental. The result showed that economic students are significantly more corrupt than others, which is due to self- selection rather than indoctrination. Male students are more corrupt than female ones. In contrast to Frank and Schulze (2000), Abbink et al. (2002), Lambsdorff and Frank (2007) and Giamattei (2010) introduced risk and punishment in their corruption game. Abbink et al. (2002), made a bribery game which consists of three different treatments, each treatment consecutively captures (1) reciprocity behavior, (2) negative externalities, and (3) risk of getting caught. The main difference between a standard ultimatum game (or gift exchange game) and any reasonable corruption experiment is that in the latter case, "successful" bilateral negotiations should impose an externality on a third party.

            Abbink et al. (2002) designed this externality as a certain amount subtracted from the payoff of all other participants in the experiment who played the same game at the same time. The result showed that reciprocity could establish bribery relationships, where negative externalities have no apparent effect. The penalty threat significantly reduces corruption, although discovery probabilities are typically underestimated. However, according to Lambsdorff and Frank (2007), that kind of reciprocity in Abbink et al. (2002) might be an issue if a subject expects other participants to prefer the corrupt action, this might provoke, or justify, his or her decision to do the same. In Lambsdorff and Frank (2007) and Giamattei (2010), this possible effect is avoided by not involving the externality on third parties in the game between two players. This corresponds better with real cases of corruption, where bribers and bribee harm people who will never get the chance to pay them out.

            In Lambsdorff and Frank (2007) and Giamattei (2010), the third party is Mdecins Sans Frontires, a well-known non-profit organization, the donation to the organization depending on the subjects' behavior. The result from Lambsdorff and Frank (2007) showed that female public servants were more inclined to behave opportunistically, while female businesspeople were less engaged in negative reciprocity. Businesspeople that strongly preferred a corrupt framing of the game and obtained a form with corrupt wording were more willing to punish non-delivering public servants. Giamattei (2010) an extended version of Lambsdorff and Frank (2007)'s game. The payoffs were not modified to keep the two models comparable. According to Giamattei (2010), the largest concern on Lambsdorff and Frank (2007) is that the game focused on the problem where firms have to be corrupt and cannot abstain from bribery. Therefore Giamattei (2010) modified the experiment. The crucial change was adding a new decision point for the firm whether to bribe or to abstain from bribing. This new decision branch introduced the possibility for the firm to play honestly up front. In the Lambsdorff and Frank (2007), this was only possible by doing whistle-blowing after the public servant did opportunistic behavior. In order not to render the complication of the game, the gift exchange and bribe framing (which could be decided on in Lambsdorff and Frank (2007)) was eliminated. This means, if money is paid to the public servant, it is always declared as a bribe.

            The result from Giamattei (2010) showed that women play less corruptly as being a firm, but more opportunistically as a public servant. Also, the academic major was found to influence strategic decision. Economists tend to play more profit-maximizing than moral. This effect dilutes once controlled for gender. The last element to be studied was the timing of decision-making. The decision to refrain from bribing took much longer than to engage in bribery. This paper is adoption from Giamattei (2010), where the firm Alpha owner makes a payment to a public servant to win a public contract. The payment is submitted to the public servant who has three possibilities to decide on. First, the public servant can do whistle-blowing and denounce the firm owner's corrupt approach. The firm owner is fined, and the public servant gets a bonus.

            Second, the public servant can give the contract to Firm Beta, which would execute the contract in a better way. This case would lead to the decision of the Firm Alpha owner to do whistle-blowing or not. In the case of whistleblowing, the Firm Alpha owner is still punished, and the public servant loses the paid bribe. If the Firm Alpha owner does not take revenge for not getting the contract, the public servant can keep the whole bribe, and Firm Alpha owner keeps only the rest of its initial endowment. The third case is that the public servant reciprocates the bribe and gives the contract to Firm Alpha owner. This would lead in the case that the Firm Alpha is winning a large amount for the contract and to the public servant sharing his bribe with others to enforce the corrupt arrangement. Also, all actions besides a successful corrupt deal would lead to a benefit for society, a positive externality, which was modeled as a donation to Medecins sans Frontiers.

            3.METHODOLOGY

            The experiment consists of two players, player one and player two. Player one is the briber, acts as the owner of Firm Alpha, who tries to win a contract from a public servant. Player two is the bribee acts as the public servant. This situation is played ten rounds subsequently. The flow of the game is explained in Figure 1. According to Figure 1, firm Alpha was endowed with 25$, from which they could pay a bribe of 20$. Fines were set at 5$ for the firm so that in case of discovery Firm Alpha would get a payoff of 0$. In the beginning, Firm Alpha could decide whether they want to bribe or not; the latter would lead to a small payoff of 2$ for Firm Alpha, which was caused by the absence of other orders. The decision for the 2$ payoff was to render the corrupt transaction more attractive. The public servant's bonus was set to be 2$ if he reports the bribery (blow the whistle). The bribe would be confiscated, and the Firm Alpha would need to pay fine; hence, his payoff would be 0$. A public servant might implement opportunistic behavior where he accepts the bribe but not giving the contract to Firm Alpha. In that case, the public servant would keep the bribe for themselves. Opportunism behavior used by the public servant would mean that he concedes the contract in a tender to another firm than Firma Alpha, let's say Firm Beta, which would perform better.

            Therefore society would not be harmed, which is illustrated by the little red image, the logo of Medicines Sans Frontiers. Regarding this condition, Firm Alpha has two choices; whether to report the public servant or do nothing. If Firm Alpha decides to report, the bribe would be confiscated, and he still needs to pay 5$ fines. Both players would earn nothing. If Firm Alpha decides to do nothing, he got to keep residual endowment of 5$ and the public servant would keep the 20$ bribe. If the public servant plays reciprocity, he concedes the contract to the firm he was bribed by. The corrupt deal would be successful, and therefore, society is harmed. In this case, the public servant needs to share the bribe with other public servants to enforce the deal. Hence his payoff would be 10$. Meanwhile, Firm Alpha's profit from getting the contract was set to 35$. Firm Alpha's final profit would be 40$ (from 35$ profit plus residual endowment 5$). When the game is solved via backward-induction, we will see the following strategy as equilibrium. First, the firm bribes, then this payment is not reciprocated by the public servant, and the opportunistic play is not punished by the firm. This would lead to a payoff of 20$ for the public servant and 5$ for the firm. This means that bribing and opportunistic behavior should win out. There are several hypotheses we want to test by conducting different treatments in the experiment:

            H1: No firm chooses to blow the whistle in case of reciprocated corruption.

            H2: Female public servants are less likely to reciprocate.

            H3: Students with more economic lectures tend to act more profit- maximizing and less morally.

            The experiment was conducted using z-Tree by utilizing the client-server-system. Respondents of this experiment are the students of faculty of Economics of Business Universitas Gadjah Mada. The experiment was conducted at IT Corner Faculty of Economics and Business Universitas Gadjah Mada on September 3rd and 4th 2015. Duration of the experiment is around one hour (11.00-12.00). The instruction of the game was read by experimenters and shown in welcoming screen. No other additional oral instructions were provided to the participants. The participants were not allowed to talk during the experiment. The participants got real payoff based on their point in decision making; the exchange rate was Rp 250. This means 1$ in the game equals Rp 250 in the real world. The payoff is based on the decision they make during the game.

            The matching of the players was done randomly, and the participants did not know who they played with. All games were supervised by experimenters. There were 32 persons joined the experiment. Since this is a sequential game, the participants were divided into a group of two persons whose decision affects each other. Therefore there were 16 pairs that played the game for ten rounds. In each round, the pair was randomized so that a person can play as briber in one round and as bribee in another. With this condition, the experiment ended up having 160 observations to analyze. All subjects were students with an average age of 22. The participants consist of 19 female students and 13 male students. The participants are students from the Faculty of Economics and Business, which divided into 11 accounting students (34%), 12 economics students (38%), and nine management students (28%).

            4.RESULTS AND DISCUSSION

            In the previous section, we discussed the differences between the original setting in Lambsdorff and Frank (2007) and the modification in Giamattei (2010). In latter research, Giamattei (2010) introduce new choice for the firm whether to bribe or not to bribe.

            This study tries to see if this modification makes any difference in terms of the firm's behavior.

            This paper adopts Giamattei (2010), and its analysis is called "new game" in Table 1, Table 2, and Table 3, and Table 4. Meanwhile, "original game" represents the result from Giamattei (2010).

            A z-test is used to see if there is a statistically significant difference between the two sample proportions.

            The first hypothesis is confirmed; the data show that once the public servant reciprocates, none of the firm report the corrupt behavior of public servant. The public servant is less likely to play opportunistically. Table 2 shows that only one of the public servants played opportunistically. This result is quite puzzling since playing is the Nash Equilibrium of the game. First, the firm bribe, then this payment is not reciprocated by the public servant, and the opportunistic play is not punished by the firm. This would lead to a payoff of 20$ for the public servant and 5$ for the firm. This means that bribing and opportunistic behavior should win out, but it did not. This might be caused by the subject's risk-aversion behavior. In comparison to the original game, whistle-blowing by the firm is more likely in case of opportunistic behavior. The result is rather inconclusive since only 3% of public servants play opportunistically. Regarding this action, none of the firms blows the whistle.

            The second hypothesis is not confirmed. The result shows that 75% of women are willing to reciprocate, while only 58% are willing to reciprocate. This shows that, contrary to the hypothesis, female public servant are more likely to reciprocate. Table 3 shows the frequency of public servants' reaction; we can see the different between men and women here. Table 3 shows public servant's reaction to bribery. From all of the people who blew the whistle, 44% are women, and 56% are men. And there is only one person who played opportunistically, and it was a man. Meanwhile from all the people who reciprocated 61% are women and 31% men. This result is also different from Lambsdorff and Frank (2007) and Giamattei (2010). Lambsdorff and Frank (2007) and Giamattei (2010) find that female public servants are more likely to induce opportunism behavior. In this paper, opportunism behavior is hardly showed in both men and women. Also, contrary to Lambsdorff and Frank (2007) and Giamattei (2010),

            Table 3 shows that women are more likely to engage in reciprocity behavior. Since reciprocity behavior is favorable, according to the participants, we cannot analyze how a firm would react to opportunism behavior. The two-sample test of proportions for whistle-blowing behavior and reciprocity behavior between women and men were done, and the result for both cases is statistically significant. Hence we can conclude that: (1) average number of women who blow the whistle is not equal of an average number of men who blow the whistle in ten rounds subsequently, (2) average number of women who reciprocate is not equal of an average number of men who reciprocate in ten rounds subsequently. In other words, gender difference does correlate with how people respond to corruption; in this case, whistle-blowing behavior and reciprocity behavior.Female firm owners are less likely to report on bribe-taking when the public servant played opportunistically. The result is rather inconclusive since only 3% of public servants play opportunistically. Regarding this action, none of the firms blew the whistle.

            The result shows that 53% of women are willing to bribe, while only 46% are willing to bribe. This shows that, contrary to the hypothesis, female firms are more likely to bribe. Table 01 shows the frequency of firm's bribe decision; we can see the difference between men and women here. We can see in Table 1 that bribe practice is more often done by women than men. We can see that from all of the people who bribe, 65% are women, and 35% are men. This is a little bit surprising since the previous literature stated that women are more reluctant to engage in corrupt behavior. After we did z-Test: Two-sample test of proportions, it turned out to be not statistically significant. Hence we can conclude that the average number of female who does bribery is equal to the average number of male who does bribery in ten rounds subsequently. In other words, gender difference does not correlate with corrupt behavior.

            In this research, we associate students with more economic lectures as a student from the economics department. And other students, from the accounting department and management department, are associated as Non-Econ student. The experiment has resulted in the data in Table 3 and Table 4. The third hypothesis is not confirmed. Table 3 and Table 4 shows that non-economics students are more likely to engage bribery than other majors. In terms of public servant's behavior, economics students are more willing to blow the whistle than reciprocate. This is contrary to Frank and Schulze (2000) and Giamattei (2010), whose researches have shown that economics student is more likely to engage in corruption. Compared to non-economics students, based on the result, economics student tends to be less profit-maximizing. After we did z-Test: Two-sample test of proportions for bribery, whistle-blowing, and reciprocity behavior, it turns out none of the cases are statistically significant. Hence we can conclude that: (1) average number of economics students who do bribery is equal of the average number of non-economics students who do bribery in ten rounds subsequently, (2) average number of economics students who blow the whistle is equal of average number of non-economics students who blow the whistle in ten rounds subsequently, (3) average number of economics students who reciprocate is equal of average number of noneconomics students who reciprocate in ten rounds subsequently. Or in other words, e can say that the difference in academic major does not correlate with corrupt behavior.

            The finding of this paper has shown that almost none of the participant chose to play opportunistic behavior. The majority of public servants in the Lambsdorff and Frank (2007) chose opportunistic behavior, while in Giamattei (2010) reciprocity is the most preferred. In Lambsdorff and Frank (2007) firms had no choice but to bribe, the situation in the game made bribing was an obligation. In Giamattei (2010), Firms got to decide whether to bribe or not. Giamattei (2010) then stated that opportunistic behavior in a corrupt system seems to be less likely if the firm decides for bribery than if it is forced to. This paper is consistent with Giamattei (2010). Giamattei (2010) stated that most of the participants choose to reciprocate due to the explicit character of the bribe and the higher fear of punishment in case of opportunism. If a firm decides for bribing, it makes punishment more probable. Giamattei (2010) and then asked public servants who acted reciprocally about their motivation in the questionnaire. The risk aversion here exceeds the feeling that a corrupt deal has to be maintained. One of the limitations of this research is we did not hand out such a questionnaire. Nonetheless, we agree with Giamattei (2010), where there is a high possibility that risk aversion is the reason why almost none of the participant chose opportunistic behavior.

            The fact that almost half of the participant choose not to bribe in each round strengthen the assumption. It has been known that Indonesian people tend to be risk-averse: the data from the fourth wave of the Indonesia Family Life Survey (IFLS) has shown that 45% of Indonesian people are risk-averse. They would choose something that is guaranteed even though it is less valuable than going after something more valuable that is not guaranteed. There is a high possibility that this study reflects the risk-aversion characteristic of Indonesian.

            5.CONCLUSION

            This paper is designed to see how reciprocity and risky characteristics of corruption work on the individuals' behavior. There are several concluding remarks we can learn from this research. First, opportunistic behavior is not preferable when the subjects are highly risk-averse. This can be said so because most of the Indonesian people are risk-averse. Second, since opportunistic behavior has inherited risk of being reported, people who are bribed are more likely to reciprocate. Third, gender difference between men and women does not correlate with their behavior towards giving bribe. Although, according to this research, women are showed to be less reluctant to bribery. Forth, there is a difference in terms of behavior between men and women towards whistle-blowing behavior and reciprocity behavior. Fifth, academic major does not correlate with someone's behavior towards corruption. Nevertheless, this paper has a unique case where major, which is more profit maximizing is noneconomics major.

            Regarding the results, there are still plenty of factors this research has not yet included, such as personal background and ability of the subjects. Also, it would be interesting to use subjects that are from different faculties outside economics and business. That way, we can see how economic lecture make a difference in terms of decision making. In this game, we can only see one shot corruption game, while in many cases, bribery is continuous. By analyzing the continued model of corruption, we can see if there is any change or consistency in corrupt behavior, we can also see if a learning curve emerges. To construct such model, the participants should be fixed paired and play for many rounds. Hence a lot number of subjects is required. Lastly, in the real world, extortion is also possible where bribes are initiated by a public servant instead of a firm. Therefore, it would be thought-provoking if such situation is modeled and then tested using laboratory experiments.
