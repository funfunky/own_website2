Personality and Individual Differences

Volume 107, 1 March 2017, Pages 237-245

Personality traits of prisoners as compared to general populations: Signs of adjustment to the situation?

Author links open overlay panelT. GerhardErikssona1Johanna G.Masche-NobAnna M.Dådermanc
https://doi.org/10.1016/j.paid.2016.11.030Get rights and content

Highlights

•

Two samples of inmates in Swedish prisons were compared to five control groups.

•

Inmates exceeded controls on conscientiousness facets reflecting their environment.

•

Also prison guards scored higher on items reflecting their environment.

•

These findings are interpreted as an adaptation to the prison environment.

•

Conscientiousness scores may not be used for predicting inmates' future offences.

Abstract

Two recent studies have challenged the well-established belief that offending behaviors are inversely related to the personality trait of conscientiousness. Therefore, the aim of this study was to explore prisoners’ levels of traits according to the Five-Factor Model (FFM) of personality compared to control groups, with a focus on conscientiousness. Two separate samples of inmates in Swedish high-security prisons were investigated in three studies. Inmates and non-inmates completed a Swedish-language translation of Goldberg’s (1999) International Personality Item Pool questionnaire (IPIP-NEO, Bäckström, 2007). Male inmates (n = 46) in Studies 1 and 2 scored higher on conscientiousness than non-inmates (norm data based on approximately 800 males, and a students’ sample), which conflicts with previous results. Study 3 further explored the conscientiousness differences on the facet level. Male and female inmates (n = 131) scored higher on order and self-discipline (even after an adjustment for social desirability) than students (n = 136). In conjunction with previous findings, these differences are interpreted as being either temporal or enduring adjustments to the prison environment. It is suggested that researchers and clinical teams should cautiously interpret the FFM factor of conscientiousness (and its facets) when planning the further treatment of inmates.

1. Introduction

The search for the “criminal personality” is not new (e.g., Lombroso, 1876, cited after Thiry, 2012). Antisocial behavior has sometimes been “explained” tautologically by an antisocial personality, implying that stable traits predict the antisocial behavior. For example, Clower and Bothwell (2001, p. 234) argued for low levels of conscientiousness and openness as causes of a criminal way of life, although they had measured prison inmates’ personality after their most recent conviction. However, it has been shown that even global traits can change due to life circumstances. For example, the level of conscientiousness increased in German high school students just before the final examinations, especially among those who had not shown much achievement behavior before (Bleidorn, 2012). Thus, particular personality characteristics among convicted criminal offenders are not necessarily causes of their criminal behaviors, but may be explained in other ways, for example as an adaptation to their prison environment.

A comprehensive taxonomy of personality traits is the Five-Factor Model (FFM) of personality (McCrae and Costa, 2008, Widiger and Costa, 2012), which consists of the dimensions of neuroticism, extraversion, openness, agreeableness, and conscientiousness. These five traits are composed of six facets each. The strongest and most consistent associations with criminal and antisocial behaviors have been found for agreeableness and conscientiousness. Two prospective studies found that low levels of agreeableness and conscientiousness predicted juridical sanctions (Mõttus et al., 2012, O'Riordan and O'Connell, 2014). Likewise, two meta-analyses revealed an inverse relation of agreeableness and conscientiousness with offending behaviors (Jones et al., 2011, Miller and Lynam, 2001). Even when comparing white-collar professionals with white-collar criminals, thereby comparing similar groups in terms of education and intelligence, they differed on a discriminant function composed of characteristics of conscientiousness and agreeableness (Collins & Schmidt, 1993). Thus, delinquency is associated with and can be predicted by low levels of agreeableness and conscientiousness.

Criminal behavior can even be linked with the FFM via psychopathy, which, by definition, is associated with norm-breaking and remorseless behaviors, including criminal acts, and is theoretically related to low agreeableness and to some degree even to low conscientiousness (Decuyper et al., 2009, Miller, 2012, Ross et al., 2009). Approaches to define and measure psychopathy range from the clinical entity of antisocial personality disorder (DSM-5, Section II, American Psychiatric Association, 2013) over clinical dimensions (DSM-5, Section III, see also Watson, Stasik, Ro, & Clark, 2013) to one- and multi-dimensional psychopathy measures of varying content (see Lilienfeld, Watts, Smith, Berg, & Latzman, 2014). Despite this conceptual variety, findings converge in that psychopathy predicts criminal offences and is associated with low levels of agreeableness and conscientiousness, thereby linking them with criminal behaviors. For example, rates of antisocial personality disorder in prison populations have been estimated to be 70% and higher, in contrast to 0.2–3.3% in the general population (American Psychiatric Association, 2013). The psychopathy scale PCL-R has predicted reoffending both in a recent meta-analysis (Mokros, Vohs, & Habermeyer, 2014) and in a sample of released life-time prisoners (Sturup, Karlberg, Fredriksson, Lihoff, & Kristiansson, 2015).

With respect to psychopathy–FFM associations, meta-analytic findings reveal strong associations of antisocial personality disorder with low levels of agreeableness and conscientiousness (Saulsman and Page, 2004, Saulsman and Page, 2005; see also Widiger & Costa, 2012). The same holds for the dimensional definition of psychopathy according to DSM-5 Section 3 (Crego et al., 2015, Watson et al., 2013). Although psychopathy as a personality trait has been defined and measured in various ways, the findings converge in links between psychopathy and low levels of agreeableness and conscientiousness (Decuyper et al., 2009, Lilienfeld et al., 2014). Thus, research on psychopathy corroborates the notion that low levels of agreeableness and conscientiousness are associated with criminal behavior.

In contrast, the personality trait of openness appears to be unrelated to criminal behaviors. Neither did it predict justice sanctions (Mõttus et al., 2012, O'Riordan and O'Connell, 2014) nor did meta-analyses reveal any associations with antisocial behaviors (Jones et al., 2011, Miller and Lynam, 2001). Theoretically, no (Decuyper et al., 2009) or only a few associations between psychopathy and facets of openness are expected (Miller, 2012, Ross et al., 2009), and, empirically, the associations were negligible (Crego et al., 2015, Decuyper et al., 2009, Saulsman and Page, 2004, Saulsman and Page, 2005).

The findings for neuroticism and extraversion have ranged between the extremes of relatively consistent associations with low agreeableness and low conscientiousness and no association with openness. Two longitudinal studies were able to predict justice sanctions from high levels of neuroticism (Mõttus et al., 2012, O'Riordan and O'Connell, 2014). High extraversion predicted justice sanctions in one study (O’Riordan & O’Connell, 2014), but not another (Mõttus et al., 2012). Meta-analyses revealed significant, but tiny, associations between neuroticism and offending behaviors, and extraversion was not significantly associated (Jones et al., 2011, Miller and Lynam, 2001). This sparseness of findings may be theoretically explained by opposite associations of single facets and the sub-dimensions of psychopathy (Miller, 2012). Confirming this explanation, associations of antisocial personality disorder and psychopathy with neuroticism and extraversion were generally negligible overall, and were composed of heterogeneous associations on the levels of facets, sub-dimensions, or single studies in meta-analyses (Crego et al., 2015, Decuyper et al., 2009, Ross et al., 2009, Saulsman and Page, 2004, Saulsman and Page, 2005). In summary, delinquents tend to score low on agreeableness and conscientiousness, average on openness, and might show either elevated, low, or average levels of neuroticism and extraversion, depending on which facets of these factors and which sub-dimensions of psychopathy and antisocial behaviors are measured.

However, the results showing that low levels of agreeableness and conscientiousness are linked to criminal behavior have been challenged by two recent studies conducted in the European countries of Belgium and Croatia, respectively (Thiry, 2012, Trninić et al., 2008). In both studies, prisoners scored higher on conscientiousness than students and a normative sample, respectively, and they scored higher on agreeableness than the normative sample. In Thiry’s (2012) sample, the prisoners provided data in the hope to be released from prison early; thus, social desirability and dissimulation might be an issue. However, inmates did not score higher than a normative sample on a scale of positive presentation management and low on the positively evaluated trait of openness. Moreover, the findings are in accord with Trninić et al.’s (2008) study, although the latter assured confidentiality to its participants. However, the student participants in the latter study might not be entirely representative of a general population. Further studies are needed to investigate the ways in which prisoners’ personality traits differ from the average population in more normative samples.

This article compares imprisoned populations with various normative samples in three studies, using various methods of measurement. For the sake of completeness, neuroticism, extraversion, and openness were explored in Studies 1 and 2, but given previous findings, no particularly high or low levels were expected for these factors. According to most previous research, low levels of agreeableness and conscientiousness would be expected in inmates. If higher levels were revealed instead, as in the studies by Thiry (2012) and Trninić et al. (2008), these results would be at odds with research in the context of psychopathic traits and antisocial personality disorder. In this case, we aimed at exploring this issue further and providing a tentative explanation for this discrepancy to the majority of findings.

The aim of Study 1 was to examine the differences in FFM scores between inmates and non-inmates. Study 2 explored these findings further by comparing inmates with two specific comparison groups. Finally, Study 3 investigated the differences in conscientiousness at the facet level in a new sample.

2. Study 1

2.1. Methods

2.1.1. Participants

Participants were recruited from the inmate population of a maximum security prison in the southern part of Sweden. All of the participants (N = 46) were men, M = 34.6 years, SD = 11.6. The length of sentence ranged from four years to life imprisonment and averaged approximately 20 years. All participants had been convicted for serious and/or repeated crimes, such as murder, robbery, and dealing drugs.

The control group consisted of Bäckström, 2007, Bäckström, 2010 Swedish norm data (N = 2021, M = 29.8 years, SD = 9.9, range: 18–84 years). Only the male data, composed of approximately 800 men, were used. Approximately 60% of the participants in the normative sample had some form of college education. Thus, the general population is more highly educated than the population of prison inmates, among which higher education is the exception. Most inmates who study during their imprisonment attempt to complete elementary school (9 years) or high school (12 years), and only a few participate in higher education.

2.1.2. Measures

A Swedish version (Bäckström, 2007) of the IPIP-50 inventory (Goldberg, 1999) was used. This inventory measures the factors of neuroticism, extraversion, openness, agreeableness, and conscientiousness by ten items each, on a five-point Likert-scale, ranging from Strongly disagree to Strongly agree. The internal consistencies for the factors in this study were good (Cronbach’s alpha = .87, .86, .89, .84, and .82, resp.). These reliability estimates are comparable to those reported by Bäckström (2007).

2.1.3. Procedure

After invitation by another inmate serving a life sentence, the inmates received the questionnaire in envelopes with pre-paid postage, just before evening lock-down. They filled out the questionnaire individually and did not receive any compensation.

2.2. Results and discussion

We performed five independent t-tests between the inmate data and the Swedish norm data (Table 1). The inmates scored significantly lower than the members of the normative sample on extraversion, openness, and agreeableness, and they scored significantly higher on conscientiousness. No differences were revealed with respect to neuroticism.

The non-significant result for neuroticism reflects earlier meta-analytic results and the findings from much larger samples, where the effects were small at best. In contrast to the previous findings, the inmates scored lower on extraversion. However, Trninić et al. (2008) also obtained low extraversion levels in inmates compared to athletic students, and, as in the present study, Thiry (2012) also measured low levels of openness in inmates.

The lower level of agreeableness in Swedish inmates replicates the majority of the findings. Thus, the conflicting finding in the Belgian study by Thiry (2012) remains a singularity, which may be due to a tendency to dissimulate because the assessments were made in the context of a decision on early release from prison.

Most importantly, this study replicates both Thiry’s (2012) and Trninić et al.’s (2008) findings of higher levels of conscientiousness in imprisoned persons. Both in this and in Trninić et al.’s study, the participants were assured confidentiality, limiting social desirability effects. Nevertheless, as in Thiry’s study, subtle differences in the administration of questionnaires in the actual sample as opposed to the respective large normative sample may have caused differences, although this appears unlikely, given that the various traits differed in different ways between the delinquent and the normative samples. Still, Study 2 was conducted to eliminate this possibility. More specifically, a sample of students and a sample of prison guards was investigated, who filled out the same questionnaires under similar conditions. The comparison sample of prison guards shares many features of the daily environment with the inmates, which might add to an understanding of the possible differences.

3. Study 2

3.1. Methods

3.1.1. Participants

The sample and personality data of inmates were identical to Study 1. The first control group consisted of 45 prison guards including 28 men, Mage = 37.8 years, SD = 10.8, and 17 women, Mage = 39.9 years, SD = 10.3. The second control group consisted of 32 students from a Swedish university, most of them enrolled in humanities. Of these, 7 were men, Mage = 24.8 years, SD = 6.5, and 25 were women, Mage = 26.5 years, SD = 8.4.

3.1.2. Measures

In addition to the IPIP-50 (Goldberg, 1999) used in Study 1, the respondents filled out 20 items on Impression Management (IM) (Paulhus, Bruce, & Trapnell, 1995). IM estimates the respondents’ inclination to deliberately give advantageous judgments (flattering) of their own person. After excluding three items to increase internal consistency, Cronbach’s alpha reached .82.

3.1.3. Procedure

The participants in the control groups were given envelopes containing the same set of items as was given to the inmates. They were told to complete the questionnaire individually. All participants in the study were anonymous and they were not compensated for their participation.

3.2. Results and discussion

To compare the FFM personality traits between the three sub-samples of inmates, prison guards, and students, a 5 × 3 between-groups multivariate analysis of covariance (MANCOVA) was performed, controlling for gender as a covariate to account for the different gender compositions in the three groups. In a second analysis, age and IM were added as control variables. The Box M test did not reveal any significant differences between the variance-covariance matrices in the three sub-samples, F(30, 34,518.66) = 1.28, ns. The data complied with assumptions of normality, linearity, and an absence of multicollinearity.

Wilks’s lambda indicated a significant main effect of sub-sample, Λ = .59, F(10, 228) = 6.87, p < .001, partial η2 = .23, whereas males and females did not differ significantly, Λ = .92, F(5, 114) = 1.93, ns, partial η2 = .08. All five personality factors differed significantly between the sub-samples in univariate analyses. As summarized in Table 2, post-hoc comparisons revealed that the three groups differed in different ways for each of the five personality traits. More specifically, both the inmates’ and students’ levels of neuroticism were higher than those of the guards, but the inmates and students did not differ from one another. The inmates scored below the other groups on extraversion and agreeableness. Both the inmates and prison guards scored lower than the students on openness and higher on conscientiousness.

To further explore in which specific way inmates and prison guards exceeded students in their levels of conscientiousness, planned contrasts were computed between the students and each of the two other groups for each of the ten conscientiousness items, controlling for gender. Three items differed significantly between the students and both the inmates and the guards (Table 3), and two additional items distinguished the prison guards from the students. Both the guards and the inmates differed on items addressing concrete behaviors required for both guards and inmates in a prison environment, such as putting things where they belong or completing their duties. In addition, the prison guards differed from the students in the extent to which they followed their schedule and were exacting in their jobs.

In a nutshell, Study 2 replicated the findings of Study 1. That is, the inmates scored lower on extraversion, openness, and agreeableness, and higher on conscientiousness not only compared to a normative sample, but also compared to university students. The inmates’ neuroticism was higher than that of the prison guards, but on approximately the same level as the students, which may explain why the inmates had not differed when compared to a more general population. In summary, irrespective of whether a representative sample or a student population was used for comparisons, the results were essentially the same, even when age and social desirability were statistically controlled. In addition, gender was not an issue here, as gender was controlled in these analyses and the gender differences were negligible. In addition to the control of impression management, the results themselves also speak against social desirability as an explanation of findings because the inmates, in part, described themselves in a less social desirable manner, most importantly by admitting their low agreeableness. Furthermore, the similarity of the findings with the prison guards on specific personality traits speaks against an artifact due to social desirability in the inmates’ responses to the questionnaire. Thus, the findings appear valid.

The comparison to the prison guards might contribute to an understanding of these findings. In some aspects, the prison guards did not differ significantly from the students; that is, the inmates differed consistently from both groups with respect to low extraversion and low agreeableness, indicating deviating personality traits among criminals. However, both the prison guards and inmates scored lower on openness and higher on conscientiousness. In addition to the possibility that similar traits might motivate one to guard criminals as to commit criminal offences, the prison environment may enhance the expression of certain traits. This would explain why prospective studies revealed the prediction of criminality using low levels of conscientiousness (Mõttus et al., 2012, O'Riordan and O'Connell, 2014), in contrast to the present and two previous studies that all found high levels of conscientiousness in prisoners (Thiry, 2012, Trninić et al., 2008).

This tentative conclusion is further fueled by results on item level. The tough restrictions on possessions in a prison, and the fact that private space is limited might lead to rejections of such items as “Leave my belongings around”. Furthermore, prison guards face special demands by the highly structured environment in a prison, which leads to affirmatory responses to the “Follow a schedule” item. In summary, both the inmates and the prison guards appear to have responded to these specific items as imposed on them by the prison environment, rather than as a consequence of a general personality trait. Alternatively, the results may express the inmates’ internalization of the demands of the prison environment, which may have led to adjustment of their traits. Basic personality traits are flexible to some extent (Pervin, Cervone, & John, 2005), and more specifically, conscientiousness has been proven to increase when needed (Bleidorn, 2012). Adjustment may explain why the inmates scored higher than non-inmates on conscientiousness.

Because the items used in this study had not been designed to tap into the various facets of conscientiousness, Study 3 further explored whether there are differences in specific facets of conscientiousness that may relate to the prison environment rather than differences in the global trait of conscientiousness. Furthermore, Study 3 included prisoners of both genders.

4. Study 3

4.1. Methods

4.1.1. Participants

The participants were recruited from the inmates of several prisons in the south of Sweden. One female prison had the second-highest security level, the others had the highest security level. Three hundred questionnaires were distributed, of which 131 (43.7%) were returned. The respondents were between 19 and 73 years old (47 women, M = 36.0 years, SD = 12.0, range: 21–62 years, and 84 men, M = 34.8, SD = 11.1, range: 19–73 years). The length of sentence ranged from one year to life imprisonment. The participants had been convicted of crimes similar to those of the participants in Studies 1 and 2. Two participants were excluded as univariate outliers (Tabachnick & Fidell, 2007).

The control group consisted of 136 subjects (79 women, M = 33.3, SD = 11.1, range: 18–57 years, and 54 men, M = 34.0, SD = 9.5, range: 18–59 years). They were recruited through an invitation published on the internet at a university in the south of Sweden. Three participants were excluded as multivariate outliers (they had extremely low values of self-discipline and extremely high values of competence).

4.1.2. Measures

Conscientiousness was measured using the Swedish version of IPIP-NEO (Bäckström, 2010), which consists of six facets: competence, order, dutifulness, achievement striving, self-discipline, and deliberation, each measured by 10 items. The internal consistencies were satisfactory for all facets: .82, .86, .81, .82, .87, and .83, respectively.

The Impression Management (IM) and Self-deception (SD) scales from the “Personal Attribute Survey” of the IPIP archive (Paulhus et al., 1995) were used in a Swedish translation (Bäckström, 2007). Twenty-four items (14 IM and 10 SD items) were combined into a composite measure of social desirability. The internal consistency of this composite was satisfactory (α = .85).

4.1.3. Procedure

The procedure was similar to that used in Study 1. However, the student control group filled out an on-line version of the questionnaire on the internet.

4.2. Results and discussion

To test the differences in the six facets of conscientiousness between the groups of inmates and students, a 6 × 2 × 2 between-groups multivariate analysis of variance (MANOVA) was performed with the values of the six facets competence, order, dutifulness, achievement striving, self-discipline, and deliberation as the dependent variables. The independent variables were the inmate or student groups, respectively, and gender. Box’s test revealed statistically significant differences in the covariance matrices, F(63, 95,553.68) = 1.62, p = .001. However, none of the differences in variance exceeded the ratio of 10:1, indicating satisfactory homogeneity of variance (Tabachnick & Fidell, 2007, p. 280). The data complied also with assumptions of normality, linearity, and the absence of multicollinearity.

According to the multivariate analysis, the conscientiousness facets were significantly affected by group, Λ = .66, F(6, 244) = 20.69, p < .001, partial η2 = .33, gender, Λ = .83, F(6, 244) = 8.04, p < .001, partial η2 = .17, and the group-by-gender interaction, Λ = .93, F(6, 244) = 3.14, p = .006, partial η2 = .07. Table 4 contains the means of inmates and students split by gender, as well as the results of the univariate analyses for each facet of conscientiousness. After adjusting the alpha levels of these six univariate analyses (Holm, 1979), males scored higher than females on all facets of conscientiousness except for competence. The gender-by-group interaction did not reach significance for any of the six facets; thus, all group differences applied to both genders. After correction for the number of tests, the inmates significantly exceeded the students on order and self-discipline. The remaining four facets did not differ significantly, suggesting that the overall difference in conscientiousness is due to the inmates scoring higher on order and self-discipline.

In a 2 × 2 ANOVA of group and gender, the inmates’ estimated mean score of social desirability did not differ significantly from that of non-inmates, F(1, 257) = 0.01, ns, η2 = .00. Men provided more socially desirable reports than females, but the group-by-gender interaction failed to reach significance, F(1, 257) = 0.04, ns, η2 = .00. However, the zero-order bivariate correlations between all facets of conscientiousness and social desirability ranged from .52 to .79. Although age and social desirability are unlikely to cause artifacts due to the similarity of the inmates and the students on these variables, the large correlations between social desirability and the various facets of conscientiousness warrant attention.

Thus, the MANOVA of differences in conscientiousness between the inmates and the students was repeated, adding age and social desirability as covariates. Because preliminary analyses indicated that the effects of these covariates were not homogeneous in all subgroups, the age-by-group and social desirability-by-group-by-gender interactions were included in a first MANCOVA as well as the lower-order main and interaction effects contained in them. As can be expected from the high zero-order correlations, social desirably was associated with conscientiousness as well as each its six facets. Despite these effects and interactions of the control variables age and social desirability, the differences between the inmates and the students were still significant, Λ = .88, F(6, 233) = 5.22, p < .001, partial η2 = .12. As before, the inmates scored higher than the students on order, F(1, 242) = 74.58, p < .001, partial η2 = .24, and higher on self-discipline, F(1, 241) = 17.93, p < .001, partial η2 = .07. In addition, a minor group difference in competence emerged in favor of the inmates, F(1, 242) = 6.53, p = .011, partial η2 = .03. In summary, social desirable reports had masked a self-reported superiority of the inmates over the students. However, despite a major overlap between social desirability and all six facets of conscientiousness, the differences between the inmates and the control group in order and self-discipline remained significant even after the statistical control of age and social desirability.

Confirming and extending the findings of Studies 1 and 2, Study 3 showed that the inmates had higher mean scores than the non-inmates on the conscientiousness facets of order and self-discipline. These differences were robust even after the adjustments for social desirability and age had been applied. Thus, all three studies converged in that (a) conscientiousness is higher in prisoners than in the general population and (b) that this difference is specific to those domains of conscientiousness that are ruled by the special prison environment. Furthermore, Study 3 extended these findings to female prisoners.

We further confirmed this result by performing two independent t-tests using Swedish norm data (Bäckström, 2007, Bäckström, 2010), based on 2021 subjects. Inmates exceeded the norm sample on order, t(2150) = 6.49, p < .001, d = 0.56, 95% CI [0.27, 0.84], δ = 5.88, and self-discipline, t(2150) = 2.07, p < .05, d = 0.19, 95% CI [− 0.09, 0.47], δ = 2.04. The means of order and self-discipline in the norm data were 23.12 and 23.26, respectively.

If social desirability was statistically controlled, the inmates described themselves as more competent than the students. One possible interpretation might be that competence is especially important to students, so that they may tend to rate their competence higher when giving general socially desirable descriptions of themselves. However, this would imply a stronger association of competence and social desirability in the students compared to the inmates (which was not found in the data and this interaction was thus excluded from the statistical model). Similarly, the lack of a social desirability-by-competence interaction speaks against the possibility that the inmates, due to their presumably high psychopathy levels, would give grandiose ratings of their abilities irrespective of their tendency to present themselves in a favorable way. Given the strong associations between social desirability and the various facets of conscientiousness, it is doubtful that social desirability is entirely a source of bias. Rather, social desirability items such as “I try to comply with rules” may very well overlap with facets of conscientiousness. Thus, the statistical control of social desirability does not only reduce bias, but also strips part of the meaning from the studied variables. For this reason, the additional group difference that only emerged after control of age and social desirability should be interpreted with caution.

Because inmates outperformed comparison groups on those facets of conscientiousness relevant for the prison environment, the question arises whether the length of incarceration might play a role. Even though participants were not asked how long they had been in prison, they could be grouped according to the length of sentence, with the answer categories up to four years, over four to nine years, over nine to 18 years, and lifetime imprisonment, respectively. As participants had been incarcerated any time period from zero to their respective length of sentence, the longer the sentence, the longer the respective group’s average time elapsed since imprisonment. Obviously, this time period is confounded with the severity of the committed crime(s), as more severe or repeated crimes result in longer convictions. Thus, two opposite hypotheses can be formulated. The majority of previous research suggests that more serious crimes, i.e., longer convictions, might be associated with lower conscientiousness. On the other hand, the more time elapsed since imprisonment, the higher may be the level of conscientiousness, especially of the facets of order and self-discipline.

A MANOVA of the six facets of conscientiousness among the inmates, as predicted by length of sentence, gender, and their interaction, revealed significant gender differences (female inmates scored higher on deliberation than males), but no differences between lengths of sentences, F(18, 330) = 0.18, ns, η2 = .06. Descriptively, inmates convicted for the shorter three time periods scored similarly on order and self-discipline, whereas life-time prisoners scored lower (e.g., estimated marginal mean scores on order were 26.3, 28.8, 29.7, and 22.8, resp.). However, there were too few life-time prisoners for this difference to reach significance. A tentative conclusion from these additional analyses might be that most of the adaption to the prison environment occurs soon after imprisonment. The lower level of conscientiousness among life-time prisoners, if statistically confirmed in future studies, might be due to associations between low conscientiousness, psychopathy, and delinquency according to the literature. Alternatively, this group might not adapt to the same extent to the prison environment, possibly due to a lack of hope to be ever released.

5. General discussion

5.1. Group differences in conscientiousness, agreeableness, and openness

Previous research has demonstrated that offenders have lower scores than non-offenders on conscientiousness and agreeableness (Jones et al., 2011, Miller and Lynam, 2001). Two prospective studies have even shown that these characteristics predict future illegal activities and conflicts with the law (Mõttus et al., 2012, O'Riordan and O'Connell, 2014). Even from a theoretical point of view, the associations between psychopathy and general personality traits suggest that offenders should score lower on agreeableness and conscientiousness (see also Derefinko & Lynam, 2013). However, two European studies from Croatia and Belgium, respectively, have challenged this picture (Thiry, 2012, Trninić et al., 2008). They found that prisoners have higher levels of conscientiousness, compared to more general populations. Thiry (2012) also investigated facets of the FFM traits and revealed that prisoners scored higher on order, self-discipline, and achievement striving, with Cohen’s d ranging from 0.46 to 0.53 whereas the other three facets of conscientiousness did not differ (− 0.05 ≤ d ≤ 0.06). However, his inmate sample was tested in the context of applications for early release from prison, which may have caused attempts to dissimulate unfavorable traits, even if measures of presentation management did not indicate a biased self-presentation. Trninić et al.’s (2008) study, on the other hand, relied on young students enrolled in physical education teachers’ and sports coaches’ programs, respectively, as the control groups, and part of the prisoners’ group had not yet been convicted of the crimes for which they had been accused. Thus, these opposing findings came from only two and somewhat questionable studies.

Therefore, the present set of studies filled a need to either replicate or disconfirm the previous findings. The present studies consistently confirmed elevated levels of conscientiousness in prisoners of both genders, although they employed two different measures of personality factors that were not used in previous reports, used two different samples of prisoners, one of them containing both genders, and compared both samples to various groups of students and norm samples. Beyond confirming this general finding, this study showed that these differences were specifically due to items or facets that are relevant to the prison context. Therefore, the results were more clear-cut than Thiry’s (2012) findings, in that the prisoners’ alleged superiority on agreeableness and achievement striving was not replicated. Rather, the prison inmates scored lower on agreeableness, in accord with the majority of previous studies, and lower on openness, as in Thiry’s study. Thus, this study replicated and specified the higher levels of conscientiousness in inmates, while simultaneously suggesting that results of higher levels of agreeableness and achievement striving are unique to Thiry’s (2012) study and probably due to the specific assessment situation in his study.

So why do prisoners in these three Swedish studies, as well as their Croatian and Belgian predecessors, score higher than others on specific aspects of conscientiousness? Given that inmates and prison guards exceeded the various control groups and norm samples, respectively, just on those items and facets of conscientiousness which addressed behaviors imposed on inmates and guards by the prison environment, the most reasonable conclusion appears to be that these groups have adapted their behavior to the situational demands. The major part of this adaptation might occur soon after imprisonment.

However, this conclusion needs to be pitted against alternatives, some of which imply methodological artifacts. First, self-report biases such as social desirability might be an issue. Thiry’s (2012) inmates provided their data in the hope of early release. However, Trninić et al.’s (2008) participants were assured confidentiality, as were the participants in the present studies. Moreover, the inmates in the present studies were invited to participate by another inmate, who was serving a life sentence, which may have further reduced the tendency to give biased responses. Furthermore, findings were robust when controlling for social desirability. Addressing a second potential source of artifacts, the choice of the FFM scales is unlikely to have caused artifacts, as each study used a different, published measure: NEO-PI-R (Thiry, 2012), IPIP 100 (Trninić et al., 2008), IPIP 50 (Studies 1 & 2), and IPIP NEO (Study 3).

Third, one may question the representativeness of control groups. Some studies (Trninić et al., 2008; Studies 2, 3) compared inmates with students who may have rather low levels of conscientiousness (Rolland, Parker, & Stumpf, 1998), but other studies (Thiry, 2012, Studies 1, 3) used other, more representative comparison groups. Unfortunately, Bäckström’s (2007) normative sample consisted of a blend of students and unspecified participants surfing on the internet, which may have lowered the level of conscientiousness in this control group. Nevertheless, the level of conscientiousness may not be pathologically low in the average student. Moreover, in contrast to Rolland et al. (1998) who found a general difference between students and military recruits on each and every facet of conscientiousness, the inmates and prison guards in the present studies outperformed the students and the control groups in specific items and facets, respectively, which tap into domains specific to the prison environment.

Fourth, one might ask for matched instead of representative control groups. Inmates differed not only in their criminal past from the control groups but also in aspects such as education level. However, it has not been stated in the literature that it would be just the criminal past which makes prisoners more or less conscientious, compared to the average population. On the contrary, notions of psychopathy suggest that criminal behaviors, low education, and disadvantaged family background all evolve in the same process (Granic & Patterson, 2006). Thus, matching an inmate and control group on characteristics such as education level would result in a biased, pathological control group.

Fifth, the inmate samples are obviously not representative of the whole prison population. However, the selection of inmates of high-security prisons and thus of inmates regarded as especially dangerous should result in lower rather than higher levels of conscientiousness. Thus, the selection of the inmate samples works against the actual findings rather than explaining them.

Sixth, there might have occurred a reference-group effect (Heine et al., 2008, Heine et al., 2002) so that inmates compared themselves with other inmates, the controls with other people outside prisons, thereby obscuring a possible mean difference between groups. However, Heine et al. (2008) failed to demonstrate that a reference-group effect would eliminate cross-cultural differences in conscientiousness. Moreover, Heine et al. (2002) suggested that reference-group effects would be less an issue when rating concrete behavior descriptions, such as the conscientiousness items used in this study, and if the studied groups have contact and knowledge of each other. Even inmates in high-security prisons are not held in isolation but have contacts to relatives, etc. Furthermore, a reference-group effect could explain an absence of group differences, but not why specific facets of conscientiousness were higher in two inmate samples compared to four comparison groups, and similar to a group of prison guards who also differed systematically from control groups.

Finally, the factor structure of personality might differ in specific populations such as prisoners. If so, the validity of findings would be questionable. In Studies 1 and 2, scree plots for inmates, guards, and students favored a five-factor solution, albeit the fifth factor did explain little variance. After varimax rotation, the majority of items loaded highest on the respective factor, except for openness in the student sample. Most importantly, 8 of 10 items loaded highest on conscientiousness in the prisoners’ group, among them those items that significantly differed from students. In Study 3, a general factor of conscientiousness explained most of the variance. When estimating a six-factor oblique solution, reflecting the six facets, the factor of order, where the largest differences between inmates and non-inmates had emerged, was clearly identified in both groups, i.e., of no other facet did more items load on this factor. For inmates, 8 of 10 order items loaded highest on this factor. Thus, in general, the factor structure suggested the validity of measures, particularly of those where inmates scored higher than the comparison groups. In summary, it appears unlikely that the present findings would be due to methodological artifacts.

Another possibility would be that the prisoners already had higher levels of conscientiousness before they committed their crimes. However, even though longitudinal predictions of crime and being convicted are not strong and even spurious when intelligence is taken into account (Mõttus et al., 2012, O'Riordan and O'Connell, 2014), this would be at odds with the bulk of previous findings (e.g., Decuyper et al., 2009, Jones et al., 2011). Given the size of the present samples in relation to the small population of inmates in high-security prisons, and given their variety in terms of crimes committed, it also appears unlikely that we sampled a group of “successful psychopaths” who – provided that such a group exists at all – would combine psychopathic traits with high levels of conscientiousness. Mullins-Sweatt, Glover, Derefinko, Miller, and Widiger (2010) asked experts to name and describe such “successful psychopaths” but these were usually colleagues and, in some cases, even friends of the responding experts. Only attorneys tended to mention clients when asked for “successful psychopaths.” This, however, does not imply that attorneys would regard many of their clients as falling into this category. Also, Derefinko and Lynam (2013) summarized accounts suggesting that successful psychopaths are most likely to be found outside of prison. Thus, there is no reason to believe that the present samples, as well as their Croatian and Belgian counterparts, would be biased towards “successful psychopaths.”

The environment in a prison is very strict with respect to both regulations and norms, and private space is limited. Such an environment places demands on inmates to acquire order to avoid both formal punishment and negative acts from co-inmates. One item that distinguished the groups (Study 1) on order is: “Leave my belongings around”. It is obvious that such a behavior would not be tolerated by either the guards or other inmates with whom they have to share their highly limited space.

These results may also be the result of current cultural differences and characteristics. Most studies confirming links between psychopathy or delinquency and personality traits that included samples of criminal offenders were conducted in the USA (e.g., Clower and Bothwell, 2001, Few et al., 2015, Ross et al., 2009; see also the meta-analyses by Decuyper et al., 2009, Jones et al., 2011). In contrast, the two prospective studies predicting criminality and convictions over time were conducted in Europe, namely in Great Britain and Estonia, respectively. The British study showed only small effects of personality (O’Riordan & O’Connell, 2014), which were much smaller than the effect of male gender (which is a highly common and non-specific characteristic) and smaller than the effect of school problems. Similarly, in the Estonian study (Mõttus et al., 2012), predictions by personality traits were spurious when intelligence was taken into account. Thus, the prospective European studies showed weak conscientiousness effects to begin with. Moreover, the Belgian, Croatian, and the present Swedish studies revealed that conscientiousness is higher after imprisonment than in the general population. The results from the present study may, therefore, show a cultural difference (Hofstede & McCrae, 2004) between European countries and the USA. One possible reason might be that correctional care in Sweden has a greater focus on treatment and rehabilitation than it has in the USA, where the previous studies on FFM in inmates were conducted, thereby fostering personality development and adjustment.

To sum up this reasoning, these studies suggest that prisoners acquire relatively high levels of specific domains of conscientiousness as an adjustment to the prison environment. This conclusion is supported by adjustments of conscientiousness to demands of their living situation, even in other populations (Bleidorn, 2012). However, one question remains: do inmates internalize the demands of the situation, that is, do they undergo a personality development that also would affect their behavior after their release from prison? Or do the present findings reflect a more temporal adjustment that would be lost as soon as the situation will no longer enforce these behaviors? Longitudinal studies re-testing inmates before and after release would be needed to distinguish these two possibilities.

Personality traits, especially those covered by the FFM, have been regarded as deeply based on biological factors and highly stable over time (e.g., McCrae & Costa, 2008). If so, it would not matter at which point in time a criminal offender’s personality is tested, and the results would be highly predictive for future relapses. In fact, psychopathy estimates can be computed from measures of the FFM facets (e.g., Miller, 2012). If these estimates are used to predict delinquents’ future behavior, but actually are based on unstable trait measures, the predictions will be unsafe. If the risk of relapse is erroneously estimated to be too low, for example due to a high conscientiousness score, prisoners might be released who still put innocents in danger of victimization. On the other hand, unreliable risk assessments also conflict with the delinquents’ human right of freedom, if they are kept in prison when they could be safely released. Thus, these studies question the use of conscientiousness estimates in the risk assessment when deciding upon prisoners’ release from correctional facilities.

5.2. Unexpected findings on neuroticism and extraversion

Studies 1 and 2 revealed an average level of neuroticism among inmates. In contrast, previous research suggested elevated levels of neuroticism in people engaging in antisocial behavior or who get sanctions by the justice system (Jones et al., 2011, Miller and Lynam, 2001, Mõttus et al., 2012, O'Riordan and O'Connell, 2014), with considerable heterogeneity between studies, however (Miller & Lynam, 2001). Consistent with this, the inmates scored higher on neuroticism than the prison guards. However, the guards scored significantly lower than the students, and neither the students nor Bäckström, 2007, Bäckström, 2010 norm data did differ from the inmates. Thus, both international and Swedish findings (Dåderman, 1999, Dåderman and Edman, 2001) on slightly elevated neuroticism among inmates compared to the general population were not consistently replicated. However, Dåderman’s samples were small and contained adolescents with a diagnosis of conduct disorder (Dåderman, 1999) and male patients in forensic psychiatry (Dåderman & Edman, 2001), respectively, who obviously are not representative of the population of adult prison inmates of both genders. Rather, it is likely that persons with psychiatric diagnoses score higher than average on neuroticism. As Bäckström’s (2007) normative sample, which was used for comparisons, was a composite of students and the more general population and given that university students scored significantly higher on neuroticism than the prison guards (Study 2, see also Rolland et al., 1998), a likely explanation for this lack of difference between the inmates and the non-inmates is that both groups had elevated neuroticism. The difference between inmates and prison guards thus replicates average previous international findings.

In general, extraversion has most often been found to be unrelated to antisocial behavior (Jones et al., 2011, Miller and Lynam, 2001, Mõttus et al., 2012), with the exceptions of O’Riordan and O’Connell (2014) and Decuyper et al. (2009) who found a positive association. In the present study, we unexpectedly found that the inmates were less extraverted than the other studied populations. However, this replicates results from Trninić et al. (2008) and also findings from Iceland where the complete prison population has been investigated and scored lower on extraversion than a representative sample of young Icelanders (Gudjonsson, Sigurdsson, Bragason, Einarsson, & Valdimarsdottir, 2004). As Decuyper et al. (2009) found opposite associations at the facet level and because the theoretically expected associations between psychopathy and various facets of extraversion differ in sign (Decuyper et al., 2009, Derefinko and Lynam, 2013, Miller, 2012, Ross et al., 2009), the results may simply differ due to the various foci of the respective measures of extraversion and antisocial behaviors. As the opportunities for inmates to gather and have social contacts is restricted by definition, especially in the more secure prisons involved in the present study, low extraversion levels might also be a consequence of the prison environment.

5.3. Strengths and limitations

Two separate samples of prison inmates have been compared to two relatively representative samples and to samples of students and prison guards. Despite the differences in the comparison groups and methods of measurement, all of the studies converged on the same difference in conscientiousness between the inmates and the guards compared with students and general population. Also, the more specific analyses of the various domains of conscientiousness were consistent.

Even though the difference has been clearly established, its interpretation is not entirely free from speculation. However, it appears that prison inmates most likely adjust to their environment by elevating specific facets of conscientiousness.

It is unclear whether this apparent adjustment will be maintained when the inmates leave prison, and this is a limitation of this study. More longitudinal research is needed to follow criminals after their release. More generally, a limitation of the studies presented here is their cross-sectional design, which does not allow for testing causal relationships. To address the possible weaknesses of self-reports in a sample with a potentially high prevalence of psychopathy, it would also be valuable to conduct a replication study in which correctional officers assess the traits of inmates.

At the time of data collections (2010 − 2012), Sweden had only three prisons with the highest security level, each with a capacity of approximately 200 inmates. In other words, the potential sample sizes in the present study were small. However, this implies that the present samples represented the population of inmates of high-security prisons well.

Future studies should confirm and further explain the low level of extraversion and the average level of neuroticism found in these studies. Furthermore, this study is limited to the Five-Factor Model framework. Several studies have suggested a sixth factor, honesty/humility, that is associated with agreeableness in the Five-Factor model but adds to the explanation of psychopathic traits and delinquency, at least in normative samples (de Vries and van Gelder, 2013, Gaughan et al., 2012, Jonason and McCain, 2012). Future studies should examine whether prison populations score lower than average on honesty/humility, above and beyond low agreeableness scores.

5.4. Conclusions

This study has shown that inmates have higher mean scores than non-inmates on conscientiousness, which contrasts with previous results, but is in accord with two recent studies (Thiry, 2012, Trninić et al., 2008). This difference may be the result of personality changes or short-term adjustments in response to the demands of the prison environment. Depending on which of the two occurs, the conscientiousness factors and facets may not be valid indicators when diagnosing various personality disorders. This becomes an important issue when planning the treatment of European inmates who have been convicted for serious crimes, and when assessing their danger to the public if released.
