The relationship between everyday sadism, violent video game play, and fascination with weapons

Author links open overlay panelJeanette M.GonzalezTobiasGreitemeyer

Abstract

Previous research has shown that everyday sadism is positively related to the amount of violent video game play. The present research extends this line of work by examining whether everyday sadists are attracted to violent video games because they are fascinated with weapons. In fact, a survey (N = 613) showed that everyday sadism was significantly related to both violent video game play and the fascination with weapons. These relationships remained significant when controlling for the impact of trait aggression and the Dark Triad (i.e., narcissism, psychopathy, and Machiavellianism). Most importantly, fascination with weapons mediated the relationship between everyday sadism and amount of violent video game play. It thus appears that everyday sadists use virtual weapons during violent video game play to satisfy their fascination with weapons.

1. Introduction

Violent video game play has been shown to be related to aggression and related outcomes (for a recent meta-analysis, Greitemeyer & Mügge, 2014). Importantly, violent video game play is only one factor among many that might enhance antisocial behavior and personality (Bensley & Van Eenwyk, 2001). Yet, some video games allow to vicariously act out cruelty-related traits, which might affect the expression of personality in the long run. For example, people who enjoy inflicting pain on others are more attracted to violent video games than others (Greitemeyer, 2015, Paulhus and Dutton, 2016). These everyday sadists seem to start playing violent video games because it suits their personal desire for cruelty (Buckels, Jones, & Paulhus, 2013). Long-term gaming then further increases their sadistic tendencies (Greitemeyer & Sagioglou, 2017). Advancing previous research, we examined to what extent sensational interests account for the relationship between everyday sadism and violent video game play. Concretely, we proposed that everyday sadists would play violent video games in order to satisfy their fascination with weapons.

Sensational interests denote a curiosity for violence (e.g., weapons, crime, and military) and the macabre (e.g., conspiracy theories). Research found that sensational interests are associated with criminal offending (Charles and Egan, 2009, Egan et al., 2001). In addition, those high on the Dark Triad traits (i.e., narcissism, psychopathy, and Machiavellianism) tend to have increased levels of sensational interests (James, Kavanagh, Jonason, Chonody, & Scrutton, 2014). In non-clinical, non-forensic populations sadistic tendencies have been associated with sensational interests (Hagger-Johnson & Egan, 2010).

The goal of the current study was to examine if everyday sadists in particular are attracted to violent video games (which is a replication of previous work) and if they are more prone than others to be fascinated with weapons. Fascination with weapons should also be related to violent video game play. Finally, we tested whether fascination with weapons might account for the relationship between everyday sadism and violent video game play. It was anticipated that everyday sadists are fascinated with weapons and might therefore play violent video games. We also examined whether the relationships between everyday sadism and the amount of violent video game play and fascination with weapons, respectively, would remain significant when controlling for the impact of other facets of the dark side of human personality (i.e., the Dark Triad and trait aggression). Everyday sadism shares a common core with the Dark Triad (e.g., Međedović & Petrović, 2015), but is conceptually distinct in that everyday sadists in particular enjoy harming others (Pfattheicher and Schindler, 2015, Plouffe et al., 2017, Reidy et al., 2011). Moreover, previous work (Greitemeyer, 2015, Greitemeyer and Sagioglou, 2017) has shown that everyday sadism is a better predictor for amount of violent video game play than trait aggression or the Dark Triad. Hence, we focused on the interplay between everyday sadism, violent video game play, and fascination with weapons.

2. Methods

2.1. Participants

A link to an online questionnaire was sent to members of Austrian, German, and US-American student groups via social networks (questionnaires in German and English were provided). The questionnaire was also posted on on-line research centers (e.g., Psychological Research on the Net) and various social networking sites. There were 477 student participants. One-hundred and thirty-six community members also participated. Hence, in total 613 individuals aged 16 to 81 (M = 26.7, SD = 10.15) took part (422 female).

2.2. Materials

Calculation of a violent video game play score was adapted from Anderson and Dill (2000). Participants were asked to name their three favorite video games. They were also asked to indicate how often they play each game on a scale from 1 (rarely) to 7 (very often) and how violent each game's content was (1 = no violent content to 7 = extremely violent content). For each game, the amount of time spent playing was multiplied with the violence of its content. These individual game scores were summed to receive an overall play score. Although this is the standard approach to measuring the amount of violent video game play, we compared the participant's ratings with ratings from an objective source. We employed ratings from The Entertainment Software Rating Board that assigns ratings to video games based on their content. The ratings were from 1 = (early childhood) to 6 = (adults only, may include prolonged scenes of intense violence, graphic sexual content and/or gambling with real currency). For each of the three video game ratings, the correlation was large (all rs > 0.68, all ps < 0.001), suggesting that participant's ratings indeed provide a valid index of the game's violence level.

To assess fascination with weapons, we adapted a procedure that has been successfully employed to measure sensational interests. Whereas an initial instrument only focused on interest intensity (Egan et al., 1999), subsequent research also assessed knowledge and importance (Weiss, Egan, & Figueredo, 2004). Therefore, participants were asked to indicate their personal interest in (1 = no interest, 5 = great interest), knowledge about (1 = no knowledge, 5 = advanced knowledge), and importance of (1 = completely unimportant, 5 = completely important) each category of four superordinate categories of weapons (firearms, knives and swords, crossbows/bow and arrow, bombs and mines). They also indicated their agreement with five statements concerning feelings of excitement while handling weapons on a scale from 1 (strongly disagree) to 5 (strongly agree). A sample item is: “firing a gun is fun.” We obtained an overall score by averaging all items (α = 0.92).

Everyday sadism, the Dark Triad, and trait aggression were measured using the Comprehensive Assessment of Sadistic Tendencies (CAST, α = 0.75) by Buckels and Paulhus (2014),1 the Dirty Dozen Questionnaire (all αs > 0.74) by Jonason and Webster (2010), and the short version of the Buss-Perry Aggression Questionnaire (α = 0.85) by Bryant and Smith (2001). Sample items are: “I enjoy tormenting people” (everyday sadism), “I tend to want others to admire me” (narcissism), “I tend to be callous or insensitive” (psychopathy), “I tend to manipulate others to get my way” (Machiavellianism), and “Given enough provocation, I may hit another person” (trait aggression).

As predicted, everyday sadism was significantly related to violent video game play and fascination with weapons, respectively. As also expected, violent video game play was related to fascination with weapons. It is noteworthy that all control variables—the Dark Triad and trait aggression—were also significantly related to violent video game play and fascination with weapons, respectively.

We then examined whether everyday sadism would be related to amount of violent video game play when controlling for the impact of the Dark Triad and trait aggression. In a multiple regression, everyday sadism, narcissism, psychopathy, Machiavellianism, and trait aggression were used as predictors for amount of violent video game play. The overall regression was significant, F(5, 592) = 24.82, p < 0.001, R2 = 0.17. Most importantly, everyday sadism, β = 0.23, p < 0.001, was still significantly related to the amount of violent video game play, as were psychopathy, β = 0.16, p = 0.001, and trait aggression, β = 0.15, p = 0.001.

In a second multiple regression, fascination with weapons was regressed on the same predictors. The overall regression was significant, F(5, 600) = 31.86, p < 0.001, R2 = 0.21.2 Results showed that everyday sadism, β = 0.25, p < 0.001, was still related to fascination with weapons. Psychopathy also significantly predicted fascination with weapons, β = 0.18, p < 0.001.

Finally, we examined whether fascination with weapons would mediate the impact of everyday sadism on amount of violent video game play. In fact, a bootstrapping analysis based on 5000 bootstraps showed that the confidence interval for the indirect effect did not include 0 (95% CI = 3.18, 7.27). 4. Discussion

As in previous research (Greitemeyer, 2015, Greitemeyer and Sagioglou, 2017), everyday sadism was a strong predictor of amount of violent video game play. Importantly, this relationship held when controlling for the impact of other facets of the dark side of human personality. It thus appears that causing virtual harm while playing violent video games is particularly attractive to individuals who find pleasure in harming others (i.e., everyday sadists). Extending previous work, we showed that the fascination with weapons is closely related to both, everyday sadism and amount of violent video game play. Most importantly, fascination with weapons accounted for the impact of everyday sadism on violent video game play, suggesting that everyday sadists (in part) play violent video games to satisfy their fascination with weapons.

Please note that sensational interests denote not only a curiosity for weapons but also for macabre topics, such as the paranormal and astrology. Hence, although we believe that violent video games are particularly attractive to everyday sadists because the violent content satisfies their fascination with weapons, it may well be that other non-violent (but sensational) interests also account for the relationship between everyday sadism and violent video game play.

It is noteworthy that due to the correlational design, the exact causal relationship is unknown. For instance, it may also be that everyday sadists play violent video games and because of that they are fascinated with weapons.3 Additionally, the relationship between everyday sadism and violent video game play appears to be bidirectional. Greitemeyer and Sagioglou (2017) found that everyday sadists are attracted to violent video games, but that violent video game play also increases the player's sadistic tendencies. Future research employing a longitudinal design could be helpful in addressing the direction of the associations between everyday sadism, fascination with weapons, and amount of violent video game play.

Most previous research found violent video game play to be related to measures of aggression (e.g., Greitemeyer, 2018), a finding that was replicated in the present research. Moreover, all dimensions of the Dark Triad were positively related to amount of violent video game play, suggesting that relatively malevolent people display an increased propensity towards violent video games.

To conclude, the present research documents robust associations between everyday sadism, fascination with weapons, and amount of violent video game play. Mediation analyses suggest that everyday sadists play violent video games so that they can satisfy their fascination with weapons. Future research may address whether everyday sadists' use of virtual weapons serves as a substitute for the use of real arms.
