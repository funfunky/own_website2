A meta-analysis of the association between psychopathy and sadism in forensic samples☆

Author links open overlay panelDebraO'ConnellDavid K.Marcus
https://doi.org/10.1016/j.avb.2019.02.013Get rights and content

Highlights

•

PCL-R total scores were associated with sadism in forensic samples.

•

Both PCL-R Factor 1 and Factor 2 are associated with sadism.

•

Psychopathy and sadism are related, but distinct constructs.

•

Research using broader conceptualizations of sadism in forensic samples is needed.

Abstract

This meta-analysis examined the association between psychopathic personality traits as assessed by the Psychopathy Checklist-Revised (PCL-R; Hare, 1991), and sadism. The PCL-R yields a total score as well as two factor scores. Factor 1 encompasses the interpersonal/affective components of psychopathy such as glibness, and callousness. Factor 2 captures the antisocial behavior aspects of psychopathy including impulsivity, and poor planning abilities. The meta-analysis included 19 independent adult, male forensic samples from 16 articles that included a total of 5161 participants. The average r across all studies for PCL-R total score and sadism was 0.24 (p < .001). Both PCL-R Factor 1 (r = 0.25, p < .01) and PCL-R Factor 2 (r = 0.26, p < .01), yielded similar correlations with sadism. Sadism measurement method and type of sadism assessed did not significantly moderate the association between psychopathy and sadism. However, there was low power in the moderator analyses. These results support previous research that found both the interpersonal/affective component of psychopathy and the antisocial behavior component of psychopathy are associated with sadism.

Psychopathic personality traits include characteristics such as superficial charm, irresponsibility, fearlessness, conning behavior/manipulation, and lack of empathy (Hare, 1970). Sadism is defined as deriving enjoyment from the suffering of others (Baumeister & Campbell, 1999). Although individuals who exhibit each of these personality traits often hurt others, the reasons for doing so seem to be driven by different motivations. Psychopathic individuals will hurt others in order to obtain something they want, and will not care that they have caused harm to someone (Hare & Neumann, 2008). Sadistic individuals will hurt others simply for the pleasure they experience from doing so (Paulhus, 2014). Studies have consistently found an association between psychopathic personality traits and sadism, with offenders who are both psychopathic and sadistic being more violent and likely to recidivate (Meloy, 2000; Porter, Woodworth, Earle, Drugge, & Boer, 2003) compared to individuals who are either psychopathic or sadistic. In fact, a diagnosis of psychopathy and/or sadism is often used to justify longer sentences or deny parole (e.g., Levenson, 2004; Lloyd, Clark, & Forth, 2010). Still, the precise nature of how psychopathy and sadism are related remains ambiguous, both in terms of the magnitude of the association and in terms of what components explain this shared variance.

Within forensic settings, the association between psychopathy and sadism has only been studied in male samples, and researchers have primarily utilized the Psychopathy Checklist-Revised (PCL-R; Hare, 1991) to measure psychopathy. The PCL-R yields a total score as well as two correlated factors (Hare, 1991). Factor 1 encompasses the interpersonal/affective components of psychopathy such as glibness, shallow affect, and callousness. Factor 2 captures the antisocial behavior aspects of psychopathy including impulsivity, parasitic lifestyle, and poor planning abilities. Measurement method for sadism in the literature has varied, and includes both self-report as well as clinician-ratings of behavior, personality traits, or a combination of both. Previous literature reviews on psychopathy and sadism have focused on forensic subsamples such as homicide offenders (e.g., James & Proulx, 2014; Kirsch & Becker, 2007). To date, no meta-analyses have been conducted to examine the association between psychopathy and sadism among offenders. By synthesizing the available findings across different types of forensic samples (e.g., homicide offenders, sex offenders, substance abuse offenders) via meta-analysis, it will be possible to quantify the strength of the overall association between psychopathy and sadism.

The current meta-analysis examined the overall association between psychopathy and sadism as well as the associations between the interpersonal/affective component of psychopathy and sadism and the antisocial behavior component of psychopathy and sadism. Only studies utilizing the PCL-R were included in the meta-analysis. An additional advantage of limiting the meta-analysis to PCL-R studies is that, unlike some other measures of psychopathy, none of the items on the PCL-R address sadistic pleasure at another's pain.1

1. Psychopathy and sadism

Sadism as a construct has changed over time and debate continues as to whether sadism is best conceptualized as a personality dysfunction, a type of antisocial behavior, or a combination of both (e.g., Krueger, 2010). Additionally, there has been debate over whether sexual sadism is a separate construct from other forms of sadism (e.g., sadism as a personality disorder, and sadism as a personality trait; Kirsch & Becker, 2007; Krueger, 2010). Because of continued debate over the definition of sadism, a review of the association between psychopathy and sadism will be organized by type of sadism assessed and include a brief overview of each type of sadism.

1.1. Psychopathy and sexual sadism

The severity of sexually sadistic behavior can vary across a spectrum of behavior from adults engaged in consensual acts of sadism and masochism to torture and homicide of non-consenting victims. Empirical research on the association between psychopathy and sexual sadism has exclusively examined male forensic samples, particularly sex offenders. Across studies, PCL-R total scores are consistently and positively correlated with sexual sadism (Firestone, Bradford, Greenberg, Larose, & Curry, 1998; Langevin, 2003; Porter et al., 2003). However, the associations between sexual sadism and the two factors of the PCL-R have been inconsistent. Porter et al. (2003) found an association between the interpersonal/affective components of psychopathy (PCL-R, F1) and sexual sadism, but not the antisocial behavior components of psychopathy (PCL-R, F2) and sexual sadism. Similarly, homicidal offenders had higher F1 scores and diagnoses of sexual sadism compared to non-homicidal offenders (Firestone et al., 1998). In contrast, sexually sadistic sex offenders had significantly higher F2 scores compared to other sex offenders, but sadistic sex offenders did not differ from other sex offenders on F1 (Barbaree, Seto, Serin, Amos, & Preston, 1994).

Mokros, Osterheider, Hucker, and Nitschke (2011) examined the association between psychopathy and sexual sadism using the hospital records of 100 sex offenders, using the four facet scoring model of the PCL-R. The four-facet model of the PCL-R separates F1 into Interpersonal and Affective facets, and F2 into Lifestyle, and Antisocial facets. The Interpersonal Facet is characterized by such traits as manipulation, and pathological lying, and the Affective Facet captures such items as lack of remorse, callousness, and lack of empathy. The Lifestyle Facet encompasses impulsivity, and irresponsibility, whereas the Antisocial Facet primarily refers to criminal activity during childhood and adulthood. Mokros et al. (2011) found that the PCL-R Affective and Antisocial Facets were both correlated with sexual sadism, whereas the PCL-R Interpersonal and Lifestyle Facets were not. These findings suggest that some traits from both PCL-R Factor 1 and Factor 2 are associated with sadism.

1.2. Psychopathy and sadistic personality

Sadistic Personality Disorder (SPD) first appeared in the appendix of the DSM-III-R (American Psychiatric Association, 1987) as a personality disorder to be considered for future editions of the DSM. To meet diagnostic criteria for SPD, an individual must have engaged in “a pervasive pattern of cruel, demeaning, and aggressive behavior, beginning by early adulthood” (American Psychiatric Association, 1987, p. 371) for the purpose of amusement or obtaining pleasure from the suffering of others. Ultimately, SPD was not included in later editions of the DSM, but these DSM criteria have been used in some studies that have examined the association between psychopathy and sadism (e.g., Delescluse & Pham, 2005).

Holt, Meloy, and Strack (1999) examined the association between psychopathy and sadism in a sample of 41 offenders classified as either violent, or sexually violent. Psychopathy was measured using the two-factor model of the PCL-R. Sadism was measured with the sadism scale of the Millon Clinical Multiaxial Inventory-II (MCMI-II; Millon, 1987), the sadism items from Personality Disorder Examination (PDE; Loranger, 1988), and the diagnostic criteria for sexual sadism from the DSM-IV (American Psychiatric Association, 2000) Holt et al. (1999) found significant, positive correlations between the PCL-R total score, F1, and F2 with both the MCMI-II sadism scale and the PDE. The correlations ranged from r = 0.48–0.64. Additionally, individuals classified as “psychopaths,” based on the PCL-R recommended score of 30 or higher, scored significantly higher on the MCMI-II sadism scale and the sadism items on the PDE compared to non-psychopathic offenders. Of note, the psychopathic sexually violent offenders did not differ significantly from the psychopathic violent offenders in their MCMI-II scores or their PDE scores. There were no significant correlations between DSM-IV sexual sadism criteria and psychopathy, although the researchers noted that one limitation was the small number of participants meeting diagnostic criteria for DSM-IV sexual sadism in their sample (n = 3).

In summary, a majority of the research on the association between psychopathy and sadism in forensic settings has assessed sexual sadism. Occasionally, sadism has been more broadly defined as a general enjoyment of hurting others. Although there is clear evidence that there is an association between PCL-R total scores and sadism (e.g., Firestone et al., 1998; Holt et al., 1999; Langevin, 2003; Porter et al., 2003), the findings have been less consistent regarding how each PCL-R factor is related to sadism.

2. The present study

The present meta-analysis assessed the association between psychopathic personality traits as measured by the Psychopathy Checklist-Revised (PCL-R; Hare, 1991) and all types of sadism (i.e., sexual sadism, personality models of sadism, and mixed violent/sexual sadism) in forensic samples. The meta-analysis was limited to studies utilizing the PCL-R because the literature search yielded only one study that did not use the PCL-R in a forensic sample.

Consistent with previous research on psychopathy and sadism (e.g.,Firestone et al., 1998; Holt et al., 1999; Langevin, 2003; Mokros et al., 2011), it was hypothesized that PCL-R total scores, Factor 1 and Factor 2 will all positively correlate with measures of sadism. Because of shared method variance, we also expected that the association between psychopathy and sadism would be stronger when sadism was measured by clinician-ratings compared to self-report. Additionally, we examined whether type of sadism (i.e., sexual or other) moderated the association between psychopathy and sadism.

3. Method

3.1. Identification of studies

The first author conducted a search of PsycINFO using the term “psychopathy” and variants (i.e., “psychopath,” and “psychopathic”) combined with the term “sadism” and variants (i.e., “sadist,” and “sadistic”). These search terms initially yielded 694 results, and filters were applied to only include empirical, peer-reviewed articles which narrowed the results to 92. Additional searches were conducted in PubMed and ISI Web of Science. After cross-referencing the PubMed search results with the articles returned from the PsycInfo search, PubMed yielded five additional articles (k = 97). All search results from ISI Web of Science were accounted for in either the PsycINFO or PubMed search. To be included in the meta-analysis, studies had to (a) provide codable statistics in the form of a correlation coefficient or the necessary information to calculate one; (b) measure psychopathy using the PCL-R (Hare, 1991); (c) include a measure of sadism or a description of diagnostic criteria (e.g., DSM-III sadistic personality disorder diagnosis); and (d) have researchers who have direct contact with subjects or access to criminal or psychological records.2

Sixteen of the 97 articles met the inclusion criteria and provided data for 19 independent samples. The 76 excluded references were excluded because they (a) did not include details about how psychopathy and sadism were measured (13); (b) were commentaries, theory papers, or review articles which did not report new data (44); (c) measured Antisocial Personality Disorder, but not psychopathy (6); (d) had a sample overlapping with another study (k = 3); (d) used a psychopathy measure other than the PCL-R (5); or (e) did not provide usable data to calculate a correlation coefficient (10). See Fig. 1 for a flowchart of study selection.

In cases where usable data were not provided, but all other inclusion criteria were met, the author listed to receive correspondence was contacted for further information. Out of 12 authors emailed to request data, two authors provided the location of usable data that had been overlooked during coding, one author provided a reference to a recently published article, four reported that they no longer had the data files, and there was no response from the remaining six. The reference sections of the included articles were then used to locate additional references to potentially include in the meta-analysis, which did not yield any additional usable additional samples/studies. Thus, the meta-analysis included 19 independent samples from 16 studies that included 5161 participants. All studies included in the meta-analysis are listed in Table 1.

3.2. Procedure

The studies were coded for region, sample size, percentage Caucasian, IQ, age group, type of sadism (sexual, mixed sexual/violent, and mixed sexual/personality), sadism measure used, and sadism measure method (clinician rating, self-report). All included studies used exclusively male samples. When reported, separate effect sizes were calculated for the association between PCL-R F1 and sadism, and PCL-R F2 and sadism. Any articles that did not report Pearson's product-moment correlation coefficients, but provided the requisite data to calculate an effect size were converted using an online effect-size calculator (Wilson, 2001). To correct for bias, Pearson's rs were then converted with Fisher's Zr transformation (Lipsey & Wilson, 2001, p.107), mean effect sizes were calculated, and then the Z-values were converted back to r for reporting. The effect sizes were weighted by sample size, and the inverse variance weight used was the sample size minus three (Lipsey & Wilson, 2001, p. 64).

When multiple measures of sadism were included in a study, the effect sizes from each measure were averaged. However, if a study used both a dichotomous (e.g., DSM-III Sadistic Personality Disorder diagnosis) and a continuous measure (e.g., Sadistic Attitudes and Behavior Scale) of sadism, the effect size from the continuous measure was included in the meta-analysis to maximize variance and power.

All analyses were conducted using a maximum-likelihood random effects model with Lipsey and Wilson's (2001) statistical program for SPSS. A Q test of homogeneity was used to determine whether there was significant heterogeneity among the effect sizes. I2 was calculated to determine the percentage of variance attributable to heterogeneity across studies (Higgins, Thompson, Deeks, & Altman, 2003). I2 values range from 0 to 100. Values of 25%, 50%, and 75% have been proposed as anchor points for low, moderate, and high I2 values (Higgins et al., 2003). The regression test for funnel plot asymmetry (Egger, Smith, Schneider, & Minder, 1997), the trim and fill procedure (Duval & Tweedie, 2000), and the leave-one-out analysis were conducted using the metafor package in R (Viechtbauer, 2010) to examine possible publication bias or outlier effects. The meta-analytic analogue to the analysis of variance (ANOVA) was used to test for the categorical moderators (Hedges, 1982; Lipsey & Wilson, 2001).

4. Results

4.1. Primary analyses

The mean effect size for PCL-R total score and sadism was r = 0.24, which was statistically significant (k = 19; 95% CI = 0.16–0.34; Z = 5.34, p < .001). There was considerable heterogeneity among the studies Q (18) = 109.40, p < .001, I2 = 84.5, indicating that these studies do not all derive from the same population. The funnel plot indicated that most of the effect sizes fell within the expected distribution (Fig. 2), and the regression test for funnel plot asymmetry using the standard error of the observed values as a predictor variable was not significant (z = 1.70, p = .08). Additionally, the trim and fill procedure did not impute any additional studies to the funnel plot. The leave-one-out procedure indicated that no single study was skewing the mean effect size, with rs ranging from 0.22 to 0.27, and all p values < .001.

Eight studies reported the associations between the PCL-R factor scores and sadism. The mean effect size for the association between PCL-R F1 and sadism was r = 0.25, which was statistically significant (95% CI = 0.09–0.44; Z = 3.01, p < .01). There was moderate heterogeneity among the studies Q(7) = 15.44, p = .03, I2 = 54.7, indicating that these studies do not all derive from the same population. The funnel plot generated from the data indicated that all but one study fell within the expected distribution (Fig. 3). The regression test for funnel plot asymmetry with standard error selected as the predictor was not significant (z = 0.32, p = .74). The trim and fill procedure did not suggest imputing any additional studies. The leave-one-out analysis indicated even when the study with the largest effect size was eliminated, the average effect remained significant at p < .01, with rs ranging from 0.16 to 0.29, and all p values ≤ .01.

The mean effect size for the association between PCL-R Factor 2 and sadism was r = 0.26, which was statistically significant (95% CI = 0.08–0.46; Z = 2.82, p < .01). There was a moderate amount of heterogeneity among the studies Q(7) = 18.52, p < .01, I2 = 62.2, indicating that these studies do not all derive from the same population. Examination of the funnel plot indicated that all but two studies fell within the expected distribution (Fig. 4). The regression test for funnel plot asymmetry was not significant (z = 0.65, p = .51). Additionally, the trim and fill analysis indicated there were no missing studies in the funnel plot. Finally, re-running the analyses leaving out one study at a time indicated that even when the study with the largest effect size was eliminated, the average effect remained significant at p < .05 with rs ranging from 0.20 to 0.31, and all p values < .05.

4.2. Moderation analyses

Measurement method used to assess sadism (i.e., self-report vs. clinician-rating), and type of sadism were tested as potential moderators of the relationship between PCL-R total scores and sadism. The minimum number of studies in each category for a variable to be included as a moderator was k = 3, which precluded moderator analyses for PCL-R factor scores (i.e., of the studies that reported associations with PCL-R factor scores, only two used self-report measures of sadism, and only one study examined a form of sadism other than sexual sadism).

A random effects model analogous to the ANOVA (Hedges, 1982) was conducted to test whether sadism measurement method moderated the association between psychopathy and sadism. More specifically, we investigated whether the variation among the effect sizes for psychopathy total score and sadism was attributed to differences in how sadism was measured (e.g., self-report vs. clinician-rating). The association between PCL-R scores and self-reported sadism did not differ significantly from the association between PCL-R scores and clinician-ratings of sadism, QB(1) = 2.54, p = .11; QW(17) = 19.54, p = .29. The average effect size for the three studies of PCL-R total scores and self-report measures of sadism was 0.38 (95% CI = 0.19–0.62; Z = 3.71, p < .001), and the average effect size for the 16 studies of PCL-R total score and clinician-ratings of sadism was 0.21 (95% CI = 0.12–0.31; Z = 4.47, p < .001).

Fifteen of the 19 samples assessing the association between PCL-R total scores and sadism measured sexual sadism (k = 15). The other four samples assessed mixed violent and sexual sadism (k = 2), and personality/character sadism (k = 2). There were no significant differences between the studies that specifically assessed sexual sadism and the other four studies, QB(1) = 1.79, p = .18; QW(17) = 18.45, p = .36. The average effect size for the 15 samples that assessed the association between psychopathy total scores and sexual sadism was 0.22 (95% CI = 0.12–0.32; Z = 4.24, p < .001). The average effect size for the four studies that assessed the association between psychopathy total scores and other types of sadism was 0.36 (95% CI = 0.17–0.58; Z = 3.57, p = .001).

5. Discussion

Although several studies have indicated that psychopathy and sadism are separate, but related constructs (e.g., James & Proulx, 2014; Kirsch & Becker, 2007; Porter, Campbell, Woodworth, & Birt, 2001) no prior meta-analyses have examined the strength of this association. The primary aims of the meta-analysis were to (a) assess the association in forensic samples between psychopathy as measured by the PCL-R and various forms of sadism, and (b) determine how each factor of the PCL-R is associated with sadism.

The hypothesis that PCL-R total score, as well as Factor 1 and Factor 2 would be associated with sadism was supported. These findings are consistent with previous research on the association between psychopathy and sadism. Additionally, the finding that both PCL-R Factor 1 and Factor 2 are associated with sadism supports the model proposed by Porter et al. (2001) that both interpersonal/affective components of psychopathy and the antisocial behavior component contribute to the association between psychopathy and sadism. The medium effect sizes found for the association between PCL-R total scores and sadism as well as the PCL-R factor scores and sadism indicate that psychopathy and sadism are related, but distinct constructs. Consequently, it may be fruitful in future research to examine whether psychopathy and sadism each make independent contributions to the prediction of antisocial or aggressive behavior, or to the prediction of criminal recidivism. Researchers should also examine potential interactions between sadism and psychopathy (e.g., is sadism especially predictive of antisocial behavior at high levels of psychopathy?).

Although the method of measuring sadism did not moderate the association between psychopathy and sadism, only three studies used self-report measures, which limited the power of this analysis. Although not significantly different, the associations between the PCL-R and self-reported sadism (0.38) were nearly twice as large as the association between the PCL-R and clinician-rated sadism, which was contrary to our hypothesis. Most of the clinician-rated studies diagnosed sadism based on coding case files for characteristics of criminal acts to indicate sadistic behavior (e.g., evidence of torture, recording crimes, indicators of planning for the crime, etc.), or had been classified by previous clinicians as meeting diagnostic criteria for DSM Sexual Sadism based on offense characteristics (e.g., Briken et al., 2006; Eher et al., 2015; Mokros et al., 2011; Nitschke et al., 2012). When sadism was assessed via a clinical interview (e.g., Richards & Jackson, 2011), offenders were only asked about behaviors that were later classified by the researchers as sadistic. In contrast, self-report measures of sadism may also assess sadistic thoughts or fantasies in addition to behaviors. For example, Robertson and Knight (2014) used the self-report Multidimensional Inventory of Development, Sex, and Aggression (MIDSA; Knight & Cerce, 1999) in one of the samples in their study. The MIDSA is a computerized inventory that assesses for both sexually sadistic behaviors and fantasies. Interestingly, Robertson and Knight (2014) found that the association between PCL-R total scores and MIDSA scores in the first sample was roughly twice as large as the association between PCL-R total scores and clinician-ratings of sexually sadistic behaviors in their second sample. It is possible that offenders feel more comfortable disclosing sadistic thoughts, fantasies, and behaviors in a self-report format, particularly if the participants are told that their answers will be confidential or anonymous. Still, because this moderator analysis was not significant, these speculations should be treated tentatively until there are additional studies that assess sadism using self-report measures in forensic samples.

The moderator analysis for type of sadism assessed was not significant; however, a large majority of the samples (15 out of 18) only assessed sexual sadism, limiting the power of this analysis. Of the four studies that did not exclusively assess sexual sadism, the effect sizes ranged from almost no association (Delescluse & Pham, 2005) to a medium effect size (Hill et al., 2007; Stupperich & Strack, 2016) to a large effect size (Holt et al., 1999). However, the Delescluse and Pham (2005) study, which yielded the smallest effect, also required that in order for participants to be classified as sadistic, they had to meet all eight DSM-II-R criteria for Sadistic Personality Disorder, which is an unrealistically stringent standard that may have attenuated a possible association between psychopathy and sadism. Additionally, both studies that assessed multiple forms of sadism included sexual sadism (Delescluse & Pham, 2005; Holt et al., 1999). Therefore, it remains unclear whether different types of sadism vary in how they are related to psychopathy.

The meta-analysis had several strengths. It was the first meta-analysis to examine the association between psychopathy and sadism, as well as the association between subcomponents of psychopathy and sadism. The present study was, however, limited in several ways. First, the small amount of empirical research available on PCL-R psychopathy and sadism in forensic populations limited the meta-analysis to 19 samples. Additionally, the association between PCL-R psychopathy and sadism has exclusively been studied in male, forensic samples, and most studies have limited their definitions of sadism to clinician-rated sexually sadistic behaviors. Further complicating the study of the association between components of psychopathy and sadism has been the low reporting rate of factor scores for the PCL-R. These factors resulted in the moderator analyses being underpowered, and precluded conducting moderator analyses with PCL-R factor scores and sadism. Future research on the association between psychopathy and sadism in offender samples should be conducted with women, and include broader conceptualizations of sadism and as well as different models of psychopathy (e.g., the triarchic model; Patrick, Fowles, & Krueger, 2009) to further elucidate which components of psychopathy are associated with sadism.

In summary, both the interpersonal/affective component of psychopathy (PCL-R Factor 1) and the antisocial behavior component of psychopathy (PCL-R Factor 2) are similarly associated with sadism in forensic samples. Thus, sadism among forensic populations may involve both a lack of empathy for others and willingness to exploit others for one's pleasure or gain (Factor 1), as well as a pattern of impulsive rule-breaking behaviors (Factor 2). Although the moderator analyses were null, they were likely underpowered due to the lack of published studies measuring non-sexual forms of sadism or self-reported sadism. The results of the present study hint that the overall association between psychopathy and sadism may be stronger when sadism is measured via self-report, or when sadism is assessed more broadly than a specific focus on sexual sadism (i.e., when a broad spectrum of sadistic thoughts, fantasies, and behaviors are assessed).

1

The original criteria for the meta-analysis included any measure of psychopathy; however, all of the research conducted with non-forensic samples (mostly college students) utilized self-report measures of psychopathy and sadism. Therefore, including the non-forensic samples would have confounded psychopathy measure, sample type, and method variance. Additionally, the studies with non-forensic samples utilized the “dark triad,” and “dark tetrad” models of psychopathy and everyday sadism that are conceptually different from PCL-R defined psychopathy, and sadism measured in the forensic samples. Finally, by limiting the meta-analysis to offender/forensic samples, we ensured that the data were collected from individuals who had seriously harmed others. Following a preliminary review of relevant studies, we decided to limit the inclusion criteria to PCL-R studies of forensic samples.
